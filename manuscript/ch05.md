## Chapter 5 — Under the Line

The panel came off the wall and behind it was a ladder, and the ladder went down into water, and the water was black.

Not dark. Black. There's a difference. Dark is a room with the lights off. Black is a thing that's been waiting.

I'm the guy who reads the warning label twice and puts the bottle back on the shelf. There was no label here. Just a hole, and cold air coming up out of it that smelled like the inside of the earth, and Sol already swinging her legs over the edge like she did this on Tuesdays.

"It's three minutes of swimming," she said. "Four if you panic, and you'll panic, everyone does. There's air on the other side. Don't grab me — people who grab drown the person they grab and then drown anyway. Follow the rope." She put my hand on it. A guide-line bolted into the dark, slick and old. "Lim goes first. He doesn't remember being afraid of water, which makes him very good at this."

Lim went into the black without a sound, the way you'd lower a hand into a pocket. Then Sol. Then there was just me and the hole and my own excellent advice, telling me to climb back upstairs and take whatever sentence was waiting, because at least up there the dying came with a number and a little dignity.

I went in.

The cold doesn't hit you all at once. It signs you up in installments. Feet first. Then the gut-punch when it reaches your chest. Then your skull, when you put your face under and the water closes the door behind you.

And then — I hate admitting this, because I've spent my whole life being good at sitting still — my body knew what to do.

I'm not a brave swimmer. I'm a calm one. There's a difference. Brave is for people who think they might win. Calm is just knowing that thrashing spends air you don't have, so you don't thrash. You pull. Hand over hand, slow, and you let the part of you that learned this a long time ago drive, while the scared part sits in the back and keeps its mouth shut. Pull. Pull. Don't think about whether the rope ends somewhere good. Pull.

There was a moment in the middle. There's always a moment in the middle, where the air in you goes thin and the black gets blacker and a very reasonable voice points out that this is a stupid way to die and also the only way left. I told the voice I'd get back to it. Pull. Pull.

My hand broke the surface before the rest of me understood there was a surface to break.

I came up into a pocket of old air in what used to be a stairwell, and I made a sound I'm not proud of — somewhere between a baby and a busted pump. Sol was already sitting on steps that climbed up out of the water and stopped flush against a ceiling, going nowhere, the way everything down here went nowhere. She didn't laugh at the sound I made. I gave her credit for that.

There was a little light. One of their lamps, cold and green, and by it I could see where we were. Where we were used to be a mall.

You could still read it. That's the part that got me. A hundred years underwater, the histories say, and there was still a sign bolted to the tile above the waterline, half its letters drowned, and the half that was left said *—VEL 3 PARKING*, with a little arrow pointing at a ramp that now ran straight down into more water. Somebody parked there once. Somebody complained it was full. Somebody waited by that sign for somebody who was late, and it was a whole thing, a small bad evening in a life, and now it was down here in the green dark with me, dripping.

"First time under?" Sol asked. Not unkind.

"First time anywhere," I said. My teeth were doing a drum solo. "I file tickets for a living."

"You swim like you've done it before."

"I have. In a pool. Ninety-second tier. Clean water, they bill you by the minute." I wrung out the hem of my shirt, which is a pointless thing to do while sitting in a flooded stairwell, but you do the small normal thing because the big abnormal thing is too much to look at straight. "Nobody mentioned the skill transferred to drowning."

She almost smiled. Lim sat a little apart, perfectly content, humming something with no tune to it — a man at peace with a world he'd been trimmed out of.

And then I noticed my wrist.

It was dark.

Not low. Not falling. *Dark.* No number. Just skin, pale and going pruney in the green light, the way it must have looked before they ever gave me one — except I don't remember that. Nobody remembers that. You get your number before you get your name.

Down here, under the line, past where the building bothers to look, BANTAY couldn't see me. For the first time in my life there was nothing on my wrist telling me what I was worth this morning. I'd spent twenty-nine years checking that number before I checked anything else, and now it was just gone, and I sat there in the cold with my drowned-mall ceiling and my humming trimmed friend and a woman named after the sun, and I waited to feel free.

I didn't. Not yet. Mostly I felt *unweighed.* Like something in a shop with the price torn off, that nobody's decided about.

I held my wrist a while and looked at all that nothing.
