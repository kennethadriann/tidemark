## Chapter 30 — Off My Back

The top stop didn't announce itself, which by then I should have expected. Nothing up there ever did. I kept waiting for a line drawn across the way, a door, a man to say you're here — and what I got instead was the climb going easy on me by a little and then a little more, in steps too small to catch happening. The stones on the good side crept closer together and better tended. The floor left off fighting me. Then the dark ahead had a thing in it that wasn't the wet stone I'd been breathing two days straight — smoke, and something frying in old fat, and under it the plain sour smell of a lot of people living close in one place with nowhere for it to go. I came round a shoulder of rock and the way opened up into light and noise and I was there before I knew I was anywhere.

It was bigger than the room down at the Sling and it was busier and it sat under a real roof of some kind, high up and lost in the dark, so the whole place felt like a cave that had decided to be a town. Ways came into it from every side, the marked ways, all of them topping out here the way streams come off a hill into the one low field. Loads pooled and moved. Men set them down and men picked them up and other men stood over slates doing the sums that decide which is which. There were stalls along one wall with actual goods on them and a pot going and a woman calling a price at nobody in particular, and the sound of it all came at me at once after two days of hearing mostly my own breath and my own feet, and I stood in the mouth of my way for a second just taking the size of it.

I didn't get to stand long. A man with an empty back came down off the high side and near walked through me, and said not unkindly to shift myself, this was the coming-in and not the place to plant a post. So I shifted. I did the thing I'd got good at, which was to watch what the men ahead of me did and do that, a beat behind. The loaded backs were making for one corner of the floor where the freight was going down in heaps, and a heavy squared-off man stood there with a slate of his own, taking each load in with his eyes and a word and marking it, and I got in the tail of that line with my light one riding me and waited my turn to be somebody's mark.

When it came he looked at the bundle before he looked at me, same as the parcel-man had, these men all read the load first and the man second like the load's the honest one of the pair. "Down there," he said, and tipped his head at the mean end of the heap, the small sorry stuff, and I knew my freight's kind by now well enough not to take it personal. I backed up to the pile the way I'd been backing up to ledges all day, and I got the ropes off my shoulders, and the light one went down onto the heap and stayed down.

I won't pretend it was nothing. My back came up off that weight and kept coming, floating, like it had forgot which way was heavy, and for a breath I felt I might tip over backward from the sheer lack of the thing. Two days I'd been shaped around a load. Standing up straight and empty felt about as strange as the load had felt to start with. I put a hand on the wall and let my spine work out on its own that the fight was off, slow, joint by joint, and I stood there being a man with nothing on him and found it took some getting used to, which is a sentence I'd have laughed at a week before.

The tally-man had already turned my bundle over, given the wrap a look, marked his slate. He reached into a box by his foot and counted a small mean number of little metal bits into my palm without much ceremony — what a light one pays, which the parcel-man had told me plain was little, and he hadn't lied. It sat in my hand and it wasn't much of anything. You couldn't have called it a wage. It was more like a receipt that said the thing had happened, that a load had gone up on a back and come off it at the top and here was the little the world thought that was worth.

"You dropped nothing," he said, not as praise, just closing the account. He was already looking past me at the next back. But he said it, and it was in his slate now, and I understood standing there that this was the whole of what I'd come up for and hadn't known to want. Not the metal. The line in the slate. A first back that made it up and set the thing down where it was meant to go instead of somewhere in the dark going to nobody. The parcel-man had bet I'd cost him a load and I hadn't. Down at the Sling my face was still the one thing he'd said he'd keep, and now the keeping of it had turned the other way — not a man to watch for, a man who'd carried one and come out the top with the freight whole.

Nobody made anything of it. That was the size of it and the truth of it both. A man came off two days of the hardest work his soft body had ever done, delivered, got paid a pittance, and the town didn't so much as look up. I found a place along the resting wall where the empty backs sat waiting on whatever they were waiting on, and I got down among them, and this time when I got down among strangers I wasn't a stranger in the way I'd been at every lamp coming up. I was a back off the low ways with a delivery behind me and a few bits in my pocket, which is a thing so ordinary up there that being it made me invisible in the good way at last. The men to either side of me didn't know me and had no cause to. One of them was chewing something and offered me none of it and I didn't hold it against him.

Walkers came and went across the floor the whole while, loads down and loads up, the marked ways breathing them through — men bound higher than I'd been, men off deeper runs than mine, all of them topping out and passing through this one lit field on their way to wherever they were owed. Somewhere up past here the ways went on. I sat with my back to the cold stone and my empty hands in my lap and watched the freight move, and I let my legs be legs again, and I stayed where I was.

*End of Chapter 30.*
