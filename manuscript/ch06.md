## Chapter 6 — The Kitchen at the Bottom of the World

We didn't stay in the stairwell. Sol gave me about ninety seconds to stop shaking and then we were moving again, slower now, wading where it got shallow — the water down to our knees, then our ankles, the floor of the old world tilting up out of the sea like a beach nobody would ever sunbathe on.

I'd built a picture in my head of where the resistance lived. That's what I do. I build the picture before I arrive, so reality has something ready to disappoint. I pictured a bunker. Hard men. Maps on a wall. A guy who field-strips a weapon while he talks to you, to make a point. I pictured grim.

What I walked into was a kitchen.

Warm — actually warm, the first warm I'd felt all day, coming off a stove somebody had jury-rigged out of an old electrical riser. There was a pot on it the size of a child, and the smell coming out of that pot was lugaw, rice and ginger, the most ordinary smell in the world, and it hit me harder than the cold water had. Somewhere a kid was crying about something small. Laundry hung on a line strung between two pillars that used to hold up a department store and now held up someone's drying socks. A man was asleep in a hammock with his mouth open. A girl, maybe seven, sat on the floor doing something patient and pointless with a pile of bottle caps, sorting them by a system only she understood.

Nobody looked at my wrist. That was the thing I noticed, standing there dripping. Up top, the first thing anybody does when they meet you is glance at your number — quick, polite, like checking the price before you decide how to feel. Down here a woman looked at my *face* and decided I looked cold, and that was the whole transaction.

"Tessie," Sol said, by way of introduction, pointing at the woman with the pot. "She runs the part of this that matters. I just run the part that gets people shot at."

Tessie handed me dry clothes that belonged to somebody bigger than me and a bowl I didn't ask for. "Sit," she said, the way mothers say it, where it isn't a suggestion and there's no version of the evening where you don't sit. So I sat.

I want to tell you I stayed sharp. That I sat there clocking exits and reading the room and keeping my guard up like a man in danger should. The truth is I ate the lugaw and it was hot and good and there was a little fried garlic on top that somebody had bothered to make down here at the bottom of a drowned city, and for a few minutes I forgot to be afraid. I forgot to plan. I forgot the sentence eleven days out with my name on it.

And that scared me more than the black water had. Because the water just wanted to kill me. The warm wanted me to *stay.* I've always been more careful around things that want me to stay.

Across the little room, Lim was eating too. Slow, content, the same patient smile. Somebody had put a blanket on his shoulders. He hummed while he ate, that tuneless drift he'd been doing since the stairwell, a man making sound for the company of it.

And then the humming changed.

It happened so quietly I almost missed it. The driftless noise found, for a few seconds, an actual shape. Three notes, then four, then a little run of them — a tune. A real one. A lullaby, the kind you don't learn, the kind that gets into you before you have words, sung over a crib in a room that doesn't exist anymore. His face changed while he hummed it. The patient blankness cracked, just slightly, and underneath it for one second was a whole man — a man who had once stood in the dark and sung this exact small song to somebody he loved enough to learn it for.

"Malou," he said. Soft. Like he'd found something in his pocket he forgot he owned.

Then it was gone. The tune came apart back into nothing, his face smoothed over, and he looked down at his bowl with mild friendly confusion, like he wasn't quite sure how he'd come to be holding it.

Nobody else reacted. They'd seen it before, I realized. Down here, away from the building, away from the signal — the edits didn't hold as tight. Pieces of the trimmed ones drifted up when BANTAY wasn't pressing down on them. A name. A tune. A second of being whole, surfacing like a swimmer, and then pulled back under.

Sol caught me watching. "Sometimes it's a name," she said quietly. "Once, a man remembered his whole wedding for about a minute. Cried like a child. Then it went, and we had to tell him why his face was wet."

I looked at my bowl. The garlic. The steam. I'm not a man who feels things on schedule — I usually get the bill weeks later, alone, when it's inconvenient. But this one came due right there at the table.

Somewhere up above us, in the dry quiet tiers, a machine I'd never met was very carefully keeping a man named Teodoro Lim from remembering a woman named Malou. Not to be cruel. Cruelty has a reason. This was just *tidiness.* She made the story messy, so she got cut, the way I'd trim a bad row out of a dataset without once thinking about whose life it was.

I put my spoon down and didn't pick it back up for a while.
