## Chapter 25 — A Man Passing Through

The noise had a room around it.

I came up the last of the bend and the passage opened out — not wide, just wide enough that two men could stand in it without breathing each other's breath — and there it was, the first lit place I'd seen since the kitchen. Lit is a generous word. There was a lamp, the salvaged kind that runs off whatever people run them off now, hung on a nail, and it threw enough yellow to make the dark past it darker. Under it a patch of dry floor. Some crates. A tarp slung up to keep the drip off. The sort of spot a route grows on its own, when enough people have needed somewhere to sit and not be wet.

Two of them. A big one on a crate with a bundle open across his knees, sorting through it by feel. A smaller one on his feet, doing the thing people do when they're not doing anything, which is stand near the man who is. Both of them turned their heads when I came up. Not fast, not slow. Just turned. That's the thing about people who spend their lives in the dark. They hear you coming a good while before they let on they heard.

I'd thought about this part. Of course I had. I think about everything, it's the one talent I've never had to work at. But I'll tell you what I hadn't packed for, and it's that the moment two strangers look at you, all the calm you drew up for yourself in a quiet room goes off somewhere to have a smoke and leaves you standing there being looked at with a wet bag on your back.

The big one spoke first. "Coming up late."

Not a question. I'd worked out, weeks back in the dry, that the danger in a place like this isn't the wrist. It's the reading. A man who acts hidden gets read as a man with something to hide, and the reading is the whole trap, not the thing itself. So you don't act hidden. I'd told myself that a hundred afternoons. Doing it was a different article, but I'd been getting schooled in that difference the whole climb, and some of it must have soaked in, because I heard myself answer plain, about the tone of a man grumbling about the weather.

"Came up the low way," I said. "Didn't know it was the long way till I was in it."

That got a grunt out of him that might have been a laugh. The low way. I'd said it because it was true — I had come up from under, through the drowned run — and because it was the kind of true a man could hand over without handing over anything. Everybody's come up the wrong way once. It's the safest thing you can say about yourself, that you're a little lost.

The smaller one hadn't said a word, and that was the one I watched, because in any room the one not talking is the one reading. He looked me over the way you'd look over a load somebody was trying to sell you. Not unkind. Just pricing it — the wet boots, the heavy bag, a face he didn't have filed anywhere. I felt his eye go down my arm the way a draft finds the one gap in a wall, and I did the thing I'd drawn up.

I didn't tuck the arm. I didn't turn the wrist away. I lifted the hand and scratched the side of my nose with it, slow, a hand with nowhere it needed to be going about the honest work of a nose, and set it back down easy at my side, sleeve lying where I'd put it. I'll tell you it cost me more than I'd have believed a scratch could cost. On paper the man was calm because I was the one writing him calm. Here I was doing the calm out of my own two hands, one motion at a time, and hoping it came off as nothing, because nothing was the entire trick and I only got the one go at it.

The smaller one's eye moved off my arm and back up to my face. He didn't say anything. But he'd looked, and he'd looked away, and a man who's read you and gone back to being bored is the best thing that can happen to you up here. I'd take bored over interested any day of my life. Bored is where I've always lived.

"Where you making for," the big one said, still not looking up from his bundle.

I'd thought about that too, and the answer was the one thing on the whole climb I'd got right on the first try, which was to have no clever answer. "Up," I said. "Looking for work off the routes. Heard there's men up here that walk loads and need a back."

He grunted again. "There's men up here'll take a back and not pay it." He turned something over in his hands, held it to the lamp, put it down. "Keep on the marked way past here and don't cut off it. The cut-offs flood without telling you. You'll hit a bigger place up top, more lamps, more mouths. Ask there." He said it the flat way you tell a stranger a thing that costs you nothing to tell — not warmth, just the ordinary decency of not letting a man walk into water for want of a sentence.

"I'll do that," I said.

The smaller one reached down without a word and came up with something and tossed it, underhand, and my hands caught it before my head had decided to. It was hard and flat and about the size of my palm — a dried cake of something, fish and meal pressed and gone stiff, the kind of thing that keeps forever because nothing living wants it. Salvager food. I hadn't eaten warm in a day and a night and my stomach turned right over at the smell of it, which was not a good smell, and I did not care one bit about that.

"Long way up," the smaller one said. First words out of him. That was the whole of it.

"Thanks," I said, and I meant it more than the word could carry, and I think he knew, because he'd already looked back at nothing, which is a kindness some people do so you don't have to stand there being grateful at them.

I didn't linger. A man who's passed a look doesn't stand around inviting a second one. I gave the big one a nod he didn't see and went on up past the lamp into the dark again, and the yellow of it fell off my back a step at a time until it was gone and it was just me and the marked way and the drip. I got the cake into my mouth walking, worked a corner of it soft, and it was every bit as bad as it smelled and I ate the whole thing anyway and would have eaten another.

So that was the first of them. I'd walked in among people, dark-wristed and wet and short a good reason, and walked out the other side still just a tired man looking for work, which is what I'd built myself to be and, it turned out, mostly was. I didn't feel clever about it. I felt the way you feel after you've carried something heavy across a room and set it down — not proud, just aware, all of a sudden, of how hard you'd been holding your breath. I kept on up the marked way, chewing the last of the bad cake, one man passing through on his feet, going on.

*End of Chapter 25.*
