## Chapter 42 — The Fire Nobody Owned

Morning down in the yards wasn't morning. There was no sky to go gray, no lamp to come up, just the same fires burning the same and the racket starting a little louder as more men woke into it. I knew a night had passed because my body told me so, and because the poor fire I sat at had gone down to almost nothing and come back twice while I dozed against the cold wall behind me. I hadn't slept the way you sleep in a bed. I'd slept sitting up in a place I didn't trust yet, which is to say I lost some hours and woke not much better than I went down.

The trouble showed itself plain in the light of the second day, if you could call it light. It was this. A man can sit at the edge of a place for a night and be nothing, and the place will let him, because a night is short and a worn man resting off the dark is a thing the yards had seen ten thousand times. But two nights is a different animal. Two nights of a man just sitting is a question, and the yards was a place that asked its questions eventually. I did not want to be sitting there doing nothing when it got around to asking me mine.

The old man from the night before was gone off somewhere, or asleep, I couldn't tell which. But he'd left me the shape of the thing without meaning to. He half-owned the poor fire, he'd shown me without saying so, by feeding it when it sank and staying near it long enough that men stopped seeing him as apart from it. He wasn't its owner. Nobody owned it. But he was of it, the way a pot is of a kitchen, and nobody asks a pot its business.

So I did the one thing there was to do, which was small, and which I could do without a word passing or a coin leaving my pocket. When the fire sank, I fed it. There was a mean stack of broken dunnage and wet driftwood near it, the stuff the yards burned, and I got up slow and put a piece on when the flame wanted one, and sat back down. That was all. Then a while later I did it again. A man feeding a fire he's sitting at is the most ordinary thing in the world, and the good of it was that it read as nothing. Nothing was exactly what I needed to be. But it wasn't nothing, not underneath. It was the first thing I'd done in the yards that the yards could see a reason for. Not a man waiting. A man keeping the fire.

The men who came to warm at it started giving me the small nod you give the one who's there. Not a word. Just the flick of the chin that says I see you and files you as furniture, which is the good kind of being seen. One of them, a broad back with a cough that came up from somewhere bad, jerked his head at the wood when it ran low, and I got up and dragged more over from where it was stacked further off, and he grunted, and that was the whole of it, and it left me more of the fire than I'd had before. I was buying the place with the only coin it took down at the bottom, which was being of use in a way too small to owe anybody anything.

By the middle of the day, if a place with no sky has a middle to its day, the hunger had gone from the low ache I could sit with to a thing that thinned the edges of my sight. I decided I'd let it wait long enough. There was a woman working a pot two fires in, one of the middle-ward fires where the men with something to trade were, and I'd watched a few backs go up to her with a bit of metal and come away with something steaming in a folded leaf. I got up. I felt the leaving of the poor fire like leaving a thing I'd only just started to have, which was a strange thing to feel about a spot of warm stone I'd sat on for a day. I walked over the way a man walks who's done it a hundred times, which I hadn't, and I set one of my mean metal bits down on the board by her pot.

She looked at me the way everyone in the yards looked at me, took the bit, weighed it half a second in her palm, and handed me back a fold of leaf with something hot in it, mostly salt and softness and a little of some deep root boiled down that I didn't know. I didn't ask what it was. Asking what your food is marks you as a man from somewhere the food is known, and I wasn't going to be that man for the price of my own curiosity. I said the one low word you say and carried it back and ate it slow with my fingers, and it was the first thing that wasn't water to go into me since the watering lamp a world ago.

When I sat back down at the poor fire with the warm leaf in my hands, I wasn't the man who'd walked in out of the dark the night before. I was a man who'd been at that fire since yesterday, who fed it when it sank, who'd gone off and got his supper like anybody and come back to his own spot with it. It wasn't much of a spot. It was the poorest corner of a poor place, and it was mine, as much as anything down there was anybody's, by nothing but staying and small use. But it was somewhere a man could wait without the waiting being the thing anybody looked at. I finished the food and I fed the fire and I sat.

*End of Chapter 42.*
