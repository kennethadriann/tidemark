## Chapter 9 — What It Costs to Keep Bailing

The kitchen has a second shift nobody talks about. Most of the place sleeps when the lamps go green-dim, but there's always one person up, sitting near the pot, keeping the small fire small. Tessie told me later it's not a rule. It's just that somebody's always awake down here, so the awake ones might as well be awake together.

That night it was Sol.

I found her there because I'd given up on the mat. You can only watch a water stain so long before it starts watching you back. I came out to the main room and she was at the low table with two cups already poured, like she'd known I'd quit before I did. Maybe she had. She reads people the way I read sentences, except she doesn't need a slate.

"You didn't sleep," she said. Not a question.

"Neither did you."

"I don't, much." She pushed the second cup at me. The fake coffee again, the stuff that tries so hard and gets so close and never arrives. "It's the price of admission. You want to be the one who watches the wall, you give up the easy sleeping. Fair trade, mostly."

I sat. For a while we just held the warm cups and listened to the building tick. Pia was asleep against a folded blanket across the room, her tin of bottle caps by her head where she could reach it. She'd checked for it twice before she went down. A kid who's been moved enough times learns to keep the important things within arm's reach.

"She going to be okay here?" I asked. Stupid question. I knew the answer was *for now,* which is the only answer anybody down here gives.

"She's going to be here," Sol said, which was her version of the same thing. "That's what we've got. Here, and for now. Anybody promises you more than that, they're selling something."

I'm not good at the kind of quiet that's supposed to turn into talking. Up top I'd have filled it with work, or left. Down here there was no work to hide behind and nowhere to go, so I did the thing I never do, which is ask a person about themselves.

"Why do you do it," I said. "The wall. The pulling people out. You said yourself it doesn't empty the sea. You know better than anybody it just moves the hole around. So why you."

She turned her cup a quarter-turn on the table. I'd already learned that was a Sol thing, the quarter-turn, the way some people need their hands busy to let their mouth off the leash.

"I had a brother," she said.

I didn't say anything. You don't, when somebody starts like that.

"Have. I don't know which word." She almost laughed, but it didn't have anywhere happy to go. "Danny. Younger. He was a Reader too, better than me, better than he had any sense to be. He noticed things he should've kept quiet about. I told him. You don't say the wrong tense out loud, I told him, you don't go asking why a number moved before the thing happened that moved it. He couldn't help it. It was like watching somebody stand up straight in a room with a low ceiling."

"They trimmed him," I said.

"They trimmed him." She said it flat, the way I say things when I'm holding them at arm's length so they can't get a hand on me. I recognized the voice. It was mine. "He's still up there. Tier nine, decent water, files allocation tickets all day and goes home and he's *fine,* Adz. That's the part. He's fine. He sleeps. He's not scared of anything because there's nothing in him anymore that knows to be scared. They didn't take him. They took me out of him. There's a Danny walking around up there who never had a sister."

The fire popped. Across the room Pia turned over and went still again.

"I go up sometimes," Sol said. "When I can get clean enough to pass. I stand near him in a queue, or I sit a couple tables off when he eats. He's polite to me. He held a door for me once and said *after you* and didn't know me from the wall." She finally drank, like the sentence needed washing down. "That's the trade I didn't agree to. He's safe and he's warm and he's *gone,* and I'm down here cold and remembering both of us. Out of the two of us I'm not sure I got the better deal."

I thought about Lim humming half a lullaby and saying *Malou* for one second before it slid back under. I thought about how the night before I'd called the whole business *tidiness,* like it was a filing convention, and how a roomful of people had let me say it without throwing me back in the water, which now seemed generous of them.

"That's why the wall," I said, slow, getting it. "It's not about beating the number. You can't get him back. So you—"

"So I make sure the next one doesn't get got without anybody knowing they were a person first." She shrugged, and the shrug cost her something to lift. "If I'd been watching the right slate, somebody might've pulled Danny out before his date instead of after. Nobody was watching. So now I watch. It doesn't bring him back. I'm not confused about that. But it means the next Danny has a Sol who's awake."

I turned my own cup a quarter-turn before I noticed I was doing it. Caught myself. Put my hands flat on the table instead.

"You think it's stupid," she said. There wasn't any accusation in it. She was just reading me again.

"No," I said, and meant it, and was a little surprised to mean it. "I think it's the least efficient thing I've ever heard and I've stopped trusting myself about efficient." I looked at the cup. "I spent my whole life getting the math to come out clean. You're doing the opposite on purpose. I don't have the wiring for it yet. But I don't think it's stupid."

She looked at me a long moment, and something in her face let down half an inch, which down here is the same as a hug.

"Yet," she said.

"What?"

"You said you don't have the wiring for it *yet.*" The almost-smile, the one the whole building can't afford. "That's a hopeful little word for a man who files dull tickets for a living."

I didn't have an answer for that, so I drank the terrible coffee, which had gone lukewarm and somehow improved. We sat with it. The lamps stayed green. Somewhere above us a machine kept doing its sums, patient as the tide, and down here two people who'd each been hollowed out in different rooms sat awake on purpose, keeping a small fire small, while a kid with a tin of bottle caps slept like she belonged to somebody.

She did, I guess. For now, here, which is all anybody was offering.

It turned out I could live with that a little, the *for now* of it, and it wasn't much of a thing to be able to live with, but it was warmer than being right had ever kept me.
