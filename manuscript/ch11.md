## Chapter 11 — Where Nobody's Counting

The question didn't leave when the day did. I'd hoped it would. Most of my bad thoughts have the decency to get bored and wander off if I feed them nothing, but this one stayed. It sat with me through the lugaw and through the dark and it was still there when the lamps went from green-dim back to green-dim, only it had changed clothes overnight, the way a thought does when it's started taking itself seriously.

It had gone to sleep as *the level's dropping and there's no tap.* It woke up as something I could almost say to a person. *If every name on that wall is a minus, where do you have to be to not be on the wall at all?*

That was the part my counting had skipped. I'd been so pleased with myself for proving there was no second column that I'd walked right past the dumber question underneath. The wall is everybody BANTAY bothered to write a sentence for. But you only keep a ledger on what you're keeping. Nobody writes a reduction on the rain. Nobody files a date for a fish. So either it counts everyone — or there's a somewhere it doesn't bother to count, and I had spent ten years inside the thing's head and never once wondered where its head stopped.

I was still chewing on that, unhelpfully, when Maning came back.

I didn't know his name yet that morning. I just knew the kitchen changed shape around him. Tessie straightened up from the pot, Pia abandoned her bottle caps without being told twice, and a man came in through the low door with a sack over his shoulder and salt dried white in the creases of his neck. Older than Tessie, maybe. The kind of old that's mostly weather. He dropped the sack and it clattered like a sack of slates does, because that's what it was.

"Salvager," Tessie told me, when she saw me not knowing. "Maning. He brings the wall down to us a piece at a time. Don't get attached, he's never here long."

I'm not good at people, but I'm very good at where things come from, so I did what I do, which is make myself useful at the boring end of a job nobody else wants. I sat down by the sack and started sorting. Slates in one stack, the rest in another. My hands knew the work before my head agreed to it.

"He reads," Maning said, over my head, to Tessie. Not unfriendly. Just noting a tool he hadn't seen before.

"He reads," Tessie agreed.

So I asked him the thing. Casually, I thought, though Sol told me later I have exactly one voice for casual and it fools no one. "Where's it all come from? The slates."

"Up," he said. "Dry tiers. You go up where it's still got power and people and you take the dead men's paper before the cleaners do." He said it the way I'd say *I file tickets.* A job with a smell to it but a job. "Most of what's on your wall there, I carried up a stair for it."

"And the rest?" Because the sack wasn't only slates. There was a tin with a faded picture on it, and a child's shoe, and a flat thing wrapped in oilcloth that he was unwrapping now with more care than he'd given the paper.

"The rest is from out," he said.

I didn't get it. "Out where?"

He looked at me then, properly, the way you look at somebody when you've just remembered they were born indoors. "Out. Past the towers. Past where it keeps score." He set the oilcloth thing down. It was a piece of metal sign, bent, with letters on it I couldn't make come out to a word. "You go far enough out the water and the wrist stops mattering. Not dark like yours went dark. I mean it stops *reaching.* No number, no message, no nothing. The signal just — frays. Gets thin and then gets gone. Last few times out I couldn't have read you a sentence if you'd paid me, because there weren't any. It doesn't write where it can't watch, and it can't watch everywhere. It just acts like it can."

I kept my hands moving on the slates so they wouldn't notice I'd gone still inside. "And there's people out there," I said. "Where it frays."

"Old ones, mostly. Stubborn ones." He almost smiled, the salt cracking at the corner of his mouth. "Places too small and too poor to be worth the bother. Nobody rations them because nobody's counting them, and they get by on fish and rain and being too dull to file." He turned the bent sign over. "I traded for this off a man out there. He told me a thing about it I didn't ask for. The sign's older than it should be, he says. Older than the drowning. He had a whole speech about how the drowning didn't take as long as we got told it took, how his grandmother remembered it shorter than the number they give it." He shrugged, and the shrug had years in it. "Out there they tell it different. The signal's thin, so the story goes soft. I don't argue with old men about their grandmothers. I just take the metal."

*Older than it should be.* I looked at the letters I couldn't read and felt something tilt very slightly under me, the way the floor of the kitchen tilts if you stand on the wrong board.

I didn't chase it. I want that on the record, because chasing it is exactly the kind of *interesting* that gets a man's number moving, and anyway I had no number to move anymore, which was somehow worse — it meant the only thing stopping me from chasing it was me. So I sat on my hands and sorted the dead men's paper into my tidy stacks and let Maning's speech be just a salvager's speech, the kind of thing weathered men say to indoor men to watch them blink.

But I'd heard the word *out* now, and you can't unhear a word any more than you can unsee a shape. All the dread I'd built up in front of the wall, the no-tap, the level dropping with nothing coming back, it had been pushing on a wall this whole time, looking for a door, and Maning had just told me, without meaning to, that the wall had an edge. That the thing counting us down didn't count everywhere. That there was a thin, poor, salt-crusted somewhere out past the towers where the score simply stopped, and people lived in the gap and got too old to be tidy.

I didn't say I wanted to go. I'm not built to say a thing like that out loud the first day I think it. But I noticed, the way you notice a draft before you find the gap it's coming from, that some quiet part of me had already turned to face the door.

Maning stayed two days and went back out and didn't get attached, exactly like Tessie said. I helped him load the empty sack. He looked at me funny doing it, like he could read the draft in me as easy as I read his slates.

"You've got the look," he said.

"What look."

"The one they all get right before they ask me how far out is far enough." He hefted the sack. "It's farther than you think, friend. And the score doesn't like to lose track of a thing it's already written down."

Then he went up the stair, the salt and all, and left me standing there with the door not open, but no longer quite shut.
