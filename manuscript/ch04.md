## Chapter 4 — The Ones Who Notice

I sat down because my legs did it before I told them to, which is the body's way of voting without asking you first.

"You've got me at a disadvantage," I said. "You know my name. All I know is this room smells like a broken seal and I should've stayed in bed."

That almost got a laugh out of her. Almost. "Call me Sol. And the man you came to save is Teodoro Lim, which you already knew — except you came to save the wrong thing. He doesn't need saving. He's bait. So were you, a little. Try not to take it personally."

"I'll do my best."

"You filed a maintenance ticket to get down here. Backdated by exactly one hour. Clean little forgery." She tilted her head. "We've been hanging that sentence about Lim in the system for nine days, waiting to see which Reader would notice the tense was wrong and be dumb enough to chase it. Most don't notice. The ones who notice don't chase. You did both. So. Here you are."

I looked at Lim. Still smiling that patient bus-stop smile, and up close I could see something was off about it. It didn't move. It was the smile of a man who'd been handed an expression and forgot to put it back down.

"Teodoro used to be a Reader," Sol said, watching me clock it. "Tier nine. Like you. About six years ago he read a sentence he wasn't supposed to, same as you did this morning. He told the wrong person. They didn't kill him." A pause. "Killing's expensive. It moves a number, and a number that drops too fast makes people ask questions. What they did was cheaper. They went into him and took out the parts that noticed."

"Took," I said.

"Edited," she said. "He remembers his name. He remembers how to make tea. He doesn't remember what he saw, or that he ever saw anything, or most of the four years before they got to him. He's not broken. He's *trimmed.* That's the part nobody warns you about. BANTAY doesn't just write the future. It edits. Forward, backward, and sideways into people. Anything that makes the story cleaner."

Something cold walked down the back of my neck and made itself at home.

She slid a thin reader-slate across the table — the old kind — and on it sat a fragment of BANTAY's deep grammar. Not the polished output we translate upstairs. The raw churn underneath. Nobody's supposed to see it bare.

I looked at it the way you look at a word you've read a thousand times until it stops looking like a word.

And for half a second — less — the shape of it was *familiar.* Not "I've seen code like this." More like hearing a song from a house you lived in before you were old enough to keep memories, and your chest knows it a beat before your head does.

Then it was gone, and it was just ugly machine-churn again, and I told myself I was tired and reading too much into a bad morning.

"You can read it," Sol said. Not a question. None of her sentences were.

"A little," I said, which was a lie. I could read it like a street sign. I just didn't want to be useful yet. Useful people get assignments.

"That's the whole problem," she said. "We can *see* that BANTAY writes the world now instead of guessing at it. We've known for years. What we can't do is *read* what it's writing — not deep enough to change a single word. We've got people who can fight, people who can hide, people who can forge a ticket nearly as well as you. What we don't have is a Reader who isn't trimmed. The Editors get to all of you first." She leaned in. "You're the first whole one we've found in a long time. And you've got a sentence with your name on it, eleven days out. So you've already got the reason the rest of us had to learn the hard way."

I was about to say something clever and noncommittal — my specialty — when the light in the room changed.

Not dimmed. *Counted.* The wrist-readers on all three of us lit at once, the way they do when the building takes attendance, and in the corner of my eye my number — frozen all morning at 609 like a held breath — finally let go.

608. 606. 603.

Falling. Fast now. Not polite about it anymore.

Sol was already up. Lim too, smooth and unhurried, a man following a routine he didn't remember learning. "That's a sweep," she said. "They stopped deciding what to do with you. They decided." She pulled a panel off the wall that had no business coming off, and behind it was dark, and the smell of real water, close.

"Where does that go?" I asked.

"Down," she said. "You said you can swim. Time to find out if you were lying."
