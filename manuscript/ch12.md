## Chapter 12 — Far Enough

The thing about a door that isn't quite shut is that you keep finding reasons to walk past it. I told myself I wasn't. I told myself I was only being thorough, the way a man is thorough about a route he has no plan to take, the way you read the back of a medicine bottle for a sickness you don't have. But the two days after Maning left, I caught myself doing arithmetic, and arithmetic is the most honest thing about me. You don't run the numbers on a thing you've genuinely decided against. You only run them on a thing you're getting ready to do and haven't admitted yet.

I started with water, because I always start with water. Old habit, old job. I sat by the wall pretending to sort slates and instead I worked out how much a person carries to go a distance with no tap at the end of it. Then how much two people carry. Then — and I noticed myself doing this, and kept doing it anyway — how much a small one carries, a Pia-sized one, who weighs almost nothing and drinks almost nothing and would think the whole thing was an adventure right up until it wasn't.

That was the tell. You don't provision a trip in your head for three travelers unless some quiet committee in you has already drawn up the list.

I want to be fair to the idea, because it wasn't a stupid one. I've sat with a lot of stupid ideas in my life and I know the weight of them, and this one didn't have that weight. It had the other weight, the bad one, the weight of an idea that actually closes.

Here it is, the way it laid itself out for me in the green-dim while everyone slept. I can't fix the wall. Nobody can. I'd proved that to myself with my own cold skill — every name a minus, no second column, and worse, every name you pull off the board just moves the knife to whoever's standing nearest. We'd saved Pia and somewhere a stranger inherited her death and that was the rule, not the exception. You cannot bail a boat faster than an ocean. I'd known that going in. Tessie knew it and bailed anyway, and I'd called that a kind of grace, and it was. But grace and arithmetic don't always shake hands, and the arithmetic was simple: if the board can't be won, the only winning move is to not be on the board.

And Maning had just told me where the board ended.

Out. Past the towers, past where it keeps score, where the wrist stops reaching and the signal frays to nothing and old stubborn people live on fish and rain because they're too small and too dull to file. I could take the kid. I could find Sol — she came and went, she had her own roads, but I could find her — and I could take the both of them out past the fraying edge and we could be poor and uncounted and alive. Not heroes. Just gone. Let the Spire keep its arithmetic. Let the level drop. It was going to drop with or without me standing here watching it, and at least out there it would drop without my name on the list.

That's not cowardice. I sat with it specifically to see if it was cowardice and it wasn't. It was the engineer's answer. When a system can't be fixed, you stop feeding it. You walk away from the bad machine and you go build a small good thing somewhere it can't reach. I'd done it a hundred times with a hundred dull problems at my desk. This was the same move, only the system was the world and the small good thing was three people staying alive.

I had most of it planned by the second night. I'm telling you that not to brag — there is nothing to brag about — but because it frightened me a little, how fast the lazy man moved once leaving was on the table. I'd never moved that fast toward anything. Prepare, prepare, prepare. I'm very good at getting ready to go.

"You're packing," Sol said.

She was sitting across the dead lamp from me and I hadn't heard her come, which is a thing about these people I still wasn't used to, how they arrive. I hadn't packed a single thing. There was nothing in my hands but a slate I wasn't reading.

"I'm not packing," I said.

"You're packing in your face." She wasn't accusing me. She said it the way you'd tell a man he had soup on his chin. "I've watched a lot of people do it right there where you're sitting. You all get quiet in the same particular way. You start being kind to Pia like you're saving up." She let that land, because it was true and we both heard that it was true. "How far were you going to take her?"

I didn't answer that, which was its own answer.

"Out's real," Sol said. "I won't lie to you and tell you it isn't, the way some people down here do because they need you to stay and bail. It's real. People go. Some of them even make it." She turned the dead lamp a quarter turn on the floor, an idle thing, the way I sort caps when I'm thinking. "But you should know what you're actually choosing, because you're a man who likes to know the whole table, and you're only reading one row of this one."

"Which row am I missing."

"The one where everybody who's still on the wall stays on it," she said. "Out isn't fixing anything, Adz. It's getting your own self off the board and leaving the board exactly where it is. Pia comes, sure. The next Pia doesn't. Tessie doesn't — she's too old for the water and she wouldn't go anyway. Danny doesn't." She said her brother's name flat, the way you set down something heavy so it doesn't make a sound. "He's upstairs not knowing he's got a sister, and out is the one direction that's the same as agreeing he never did."

I didn't have anything to put against that, so I didn't try.

"I'm not telling you to stay," she said, and she meant it, she wasn't running the guilt at me. "Out is a real place and being alive is a real thing and I won't insult either one. I'm telling you there's two doors in this room and you're only looking at the easy one because it's the one that doesn't ask you for anything you haven't already got."

"And the other door," I said.

She looked at me for a while. The fire was almost nothing. Somewhere behind us Pia turned over in her sleep and muttered the name of a color.

"You already named the other door yourself," Sol said. "Days ago. You said the only real fix was to change the question it's answering." She stood, slow, her knees telling her about it. "Out doesn't change the question. Out just stops being in the room when it gets asked."

Then she went to lie down, and left me with the slate I wasn't reading and the two doors she'd put in front of me, and I understood, sitting there, that I'd been provisioning so hard for the easy one because some part of me had already looked at the other one and gone quiet with fear. I didn't pick. I'm not built to pick a thing like that the first night I see it whole. But I stopped counting water. The arithmetic for out was finished and clean and I couldn't make myself add to it anymore, and the other direction had no numbers in it at all, only a swim I didn't want to take, toward a question I didn't yet know how to ask.
