## Chapter 26 — A Face Nobody Knew

The bigger place came on slow and then all at once, the way a town does.

For a good while it was only more of the same, warmer. The way widened. The drip let up. There started to be a lamp on a nail where before there'd been one lamp in a morning. Then the passage turned and gave onto a room I couldn't find the top of, and there were people in it. Not two at a crate. A dozen I could count and more I could hear, spread through a space that somebody had gone to the trouble of making into a place — floor swept down to its grain, crates stacked square, a couple of planks laid over drums with a person behind each one, and lamps enough that the dark got shoved back into the corners instead of owning the room. A pot was going somewhere. The smell of it got to me before anything else did and set my stomach talking again, though it had only just shut up from the cake.

I stopped at the lip of it and did the thing you do, which is stand still a moment and let a room see you're not in a hurry with it.

The wrist I had nearly in hand by now. I'd worn it down the whole climb to a thing I could almost do without minding it — sleeve down, arm loose, a hand that would scratch a nose when it felt like and hang quiet when it didn't. In the little lamp-lit spot below, that had been the whole game, one man's eye going down my arm and me not flinching it off. So I came in ready for that and found out fast it wasn't the game up here. Nobody's eye came hunting my arm. The trouble in this room was slower than that, and bigger, and not really a danger in the sharp sense at all.

It was that everybody in it was made out of everybody else. They knew each other the way you know your own hand in the dark. A woman called a thing across to a man and he laughed before she'd finished it, because he already had the shape of the joke from thirty years of her jokes. A kid ran a bowl from the pot to an old one in a corner without being told whose bowl or which corner. It all ran on knowing. And I was the one thing in the whole room that nobody knew, and there is no packing for that. You can dry a wrist out. You can't hand a room a past you haven't got.

So I was easy to spot and hard to place, which is a poor way to walk into anywhere. Not that anyone stared. It was gentler and worse than staring. It was that when I passed, talk didn't stop but it thinned a half-note, the way a room adjusts around a draft it can't find the source of, and then closed back over behind me. Nobody read me hard. They just filed me, quiet, under *not ours*, and went on. I understood it. I'd have done the same. I *had* done the same, upstairs, to every face that didn't have a number I recognized. It's a strange thing to stand on the wrong side of your own habit.

What I needed off this place was small and it turned out to be dear. I needed a bearing. Which way the up-routes ran from here, and where a man went if he wanted to get on carrying loads — the walkers, the ones who take a stranger on for a back and don't ask him his whole life. Route-work. I wasn't fool enough to say a name, or to say I was after any one man. I just needed the shape of the thing, the way you need to know where the water is in a house before you know anything else about the house.

I tried the plain way first, because it's cheap and I'm cheap. I stopped by the nearest plank-and-drum, where a hard little man was minding a spread of salvage — bolts, a coil of wire, cups without handles — and I asked him, easy, where a fellow went to get taken on walking loads. He looked at me the length of time it takes to decide a thing isn't worth deciding, and he said, "Up," and looked back down at his bolts. That was the whole of it. Up. I'd come a long cold way to be told up by a man selling cups nobody wanted.

I learned the lesson standing there, which is that you don't buy direction with a question in a place like this. A question from a face nobody knows is just a hole you're asking them to pour something into, and nobody pours good water into a stranger's hole. You get it another way, sideways, by first being a small ordinary piece of the room's business instead of a draft passing through it.

So I went to the pot.

The woman working it was broad and unbothered and had the look of someone who'd fed more strangers than she'd remember, which is exactly who you want. I didn't ask her anything. I asked what a bowl cost, which is the one question a stranger is allowed. I had little to trade and she took what I had — I'll not say what, it wasn't much and it was mine to give — and she filled a bowl with something gray that had grain in it and maybe fish, and it was hot, and after a day and a half of cold out of a bag hot was the only word that mattered about it. I stood off to the side and ate it slow to make it last, and while I ate I stopped being the man who'd walked in and became, for a little while, just another back at the pot, which is a smaller and safer thing to be.

It was only after the bowl, when I brought it back and she took it, that I said the thing, and I said it the way you'd say it about the weather. Long way up from here, I said. A man could walk a while looking for the load-walkers and not find where they put in. She wiped the bowl and didn't look up and said the walkers came and went, they didn't sit still to be found, but they watered at the places with lamps and you'd cross their track soon enough if you kept on the marked way and asked at the next lit stop, not the one after, the next one. She said it flat, no warmth to spare, but she said it, which the cup man hadn't, and I understood the difference now was that I'd handed her a bowl's worth of being ordinary first.

"Kept on the marked way," I said, to show her I'd caught it.

"And off the cut-offs," she said, which everyone up here apparently felt they had to tell a man, and maybe they were right to.

I didn't get more and I didn't ask for more. A stranger who takes his one true answer and quits while he's ahead is a stranger who might be let ask again someday. I found a place at the cold edge of the lit ground, near enough the lamps to not be in the dark and far enough to not be in anyone's way, and I sat down on my heels with the bag between my boots and let the warmth of the bowl work its way out through me while the room ran its knowing all around me and past me and over me, and didn't know me, and I sat in it a while, a face nobody knew, resting before I went on.

*End of Chapter 26.*
