## Chapter 37 — The First Face Down There

The far bank sloped up out of the water and then it did what the whole country had been doing, which is go on down into the dark some more, so I gave it my legs again once the shivering had settled into the ordinary background kind and stopped being news. I was wet to the chest now and I stayed that way, the cold sitting in my clothes and my clothes sitting on me, and I walked into it because the only cure for being cold in the deep was being somewhere else, later, and standing still wasn't going to get me there.

I don't know how long I went. The tended line held under my feet, fainter than up top but there, and the way kept the shape it had had since the fork, a worked passage in rock the water had made and gone on making. I'd stopped counting lamps because there weren't any to count. What there was, when it came, was a light that didn't behave like a lamp.

A lamp up the long run sat still and fed, a flame that had been lit by nobody I ever saw and would burn for nobody I ever met. This one moved. Just a little, a small warm shift of it against the far wall of a widening, the way a light moves when a person is near it doing something with his hands. I stopped where I was, back a good way in the dark, and I watched it the way you watch a thing before you decide it's seen you.

It was a man. He had a low fire going in a scoop of the rock, not much of one, enough to boil a can by and throw his shadow big, and he was sat by it on his heel with a load off his back and open at his knee, going through it. I couldn't have told you his age from there. I could tell you he was built the way the far backs were built, all in the shoulders and the thighs, and that he had the deep-country stillness in him, the not-wasting, a man who did the next needed thing and then held until there was a next needed thing and didn't fidget in between. He wasn't waiting on anybody. He was just down there, doing his own work by his own fire, weeks from a face if the tally I'd been told held true.

Here is where the thing I'd packed all that way finally had to come out and be carried, the way everything else had.

Because a man alone at a fire in the deep is not glad to hear a stranger in the dark behind him. There's no good reason for a man to be back there. The honest ones come in loud so you know they're honest. So I did the loud thing, which cost me, being a man whose whole life had been built on the other thing. I scuffed a foot on the stone, plain and clumsy, and I said, "Coming up on you," into the dark ahead, in the flattest tiredest voice I owned, which wasn't much of a stretch to find.

The fire didn't jump. The man's head came round slow and his hand went not to a knife that I saw but to the near edge of his open load, closing it, which told me more about him than a knife would have. He looked into the dark where I was and let me come out of it on my own feet, and I came, slow, hands where a man keeps them when he wants nothing, into the edge of his little light.

He read me. I felt it happen. Not the wrist first, the way I'd braced for all up the low way, but the whole of me, the walk and the wet of a man who'd been going a long time with no load on his back to say why. A far back reads a stranger by his freight before anything, because down here you're the ways you've walked and the weight you've carried, and I stood there carrying nothing but a soaked sack that plainly held a man's own poor kit and not a trade's worth of anything. That was its own kind of question, and I watched it land in his face and sit there.

Then his eye did go down my arm, the way the sharp one's had at the first waystation a lifetime up the way, and found the dark wrist. And this is the part I'd been afraid of the whole descent and it turned out to be near the smallest part of all. I did what I'd drilled into my own hand up the low way, which is nothing, which is let the arm lie easy where a tired man's arm lies and not snatch it back and not shove it at him. But his eye didn't hold on it the way the sharp one's had. His own wrist, when he'd reached to close the load, was as dark as mine. They'd told me once at the Sling that a dark wrist was nothing to a far-way back, and here was the man who made that true. He wasn't reading my arm to catch me out. He was reading it to place me, and a dark wrist this deep placed me as a far back or a fool who'd walked past where fools turn round, and the careful way I'd come out of the dark said at least I wasn't the second kind.

"Long way to be with nothing on your back," he said. It wasn't a threat and it wasn't friendly. It was the plain fact of me, handed back so I could answer it or not.

I'd learned up the way that a bare question buys nothing and a bit of the truth, cheap and offered, buys a little, so I gave him some. "Came down the load-way from the top stop," I said. "Looking for a man works out this far. Salvager. Goes deep, stays gone."

He turned that over. He didn't ask the man's name and I was glad of it, not being ready to spend Maning's name in the dark to a stranger, and not being sure the spending would buy anything but trouble if the deep country was the kind that minded its own. He fed a scrap to his little fire and watched it take.

"Plenty go deep," he said. "Plenty stay gone." Which was a door shut soft, not slammed, and fair, because I'd handed him a whole man's worth of nothing to find one out of the plenty. But he shifted his heel and tipped his chin at the fire, a small mean allowance, the far-country version of the fish-cake tossed over up top. "You'll not dry going," he said. "Sit till you've quit dripping. Then I'll want my quiet back."

So I sat, a stranger's length from a stranger's fire, in the first warmth I'd had since the top stop, and neither of us said much of anything, and that was the whole of it. I'd found a face at the bottom of the world and the face had let me sit. It wasn't the man I'd come for. But it was a man who might, some other fire, some other night, know which of the plenty had gone the way I needed, and I'd walked a long way down to be near the kind of men who'd know. I put my hands out to the little heat and let them ache their way back to being hands.

*End of Chapter 37.*
