## Chapter 31 — Which Way the Far Men Come

I sat on that wall a good while longer than my legs still needed, and I knew I was doing it. A man rests because he's spent. I'd got past spent a bit back and gone quiet instead, which is a different job. It's the one I've always been best at — sit still, let a place go on around you, and read it until you've worked out how to get a thing out of it without looking like you came for anything. Down at the lamps that reading had bought me exactly nothing, because a face with no past behind it can't ask a room for the time of day, let alone a direction. I'd learned that the hard way coming up. But a resting wall isn't a lamp, and the man sitting on it now wasn't quite the one who'd stood in the cup-seller's light with his empty question and got told "Up" and nothing after.

The chewing man to my left was no use, which I'd already guessed. I tried him, because you try the near one first, the way you knock on the near door. I said the loads seemed to come off every way at once up here, and wasn't that a thing. He agreed it was a thing. He kept chewing. That was the whole of what he had for me, and fair enough — I'd have given a stranger about that much myself, most of my life. Some men are a closed shop and there's no rudeness in it. I let him be.

It was the other one that did it, and he did it by asking me first. He came and got down on the wall to my right with the particular long groan of a man whose back has done this same sit ten thousand times, older than me and harder used, and after a while of us both watching the floor he tipped his chin at me and asked where I'd come up from. Not who I was. Where I'd come up from — which way, what load. It took me a breath to understand that up here that *was* the question about who you were. A man is the ways he's walked and the freight he's carried, and there's no more of him worth asking after on a resting wall.

So I told him. The low way, I said. A light one, top stop, dropped it whole this morning. I didn't dress it up and I didn't shave it down, because I'd learned somewhere on the climb that the plain true small thing passes where the clever thing snags, and because for once the plain true small thing was actually a thing I'd done. He took it in and gave a short nod, the kind that closes a ledger. A light one up the low way was nothing to write home about and we both knew it. But it was a real line and it was mine, and it turned out that was the coin the wall ran on. Say a true small thing about your own back and the man beside you will say one back. It's not friendship. It's more like two men showing each other they're carrying the same kind of tired.

That's when I could ask. Not the bare question that had bought nothing at the lamps, the *up* that hangs in the air with no place to land. A different one, from inside now, the kind a back asks when he's thinking about his next work and where the pay actually is. I said the near ways all seemed to feed down into this one field, which they did, but a man had to figure the real salvage — the deep stuff, the junk that came from out past everything — didn't get pulled by fellows carrying light ones up the low way. Where did the far men work, the ones who brought the deep loads in.

He didn't answer it the way the cup-seller had, with a shrug and a word to get rid of me. He answered it the way you answer a man who's earned the standing to have asked. The far men, he said, didn't much bother with the field. You'd see them come through it, is all, when they came down with a season's haul to hand off, gone again before you'd got a good look. Their work was out past the top stop, up the long run — he moved his chin at the high side of the field, the side the walkers kept climbing off into — three, four lamps on, maybe more, out to the yards where the ways stopped being friendly and forked out into the deep drowned ground. That's where the real junk was and that's where the men who went for it lived, more or less, in the weeks they weren't down selling it. Out and up. Days, not a morning. Not a place a light-one back had any call to be.

He said it flat, no warning in it, just the shape of the country the way an old walker holds the shape of country — the long run, the lamps thinning, the yards where it forked out into the deep. I turned it over and it fit the little I already knew of the man I was after — the junk from out, the far going, the weeks gone at a time. It fit and it moved nothing. A direction is not a man. Out and up past the top stop was still a great deal of dark with no one standing in it that I could see, and days of it, and the whole of what I'd bought with my one true line was the knowing which way to point my legs when they were ready to go on being legs again. Which, right then, they were not.

I said my thanks, or the wall's version of it, which is to nod and let it sit. He went back to watching the floor and so did I. Loads came down the long run and loads went up it, and I watched the high side of the field a while, the side he'd pointed at, where the ways went off into the dark toward the yards. Then I stopped watching it and looked at my own hands for a bit, and I stayed on the wall.

*End of Chapter 31.*
