## Chapter 2 — Be Boring

Standing up was a mistake.

Sixty Readers, sixty desks, and the only sounds in the Lexicon are breathing and typing. You stand up fast in a room like that and people look. Not because they care about you. Because looking at you for a second is more interesting than reading about who's going to die in tier 30.

So I did the smartest thing I've ever done under pressure. I sat back down.

Here's a thing about BANTAY that most people don't get. It isn't really watching you. Not you, specifically. It can't. There's too many of us and not enough of it, so it does exactly what I'd do — it watches the interesting ones and lets a script handle everybody else. The riots, the cost-cases, the numbers that spike or crash. The rest of us are background. Noise. Boring.

I have spent my entire life being boring. Turns out I was training for this and didn't know it.

So I didn't run. Running is interesting. I picked my coffee back up — lukewarm, rationed, genuinely bad — and went back to looking like a man doing his job. And under the desk, where the cameras can't be bothered to look, I woke Tuko up properly.

Talking to Tuko isn't really talking. It's more like leaving notes for a faster, smarter version of me. One that doesn't get tired and doesn't get scared. I gave it three things.

One: find out who sent that message. No name means it came from somewhere that didn't want a name, and I wanted to know where that was.

Two: is Teodoro Lim a real person I can actually get to, or just a row in a table.

Three — and I almost didn't ask this one, because some questions you can't un-ask —

*Pull my file.*

My number had stopped at 609. Just stopped. Which was almost worse than watching it drop, because numbers don't stop on their own. Something had a finger on mine. And it wanted me to know it could put it there whenever it felt like it.

Tuko came back faster than I wanted.

The message didn't come from upstairs. It didn't come from anywhere in the building. As far as Tuko could tell, it came from inside BANTAY itself. Not a person using BANTAY. BANTAY. Which isn't supposed to be possible, because BANTAY doesn't talk to people. It talks to us so we can talk to people. That's the entire reason I have a job.

Lim was real. Tier 22, just like it said. Tuko even dug up his water number, which is a thing you are very much not supposed to be able to see. It was low. The man was thirsty.

And then my file.

I almost told Tuko to stop. I'm telling you that so you don't get the wrong idea about me. I'm not brave. I'm the guy who reads the warning label twice and then puts the thing back on the shelf.

But I read it.

There it was, near the bottom, in that same flat dead font. An event. Mine. Dated eleven days out.

And it was written in the past tense.

I'm not going to tell you yet what it said. I just sat there and read my own future like it was something that had already happened to some other guy, while my coffee went cold and somebody across the room laughed at a joke I didn't hear and the pumps kept breathing under the floor like nothing was wrong with the world.

Somebody had already written me down.

Eleven days.

I put the coffee down. *Okay,* I thought. *Okay. New plan.*

For the first time in my careful little life, I was going to have to do something before I was ready.
