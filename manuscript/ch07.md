## Chapter 7 — The Wall of People Who Haven't Died Yet

You don't sleep your first night underwater. You lie on a borrowed mat in a borrowed shirt and you listen to a drowned city tick and settle around you, and you do the math on your own life, which is a bad thing to do in the dark and the only thing I'm any good at.

Eleven days, the sentence said. One of those was nearly gone already, spent on black water and lugaw and a man remembering his wife's name for one second. Ten, then. I lay there and watched a water stain on the ceiling that looked like nothing and counted to ten over and over until counting felt like company.

Sol came for me when the lamps brightened — their version of morning, somebody somewhere deciding it was time to pretend. She had two cups of something that wanted very badly to be coffee and was not. I drank mine anyway. Down here you don't turn down warm.

"You should see what you're for," she said. "Before you decide anything. People who decide before they see end up deciding wrong and feeling good about it."

She led me down a corridor that used to sell shoes — you could tell, there were still the little slanted benches, the ghost of a place where people sat and frowned at their own feet — and at the end of it was a room, and in the room was a wall, and on the wall were the dead.

Except they weren't dead. That was the whole thing.

They were slates. Hundreds of them. Salvaged reader-slates and scraps of panel and torn squares of the old slick paper, each one pinned up with a name, a tier, a date — and a sentence. The flat dead font. The wrong tense. *Resolved. Reduced. A cost recovered.* All of it written like it had already happened, all of it dated for days that hadn't come yet. A wall of obituaries for the living.

"We pull what we can out of the system," Sol said, low, the way you talk in a church or a hospital. "Every sentence BANTAY writes that ends a person, we try to catch it before the date. Sometimes we get them out in time. Move them down here, off the grid, before the day arrives." She looked at the wall. "Most of the time we just get to know their names before they go. So somebody knew."

I walked the wall. I didn't want to and I did it anyway, because not looking would've been worse. So many of them. A man dated for tomorrow. A whole family dated for the same Tuesday, which meant a tier was being thinned and the math had simply reached their door. Numbers with names attached, waiting in line to stop being either.

And then I got to one near the bottom, pinned low, at a child's height, and I stopped.

*PIA. Tier under-count. Nine days. A reduction, foreseen.*

I knew the handwriting of that sentence before I let myself know the name. And then I knew the name, because I'd watched her the night before, sitting on the floor of a kitchen sorting bottle caps by a logic only she understood, seven years old at the bottom of the world.

"That's why you're for," Sol said quietly. "We can see it says she goes in nine days. We can't read *why,* or whether it's the kind of thing that bends. You can. So tell me, Reader." Her voice didn't shake. She'd clearly made sure of that a long time ago. "Tell me if I can save her, or if I just get to learn her name."

So I read it.

I won't pretend I can explain what reading deep is like to somebody who's never done it. It's not looking at words. It's putting your hand flat on the thing and feeling for the grain — where the system *meant* it and where it just filled a gap. Some sentences are load-bearing. You can feel them holding weight, whole structures resting on top of them, and you know if you pull that one the whole story BANTAY's telling sags. And some are thin. Convenient. A number that needed to come down by one, and her row happened to be nearest the knife.

Pia's was thin.

"It bends," I said, and Sol breathed out, and for half a second I felt like a man who'd done something good.

Then I kept reading, because I'm careful, because I always take the one extra pass, and the one extra pass is where the floor dropped out.

"It bends," I said again, slower. "But it doesn't matter."

"What does that mean."

"It means she's not the point. The point is the number. Tier's over-count by one, so the system wrote a sentence to bring it down by one, and her name's only there because it was cheapest. You pull her out tonight — you can, the sentence is thin, it'll let her go." I heard my own voice going flat, going to the place it goes when it's protecting me. "And then tomorrow it writes a new one. Different name. Whoever's nearest the knife next. Because you didn't fix the thing it wanted. You just changed which kid was standing closest."

The room was very quiet. Down the corridor a baby was fussing, and somebody shushed it, and the sound carried the way sound carries underwater, too clearly.

"We've felt that," Sol said. Her jaw was tight. "We never had the words for it. We save one and it's like the water — you push it out of one room and it just comes up in the next. We thought we were doing something wrong."

"You're not doing anything wrong. You're emptying a boat with a cup. The hole's still there." I made myself look at the slate. *Pia. A reduction, foreseen.* "You can't argue with the sentences. They're just the answer. You'd have to change the question it's answering." I said it like a small technical fact, the way I'd say a query was hitting the wrong table. It didn't land in me as a plan yet. It just sat there, true and enormous, the way the dangerous ones do.

Sol was watching me again, reading the grammar of me. "Change the question," she repeated. "Where do you do that. Where does the question even live."

"I don't know," I said. Which was true. I didn't, then.

But some careful, lazy part of me had already started to want to find out — and that's the part of me that's always been the most dangerous, because once it wants to solve something, it doesn't have an off switch. I'd built my whole life around not letting it wake up. It does less damage asleep.

I looked at the wall a while longer. All those names, waiting their turn.

"Show me the rest," I said.

I hadn't meant to ask for an assignment. I never ask for the assignment. But there it was, out of my mouth, and Sol nodded like she'd been waiting for it, and somewhere far above us a machine kept doing its sums.
