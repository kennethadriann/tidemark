## Chapter 32 — The Long Run

I got up off the wall the next morning, which is a small thing to say and was a large thing to do. I'd had a night on the resting wall and a bowl from the pot and a few hours where nobody wanted anything off me, and my body had taken to the arrangement the way a body will. It had voted, without asking me, to stay. The soft man's whole life is that vote. Somewhere warm, somewhere fed, somewhere the trouble is other people's, and a hundred good reasons why tomorrow would be the better day to move. I knew the reasons. I'd written most of them myself, over the years, in a nicer hand. So I made a point of standing up while the reasons were still rubbing the sleep out of their eyes, before they got organized.

The high side of the field was the side the walkers kept climbing off into, the way the old back had tipped his chin at. In the morning it didn't look like much of anything. Just another mouth in the rock where a marked way went up and out, no different to look at than the one I'd come in by, except that the men going into it went in loaded and serious and didn't come back through for a good long while. I stood at the foot of it a minute the way you stand at the foot of a thing, and then I went up into it, and the field started going quiet behind me almost at once.

That was the whole of the chapter of it, if you want the truth. The going-quiet. I'd spent two days climbing up into more and more noise, lamp by lamp, each stop bigger and busier than the last, until the top stop sat there at the top of the climb like the loud beating middle of everything. I'd got it in my head, without ever saying it, that up meant toward. Toward people, toward the thick of it, toward the place the ways were all going. And here I was going up still, up the long run same as before, and the noise was falling off behind me instead of building. The pot-smell thinned out. The woman calling her price at nobody got smaller and then got gone. Before long it was my own feet again, and the drip, and the dark ahead that hadn't been walked into yet that day.

So the top stop wasn't the top of anything. It was just the last crowded place. That took some getting used to, up in my head, where I keep the map of how things are supposed to go. The busiest room I'd found wasn't the destination. It was the last porch light before the road went dark, and I'd walked out past it, and the country on the far side of it didn't care how loud the field had been. It went thin. The marked way was still marked. I could read the good side under my feet, the tended stone, the old smoke gone into the walls. But it was marked thinner, older, handled less. Fewer feet had said this is the way here. The men who came this far didn't need telling twice.

And the standing I'd spent two days earning didn't come with me. That was the part I felt in the shoulders more than the head. Back at the field I'd been a made back, a light one up the low way, dropped it whole, a line in a slate, invisible in the good way. It had taken everything I had to buy that much, the reading and the hands and the one true small thing said to the right man, and it had been worth it, and it was worth exactly nothing three lamps up the long run. Out here there was no slate with my line in it. There was nobody who'd seen me set a load down whole. If I came on a man up here he'd read me the way the sharp one at the first waystation had read me, off my back and my hands, a stranger with no past he could hand over, and I'd be doing the whole grind again from the bottom. I'd carried a lot of things up that climb. That one I'd had to leave on the wall with the rest of the resting backs, and it stayed there.

I won't pretend I didn't feel the size of what was still in front of me. Days, the man had said, not a morning. Out and up, past where the lamps thinned, to the yards where the ways forked out into the deep drowned ground, and the far men out there weeks at a stretch. I'd bought a direction and nothing else. A direction, it turns out, is a thing you can hold in one hand and it weighs nothing and it feeds you not at all. It only tells you which of the dark ahead is the right dark. Which is more than I'd had the morning before, and I made myself count it, because a man who won't count the little he's got will talk himself into thinking he's got nothing and sit back down. I'd been that man. I knew the trick of him.

The way tilted up, easy at first, the good side holding. I found the walking pace my body had learned over two days without me telling it to, the one that doesn't spend everything in the first hour, and I let it have that. There's a kind of quiet you only get when there's no one to perform being all right for, and I was in it now, further into it than I'd been since the black water under the line. Nobody up ahead I knew was needing me yet. Nobody behind who'd notice I'd gone. Just the long run going up into its own dark, and the drip, and the marked way getting thinner under my feet, and me on it, going.

I stopped once where the way widened into a dry bend and got my breath and drank a mouthful I told myself was earned. Behind and below, if I turned, there'd have been the top of the field's glow somewhere down the way, the last of the light I'd walked out of. I didn't turn to check. It didn't need checking. I put the water away and picked the pace back up and went on up the long run into the thin of it, one foot in front of the other, which by then was the only trick I fully trusted.

*End of Chapter 32.*
