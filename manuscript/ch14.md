## Chapter 14 — The Smooth Place

For two days back at the kitchen I tried to set the old woman's mother down where I set things I can't use, and she would not stay put.

It's a real shelf I have. Anybody who works with numbers all day builds one. You get a figure that doesn't fit and you can't make it fit and you can't throw it out either, so you set it on a shelf at the back, label it "later," and get on with the rows that behave. Most of what's on that shelf stays there forever and never costs you a thing. I'd put a hundred wrong numbers up there in my life and slept fine under all of them.

This one kept coming down on its own. I'd be stirring the pot or sorting Pia's caps into colors she'd lost interest in, and there it would be again, sitting in my chest in the spot where the weather used to press. *Her mother walked these streets when they were dry.* I'd turn it over, and it was the same as it had been out at the edge. It would not get small.

So I did the thing I do, because it's the only thing I'm any good at. I stopped fighting the number and started reading it.

What had caught me, out there, wasn't that the old woman said something wild. People say wild things. What caught me was *how* she said it. She'd hung a price of rice off the side of it, and a season, and some complaint about her knees. That's how I knew it was true and not a tall tale. A made-up memory comes clean. A real one comes with junk attached, some stupid little thing riding along that nobody would ever bother to invent. The junk is the proof. You can't fake the junk.

I don't know exactly when I turned that around and pointed it at myself. It wasn't on purpose. I was watching Lim.

He was by the wall humming, the way he does in the evenings, the tune that almost becomes the lullaby and then doesn't. I'd heard it a dozen times by then. That night I really listened, and I heard the shape of the hole in it. He'd go along fine, four bars, five, and then there'd be a place where the song just smoothed out — not a wrong note, not a stumble, just a stretch of nothing where a thing used to be — and he'd sail right over it and pick up clean on the other side and never once flinch. He didn't hear the gap. That was the awful part of watching him. From outside you could see the missing piece plain as a tooth gone from a smile. From inside it was just floor. He hummed across the hole like it had always been floor.

Malou, he'd said once, that first week, and then it was gone and he never knew he'd said it.

I sat with that a while, and then, the way you scratch a thing you've been told not to scratch, I reached back into my own head to check that everything was still there.

It's a stupid impulse. You can't audit yourself; I knew that even as I did it. But I started easy, with junk I knew the shape of. My mother's hands. The smell of the corridor I grew up in, which was rust and somebody's cooking oil and, for one bad year, a dead rat in the wall nobody could find. The teacher who pinched. All of it came up the right way, junk and all, the season attached, the stupid little thing riding along. Good. Fine. The texture was there. I almost left it alone.

Then I reached for the one thing I'm most made of, and there was nothing attached to it.

Here is what I mean. I have a rule, and I have had it as long as I have had anything. *Don't be interesting.* Stay boring. Don't stand up when sitting down will do. Prepare, never act; get so ready for a thing that you never have to do it. It's not a habit, it's the floor I'm built on. It's the first thing about me and the last. You met me on day one being careful at a sentence that wanted reading and you watched me sit back down because standing was *interesting,* and that was the truest thing you'll ever see me do.

A man should remember learning a rule that deep. A scare, a hiding, a parent gripping your arm too hard and telling you why. Mine cost me something to learn; rules like that always do. So I reached for the cost. I reached for the day I learned to be careful, the way I'd just reached for the rat in the wall.

And it came up smooth.

Not wrong. Not missing in a way I could point at. The rule was *there,* solid, more solid than my mother's hands. But there was no season on it. No price of rice. No stupid little thing riding along. It sat in me clean and finished and old, with nothing attached, the way the hundred years sits up the line — except the hundred years at least got smooth from a hundred years of handling, worn down honest by all the mouths it passed through. This wasn't worn smooth. I'd handled it my whole life and it hadn't worn at all. It had come to me already smooth, the way Lim's song was already smooth across the place where his Malou used to be.

I want to be careful here, because I'm a man who distrusts a tidy answer, and the tidy answer was right there and it was the worst thing I'd ever thought, so I held it off. Maybe I just don't remember being small. Plenty of people don't. Maybe the rule's so old it predates the part of me that keeps junk. Maybe I'm doing to myself exactly what I warned the old woman against, taking one number that won't sit and building a whole crooked house on it.

But I'd watched Lim hum across his hole all evening and never feel it, and the thing about a hole, the thing that makes it a hole and not just an empty shelf, is that you can't feel it from inside. That's the whole cruelty of it. Danny's upstairs right now living in a smooth place where a sister used to be, and he's *fine,* he'd tell you he's fine, because the floor goes all the way across. You don't get to notice the thing that's been taken. The taking includes the not-noticing. That's the craft of it.

So I sat by the dead lamp with the question I had no right to be able to ask and no way to answer, and it was the same question as the old woman's, only turned around to face me. Up the line I could always shrink a wrong number until it fit somewhere I didn't have to look. This one I couldn't shrink, and now there were two of them, hers and mine, and mine was worse, because hers was only about when the world drowned.

Mine was about whether *careful* was ever mine, or whether somebody handed it to me already smooth, the way you'd hand a man a leash and tell him it was his own good sense.

Pia turned over in her sleep and said a color. Green, I think. I didn't pick up the thread, didn't follow it anywhere, didn't decide a thing. I just sat there feeling the floor of myself for the first time and not trusting that it was floor.
