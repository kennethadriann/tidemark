## Chapter 18 — The Shape of Days

Sol was gone the better part of a day, and I spent it not waiting, which is a thing I happen to be good at. Waiting is just preparing with nothing to lay on the table, so I found things for the table. I read the slates I'd read already. I helped Lim re-rope a stack that had slid in the night. I counted the jugs, because counting jugs had turned into a thing my hands did the way other men crack their knuckles, and it came out the same as before, eleven and a heel, three down, and I put the spoon back in the pot and didn't say a word to anybody about a level that only drops.

When she came back down through the wet I knew she had it before she opened her mouth. Sol carries a fact the way Maning carries junk. You can see the weight of it sitting on her, see where it's roped on. She came in and took the cup Tessie put out for her and drank half of it standing, the way they all drink that first cup, like the kitchen is something you have to get inside you before you can talk. Then she said, "I found the when."

I didn't answer. I'd sent for one fact, and here it was, and the funny thing is that the second it was in the room I didn't want it anymore. That's the trouble with sending for a thing. It comes back.

"He's not due down for a while," she said, and for half a breath I felt the relief land — the lazy man's relief, the *good, then, not today* — and I had actually started to set my shoulders down when she kept going and took it back out of my hands. "Because he already went up. Past where he usually works. Deeper in this run, after some load a fellow told him about. Up where the rooms are dry and the signal's thick." She turned the cup. "He won't come back down till he's got it. The water's good up there. He's in no hurry."

I sat with that a minute, and the minute did something to me I want to put down plainly, because it's the whole of the chapter and it isn't an event. Nothing happened. A fact just changed shape in my head.

Up to that morning the owing had been a floor. I've said that. A low thing, solid, somewhere to stand. Maning's name on it, dated out, *days, not hours,* and *days* is a word you can live inside. Days is a hallway. There's room in days for one more cup of lugaw and one more night of not deciding and one more morning of laying things on the table. I'd been standing on the owing like it was the ground and not noticing that the ground was sloped.

The fact tilted it. Not the date — the date hadn't moved. What moved was the part I could count. *He already went up. Deeper in. Won't come down till he's got the load.* I did the arithmetic the way I do all of it, without meaning to, all at once. If the man's gone up past his usual rooms, then the days I had weren't days to *get ready* in. They were days getting *spent.* Every one he sat up there in the dry with his good water was a day off the front of the window, not the back. The date stayed put and the road to it got shorter while I drank my breakfast. That's a different animal than a floor. That's the eleven jugs again. For weeks the wall had been a thing I read, a flat table of other people's subtractions, and then Tessie handed me a count of jugs and the subtraction got a number I could feel in my arms when I lifted one. This was that. The owing had been a direction. Now it was a number, and the number was going down whether or not I touched it, the same as the jugs and the water that thing keeps its hand on.

"Days," I said, to hear it. "How many is days."

"Nobody up there's counting in days." Sol said it gentle, because she knew what I was asking and knew there wasn't an honest answer. "I got you the when, Adz. The when is: he's up, he's deep, and he's not in a hurry to come down. That's the truest when there is."

It was. I knew it was. The smallest fact I could've sent for, and it had gone and gotten heavy on the way back, the way they do.

There was a thing she didn't say and I didn't make her say it, because I'd had the same thought a beat before she walked in, and it's the kind of thought you'd rather find in your own head than be handed. Maning had gone *up.* Up is where the signal's thick. Up is where the wrist works and the weather sits back down on your neck and the count can see you fine. A man who's been written down, walking deeper into the part of the world where the writing reads clearest. Maning told me once, the first night I met him, that the score doesn't like losing track of what it's already put down. And here he was, of his own free want, no idea, going to stand right where it could keep its eye on him. Going toward his own date the way water goes downhill, because the water's good up there and somebody mentioned a load.

I didn't say any of that to the room. You don't.

What I did — and I'll cop to how small it is — is I asked Sol the route. Not whether I'd take it. The route. "When he's up past his rooms," I said, "which way's the line go. Where does it get dry." And she told me, plain, the way you tell a man a thing he could just be curious about, and I listened the way I listen, laying it on the table, this stair and that flooded run and where the wet stops and the dry starts and the wrist comes alive. A second small thing on the table next to the first one. I wasn't pulling the thread. I was just letting it lie there where I could see both ends of it, and noticing, again, that I hadn't reached for the knife.

The far door was still cold behind me. *Down, not out.* I still didn't turn around to it. One door. The near one had a man's name on it and now it had a road, and the road had a length, and the length was getting shorter on its own while I sat there learning it.

I ate. Tessie watched me eat the way she watches everything, not saying. Some mornings the owing only asks for one small fact. The trouble is the fact won't always stay one fact, and it won't stay the size you sent for, and once it's a number you can count, it starts counting itself.

*End of Chapter 18.*
