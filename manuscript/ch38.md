## Chapter 38 — What the Quiet Bought

So I sat and dried, which is a slower thing than it sounds when you're wet to the chest and the fire is the size of a fire a man builds when the wood is his own back's worth of carried scraps. The little heat did what little heat does. It made the front of me feel warm enough to notice how cold the back of me still was, and then it traded them around when I turned, and that was the whole of drying, a long trading-around that fools you into thinking you're getting somewhere until you put your hand inside your own shirt and find the wet still living in there like it paid rent.

The far man went back to his load. He'd closed it when I came out of the dark, and now that I'd sat and turned out to be nothing worse than a wet fool with no freight, he opened it again and went on with whatever the going-through was. Sorting, I think, though I didn't lean to look. A man's open load in the deep is his own business and a stranger who studies it is a stranger asking to be moved along. I watched the fire instead, and the edges of him past it, the way his hands went to a thing and knew it and set it where it went without his eyes seeming much involved. It was the same not-wasting I'd clocked in him from back in the dark. Down here a motion you didn't need was a motion that cost you, and he'd long since quit spending them.

The quiet sat between us and I let it. That was the thing I'd have got wrong a month ago, or however long ago it was that I'd been a man who filled a silence because a silence felt like a bill coming due. I wanted to ask him more. I had a whole second helping of questions banked up behind my teeth, where is the yards from here and how far and which way at the next split and have you ever in your work heard of a man, and I sat on every one of them. I'd already spent my one real line and got the door shut soft for it. I knew by now that you don't get a shut door open by knocking harder. You get it open, if you get it open at all, by being the kind of nothing a man doesn't mind having near his fire, long enough that keeping the door shut starts to cost him more than the easing of it would.

So my whole work, sitting there, was to be no trouble. It's a strange labor to do on purpose, being no trouble, for a man who'd made a career of it by accident. I did it anyway. I dried, and I turned, and I asked him not one thing, and when the wet had gone from a soak to a damp and the fire had given me back enough of my hands to make fists with, I got up slow, the way you get up near a man who hasn't decided about you yet, and I told him I'd take his quiet off his hands and thanked him for the loan of the fire.

That's when it bought.

He didn't look up all the way. He fed his little flame another scrap and watched it catch, and he said, like it was a thing he'd decided somewhere in the last hour of me being no trouble, "You'll want to keep on down, then out." He tipped his head at the dark past his fire, the way I'd walked in from, only farther on. "The way splits again, worse than the one back up. Take the low wet side, not the dry. The dry one climbs off to a working that's played out, nobody down it but rats and old rope. Low wet side runs you out to the yards. Day, day and a bit, if your legs are honest and you don't sleep long."

I said the yards back to him, plain, not a question, just to hold the word where I could keep it.

"The yards," he said. "Where the deep stuff comes in off the far work and goes onto fresh backs for the haul up. You'll know it. It'll be the first place down here with more than one fire." He said that last with something that wasn't quite a smile but was in the neighborhood of one, a man telling you the country you're headed into by telling you what you'd been starved of, which was other men. Then the near thing, the thing worth the whole day and a bit I'd have to spend to use it. "The kind you're after, the ones that go out and stay out and come back heavy, they don't live at the yards. They pass through it. Hand off, take on, gone again. If a man like that's still breathing, the yards is where you'd catch the smell of him. Not the man. The talk of the man."

That was more than I'd been given by anybody since the top stop, and he'd handed it to me for the price of an hour of shutting up. I didn't grab at it. I'd learned that too. I took it the way you take a full pot passed to you, both hands, no sudden move, and I set it down careful inside me next to the bearing I'd been carrying since the wall, which it did not replace so much as sharpen. Out and down, low wet side, a day and a bit, to a place with more than one fire, where the talk of the men who go gone comes in even when the men don't. It wasn't the man. But it was the nearest thing to a where I'd had, and it came from a mouth that had walked it, not a wall that had only heard.

"I'll leave you your quiet," I said again, because I meant to and because a man who's given you a day's worth of direction has earned not being sat on any longer than the fire took.

He'd already gone back into his load. "Low wet side," he said, to the load and not to me, which was as much goodbye as the deep country hands out and more than I'd any right to expect. I stepped back out of his little light into the cold that had been waiting for me the whole time, patient as everything down here was patient, and I found the down again with my feet and gave it my legs, carrying the word for where I was going the way I'd carried the word for who, both of them still a good way off, and both of them, for the first time in a long dark while, pointed the same direction.

*End of Chapter 38.*
