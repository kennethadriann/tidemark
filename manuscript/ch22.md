## Chapter 22 — The Cold Part

I went in the morning, early, before the kitchen was properly awake, and I've turned it over since and the honest reason is that there was nobody up to watch me not do it.

That's smaller than it sounds and it's the whole thing. Every other time I'd stood at that water there'd been a room behind me — Tessie clocking, Lim over his caps, the girl counting wrong and starting again — and a room behind you is an audience, and an audience is a reason. You can stand at the edge of a thing a long while if there are people around to make the standing look like thinking. Take the people away and it stops looking like thinking. It just looks like a man being cold on purpose for no result. I woke before the light, or before what passes for light down here, and the pot was banked and the shapes on the floor were all breathing slow, and there was no one to perform the hesitation for. So I quit performing it. I got up quiet and went to the foot of the run, where the bag had been waiting since I'd set it down there, patient as a dog that knows the walk is coming before you do.

I picked it up. On the shoulder again, the weight going into the place it had already worn for itself. Nothing said. There was nobody to say it to, which turned out to be the point.

The water was where I'd left it, and I was stupidly surprised by that. Some part of me had half-hoped it would've drained in the night, or grown a bridge, or done one of the small miracles that let a man off the hook without him being the one who climbs off it. It hadn't. It sat there black and flat with the cold standing up off it, in no hurry, the way water gets when it's been under the world long enough to forget there was ever an above.

I stood at the edge. My feet did their old stalling thing, counting a step that wasn't there. And I waited, one last time, out of pure habit, for the decision to show up — some clean interior click, a vote coming in, a spot I could point at afterward and say there, that's where I chose it. It didn't come. It has not come once in this whole business, and I think I finally stopped waiting for it right about then, on the wet lip in the almost-dark. There wasn't going to be a click. There was going to be a foot in the water or a foot not in the water, and the whole difference between the two was a foot in the water, and nothing else. No music with it.

So I put my foot in the water.

The cold came up my leg the way I remembered from the first swim, the night Sol sent me under the line — it doesn't arrive all at once, it bills you in installments. The ankle first, a clean hard band of it. Then the shin as the water climbed the boot, then the knee, each one its own small argument I was already too far in to win. I brought the other foot down beside it, because standing one-legged in cold water is worse than being in it to both, and then there was no edge left behind me, only the floor sloping down and the black closing round my thighs, and then the waist, and there it got me the way it gets everybody, that flinch up through the belly and the breath going high and fast.

I stood in it to the waist and did not turn around. That part I'll keep for myself, quietly, as the nearest thing to a decision I managed all day — not the stepping in. The stepping in my feet did on their own, same as the packing and the carrying before it. The not-turning-back-out was the one piece I had to do with the part of me that votes, and it voted by doing nothing, by just standing there wet and letting the cold finish climbing me instead of hauling myself back up the dry lip to the pot and the morning and the long pleasant hallway I knew was still there behind me if I wanted it.

Then I went. Not bravely and not fast. You wade a drowned run the way you'd cross a dark floor you don't trust, feeling ahead with the front foot before you give it weight, because a flooded run is full of things that used to be floor and aren't anymore. The bag rode up light as the water took its own weight off my back, and then dragged and streamed as I climbed the far slope and it came up out of the water with me, heavy, slapping cold against my hip. My clothes hung off me at twice their weight. Wet at the bottom, dry at the top, I'd said, like it was a fact about the route. It's a fact about you. You're the thing that's wet at the bottom now, and you carry the wet up into the dry and stand there dripping in the part that was supposed to be dry, ruining it a little just by showing up.

The dark up the far side was a different dark than the kitchen's. The kitchen dark has people in it. This one only had the line going up, narrow, dripping, the ceiling low enough in stretches that I'd have to stoop, the smell of old water and rust and a faint moving air that means a passage goes somewhere. Somewhere up it, days off, a stubborn man was chasing a load with no notion his name had been written down and carried past him on somebody's back. I wasn't going to reach him today. Today was the wet part and the first cold length of the dry, and my own breath loud in a low place, and that was the whole of it.

I went up into it. Behind me the water closed back flat over the way I'd come, the way it does, keeping no record of who'd been through. Ahead the line climbed off into the dark and I climbed after it, wet to the waist and getting colder, one hand on the cold wall for the counting of it, going.

*End of Chapter 22.*
