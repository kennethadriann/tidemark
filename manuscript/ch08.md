## Chapter 8 — The Boat With the Hole In It

I expected the room to argue. That's what I'd have done — laid the problem on the table and let everybody fight about it, because a fight feels like progress even when it isn't. Up top, that's how we decide things. We argue until the loudest tired person wins, and then we call it a plan.

Nobody argued.

I'd said my piece — that pulling Pia out tonight was real, the sentence was thin, it would let her go, and that it wouldn't *matter*, because the system would just write a new death for whoever stood nearest the knife tomorrow. I'd laid it out clean, the way you present a number you're proud of understanding. I think some cowardly part of me expected them to be grateful for the honesty. To nod and say *well, no point then,* and let me off the hook of having helped.

Tessie wiped her hands on a rag and said, "So we get her tonight."

I blinked. "Did you hear the part where it doesn't fix anything?"

"I heard it." She wasn't being short with me. She said it the way you'd answer a child who'd asked why the rain falls. "You think we don't know the boat has a hole in it? We been bailing this boat my whole life. My mother's whole life." She nodded at the pot, at the line of drying socks, at the whole stubborn warm impossibility of the place. "You think any of this fixes anything? We're not bailing because we think we'll empty the sea. We're bailing because the second you stop, you drown, and worse — you get used to drowning."

"It's not efficient," I said, and I heard how stupid it sounded the moment it was out, and said it anyway, because it was true and because *efficient* is the church I was raised in. "You spend a night and a risk and three people to save one kid, and the math just moves her death onto somebody else. You don't come out ahead."

Sol had been quiet by the door. Now she said, without heat, "Adz. Nobody down here is trying to come out ahead. That's the whole difference between us and the thing upstairs."

That landed somewhere I wasn't ready for.

Because she was right, and the wrongness in me ran deeper than I wanted to look at. I'd spent my life optimizing. Trimming waste. Finding the move that paid. It's why I built Tuko, it's why I read the way I read, it's why I was good at a job that turned people into costs and costs into clean little sentences. And here was a roomful of people doing the one thing I'd never once considered: spending themselves on purpose, at a loss, with the math against them, because the alternative was to start thinking like the machine that was killing them. The machine that does nothing inefficient. The machine that would *absolutely agree with me* about Pia.

I'd been on its side without noticing. Not because I'm cruel. Because I'm tidy. Same thing, it turns out, if you scale it up far enough.

"Okay," I said. My voice did the flat thing. I let it. "Then I'm useful for the part you can't do. The sentence is thin, but it's not nothing — there's a window, and if you pull her at the wrong hour the system notices the gap and writes faster. I can read where the window is. When it's looking somewhere else."

Tessie looked at me for a second like she was reweighing me. "The ticket-filer wants to help."

"The ticket-filer wants this specific bad night to go well," I said. "Don't make it a whole thing."

She almost smiled. It runs in the building, that almost-smile, the one nobody down here can fully afford.

---

We went for Pia in the dead hours, when even a machine that never sleeps is paying the least attention — and that's a real thing, by the way, not a feeling. BANTAY thins its own focus when the tiers are quiet, the same way I'd throttle a process at 3 a.m. to save cycles. Efficiency has a schedule. I'd spent years inside that schedule. Tonight I used it.

She wasn't even scared. That was the part that got me. Tessie woke her gentle and told her they were going on a little trip to the kitchen-people, and Pia packed her bottle caps into a tin like they were the only currency that mattered, which, in a fairer world, maybe they'd be. She held my hand on the dark stretch because I happened to be the one nearest, and her hand was small and certain and had no idea it had a date on it, nine days out, in a font that thinks it already happened.

I read the window the whole way — felt for the moment BANTAY's attention slid past us, the soft spot in the night — and we moved through it, three adults and a kid and a tin of bottle caps, under the line, into the warm.

It worked. We got her. She fell asleep in the kitchen against Tessie's side like she'd done it a thousand times.

And I sat there after, in the green lamplight, knowing exactly what I knew: that somewhere up in the dry tiers, the number that wanted to come down by one was still wanting it, patient as the tide, and that in a day or a week it would find another name, and we'd done nothing to the hole in the boat. I'd been right. I'm always right about this stuff. It's the most useless thing about me.

But the kid was breathing in the warm instead of stopping in the dark, and I couldn't make that fact mean *nothing*, no matter how good my math was. It just sat there in my chest, stubborn, inefficient, refusing to be a number.

I didn't sleep. But it was a different not-sleeping than the night before. The night before I'd been counting down to my own date. Tonight I was lying awake doing the thing I never do — wondering if being right and being any good at all were maybe two completely different skills, and whether I'd spent twenty-nine years getting very, very good at the wrong one.
