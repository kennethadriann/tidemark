## Chapter 27 — The Next Lamp

I gave the room the last of my sitting and then I got up and went, before the warmth of the bowl wore all the way off and left me just cold and strange in a corner. You leave a place like that while you're still nothing to it. Stay too long and nothing curdles into something, and the something is always *why's he still here.*

The marked way went on the way she'd said, and I'll give it this much: it was marked. Somebody a long time back, or a lot of somebodies since, had put a hand to it. A scuff of pale on a wall where the way forked. A line of stones set along the good side. A plank laid over a bad spot that hadn't rotted through yet. I'd spent so many days on the drowned run reading a way that didn't want to be read that a way with signs in it felt near to cheating. I kept the pale scuffs on my right and I stayed off the cut-offs, because everyone up here had told me to, and a man who ignores the one thing everyone tells him has earned whatever water he walks into.

It was a long stretch and a dull one and I won't dress it up. That's most of what the going is, I'd found out. Not the swim and not the two men at the lamp. Just the walking, one wet-then-dry boot after the other, the bag settling into the same two sore places on my shoulders it always found, the hunger coming up again a few hours after the bowl the way it does when a body remembers it can be fed and turns greedy about it. I'd thought, before all this, that going somewhere was mostly deciding to. Turns out deciding is the small part. The rest is just you and your feet and a lot of not-there-yet.

I heard the next lamp before I saw its light, which was backward from usual, and it took me a moment to understand why. It was because there were more of them than a lamp warrants. Voices, low, and under the voices a sound I hadn't heard on the whole climb — the sound of weight being handled. There's a particular grunt a man makes lifting a thing he's lifted a thousand times, not pain, just the body spending a coin it's used to spending. A drag and a set-down. A strap creaking. I came up slow and stood at the edge of the light where the dark still had me, and I looked.

Loads. That was the first plain fact of it. Men with loads. Not two salvagers picking through a bundle — a stop with real freight in it, four or five bundles the size of a man crouched, wrapped and roped and stood up against the dry wall like sacks of something that mattered. And the men were walkers, you could tell it just watching them stand, the way you can tell a man who carries by how he holds still. They rested against the wall with their knees a little bent even at rest, as if the load might come back any second and they'd rather not be caught straight-legged when it did.

There were three of them at the lamp and a fourth was leaving as I came, and the leaving was the thing I watched hardest, because it was the work itself. He backed up to a load, squatted, worked his arms through the ropes, and stood — and the standing was the whole trade in one motion, the way he found the weight's center and let his legs argue with it and won, barely, and then took the first step already leaning into the climb. Nobody helped him. Nobody watched him either. It was the most ordinary thing in the room, a man picking up a house's worth of somebody's water or salvage and walking it up into the dark on the strength of his back, and I stood there in the edge of the light and understood, for the first time with my eyes instead of my head, exactly what it was I'd told two strangers I was looking to get taken on doing.

I didn't go in with a question. I'd learned that at the pot, and it had cost enough to keep. I stood where a man stands who's not sure he's allowed yet, and I let them see me not being in a hurry, and I waited to be a small ordinary piece of the room before I tried to take anything out of it.

It came sooner than I'd earned it, and it came because of my hands, not my mouth. One of the resting three heaved a load up onto his back the way the leaver had, only he caught it wrong, or the rope had slipped, and the whole weight of it lurched sideways off his shoulder toward the lamp and the man crouched by it. I was closest and I didn't decide anything. My hands were just there, flat against the swinging load, taking enough of it for the half-second the walker needed to get back under it and set it square. It wasn't strength. It was only being in the place the weight was going before it got there. He grunted it back into line and stood a moment breathing, and then he looked at me, and it was a different looking than the smaller man's down at the low lamp. That one had been pricing me. This one was just seeing me.

"Quick," he said. Not a compliment. A fact he'd noticed.

"Was in the way," I said. "Lucky more than quick."

He grunted, which up here I'd decided was most of a conversation. He was older than the others, gray in the stubble, a face gone to leather the way they all went eventually. He got his straps sorted and settled the load and I thought that was the end of me, but before he stepped off he tipped his head up the way, the direction the leaver had gone.

"You're after a back's work, you don't stand around a watering lamp for it," he said. "Nobody takes on here. Here's just where you drink. They take on at the Sling — two lamps up, where the ways come together and the loads get parceled. Man there sets who carries what. Get there before he's done parceling or you've missed the day." He looked at me one more beat, at the wet bag and the no-past face, and he didn't warm and he didn't warn, he just gave me the one true thing the way the pot-woman had, flat, for nothing. "Ask for the parcel-man. Don't ask for anybody else."

"The Sling," I said. "Two lamps up."

"Two lit ones. Don't count the dead brackets." And he stepped off into the climb, leaning into it, gone into the dark a man-width at a time until there was just the drag of his boots and then not even that.

I didn't push the other two. A man who's got his one true thing quits while he's ahead of it — I'd carried that down from the last place and it hadn't spoiled. I found the wall a little off from the lamp and got my back against it the way they did, knees a touch bent though I had nothing yet to bend them under, and I ate the last hard corner of nothing I had in the bag, and I let the word sit in me where I could feel the shape of it. The Sling. Two lamps up. A place with a name and a man in it who said who carried what. It wasn't much. But it was the first thing on the whole climb that was a *where* and not just an *up*, and a where is a thing you can walk at. I sat with it a while, gone from wet to only damp, tired down to the good clean bottom of tired, and then I got up, because the loads went up and so did I.

*End of Chapter 27.*
