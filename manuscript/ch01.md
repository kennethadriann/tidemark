## Chapter 1 — The Wrong Tense

The first thing I check every morning isn't a clock. Clocks are for people who get to decide what they do with their day. The first thing I check is how much water I get.

It's on my wrist. Under the skin, glowing a little, like the world tattooed a number on me while I slept, which, more or less, it did. This morning it says 0.61 liters. I'll take it. I've woken up to worse. The most it ever got was the week my mom died — turns out grief makes you quiet, and quiet makes you cheap to keep around, and BANTAY likes cheap. My number went up for a week. Then I felt bad about feeling fine, and it went back down. That's the whole system, honestly. It can see you feel guilty. It charges you for it.

I stayed in bed a while. You can hear the building breathing if you listen for it — the pumps way down at the bottom, sucking the sea back out of the basement, every hour, all day, my whole life. There used to be a city under all that water. My grandmother still says "Makati" like it's somewhere you can go. It's not. It's just down there. We built a tower on top of a drowned city, put our addresses on it, and decided that was normal.

I pressed my thumb to the wall and the panel woke up. GOOD MORNING, READER ADLAWAN. Under that, the only line that matters. My tidemark. 612. Three points above the cutoff — the point where the elevators stop picking you up and you start the long walk to wherever they let you be.

Three points. That's a careful man's whole life right there.

And I'm careful. I'm so careful I made a job out of it.

---

The Lexicon sits in the guts of the building. Tier nine, close enough to the water that on bad days you can hear it pushing against the seals outside, slow and patient, like it's got all the time in the world and we don't. Sixty of us. Sixty desks. Air so clean it tastes like nothing. I sat down and let the work cover me up. It's the one part of the day I actually like.

Here's what I do. BANTAY doesn't think in words. It thinks in something worse — this big tangled mess of maybes that nobody can look at straight without going a little soft in the head. So they use us. Readers. We take what it predicts and turn it into something a person can act on. Tier 30's going to riot over rations in nine days. The east pumps die before the rains come. This guy's turning into a cost. That kind of thing.

I'm good at it. Not bragging. Okay, a little bragging. I'm good because I don't just read the answer — I can feel how it got there. The way it reaches for a conclusion. I trust that the way other people trust the floor under their feet.

Here's the other thing about me, the part the bosses wouldn't love. I'm lazy. Reading predictions all day is boring, and I hate boring more than almost anything. So a while back I built myself a little helper. Nothing fancy — a script that reads the boring ones for me and only bugs me when something looks off, so I can sit at my desk looking busy while it does the actual work. I call it Tuko, after the gecko, because it lives in the walls of the system where it's got no business being, and it makes noise when it sees something. If anyone ever found Tuko, three points would be the least of my problems. They don't fine you for running your own automation inside BANTAY. They just stop your number.

All of which is to say I had no reason to expect anything special that morning. And then, at 8:14, I forgot how to breathe.

The prediction was about a guy named Teodoro Lim. Tier 22. Fixes pumps. BANTAY flagged him as trouble coming. Happens all the time — it flags dozens a day, the exact kind of boring I built Tuko to swallow for me. But Tuko didn't file this one away with the rest. It poked me. *This one's weird, boss.* So I dug in to see why it was so sure about him.

And the reason was up ahead. In the future.

I read it again, because I don't get this stuff wrong. To prove Lim was going to be a problem, BANTAY pointed at something that hadn't happened yet — Lim getting pulled off his repair crew. Scheduled. Four days from now.

So it wasn't guessing he'd become a problem. It was using a punishment that hadn't happened yet as the proof for the prediction that would cause it.

The tense was wrong. It was talking about the future like it had already happened. Like somebody had already written it down.

I sat back. A prediction isn't supposed to have a tense. Weather doesn't have a tense. This had one. So this wasn't a forecast. Somebody was writing it.

And my hands were dead still on the desk. That's the part that got me. My hands are fast — I win the reflex game upstairs, I catch things before I even know I've dropped them. When they go quiet like that, it means the careful part of me already worked out the thing the rest of me didn't want to know yet.

Because if BANTAY isn't reading the future — if it's writing it — then the number on everybody's wrist isn't a measurement.

It's a sentence. Like a court sentence. Guilty, this many points.

---

So I did what I always do when everything falls apart. I got ready.

I pulled Lim's whole file. Ran it three ways. Checked it against the pump stuff, then six other things I had no business touching, building my neat little case, careful, with one extra check on top that I didn't even need — because the day you're wrong is the day they delete your number, and you with it.

Diligence, I told myself. Same lie I always tell. It's a good lie. It's got a calm face. Underneath it my heart was going like crazy. One more pass, I kept thinking. One more pass and then I'll do something about it.

I was on pass number four when the message showed up. No name on it. No hello. Just that flat font BANTAY uses for dead people.

READER ADLAWAN. YOU READ A SENTENCE THAT WASN'T MEANT FOR READING.

And in the corner of my eye, my number dropped a point. Then another.

611. 610.

Slow. Almost polite about it. Like the tide coming in.

Down below, the pumps shuddered. And for the first time in my careful life, I finally got it. I was out of passes. There was one move left — the one I'd spent twenty-nine years coming up with reasons not to make.

I stood up.

---

*End of Chapter 1.*
