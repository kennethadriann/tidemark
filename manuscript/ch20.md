## Chapter 20 — Something for the Bag

I didn't decide anything in the night, which I'd like on the record, and then in the morning I caught my hands deciding without me.

It started small, the way it always does with me. I was by the archive corner where the dry stuff gets kept, and there was a sack there, an empty one, a good one, the kind with the weave still tight enough to hold water bottles without sweating them out the bottom. I don't know that I went to it on purpose. A man's hands have their own errands. I picked it up and shook the grit out of it and folded the mouth back the way you do when you mean to put things in a bag, and I'd gotten two bottles standing in it before the front part of my head caught up to the back part and asked what we thought we were doing.

Because I knew what I was doing. I'd done it before. I did it in the kitchen weeks back when the *out* rumor had me, water for three, the lazy man moving quick because quick is the tell that the slow man's already half-decided. Same hands. Same quiet competence. The only thing different was the direction. Last time I was packing a bag to go *out*, away, light and gone. This time the bottles were standing up in a sack pointed at the stairs, and the stairs go *up*, and I noticed I'd already, without a meeting about it, started counting the water for one and not for three.

I didn't stop. That's the part I want to be honest about. A wiser man might have set the sack down and gone and sat by the pot and not handled the question with his hands until his head had voted. I'm not that. I kept going, slow, the way you keep eating a thing you know you shouldn't because stopping would mean admitting you'd started. I worked out the water. Not too much — too much is its own flag, a man hauling a week of water up a dry line is a man with a reason, and reasons get noticed. Enough for the climb and a margin, no more. I found a dry layer, a long-sleeved thing somebody'd salvaged and nobody'd claimed, because up where it's dry the cold sits differently and a wet man stands out. I rolled it tight. I thought about food and decided against carrying much, because the kitchen had taught me that a man who looks fed and unbothered draws less than a man provisioned for a journey.

And then I got to the wrist, and the wrist is where the prep stopped being a chore and turned into the thing itself.

Down here my wrist is dark and nobody looks. Off the grid the count loses its grip and a dark wrist is just a dark wrist, one more among the uncounted. But the road up runs back into the thick of the signal, and up there a dark wrist is not nothing. Up there everybody's wrist has a number breathing on it, and the one wrist in the room that doesn't is the loud one. The whole skill of going up marked is the skill of being a man with no number in a place where not having a number is the most interesting thing about you. I sat with that. I worked it like a problem, because it is one — a sleeve pulled long, a way of holding your arm that doesn't look like hiding, the difference between a man covering his wrist and a man who simply has cold hands. I'm good at that kind of problem. I've spent my life being the least interesting thing in every room I'm in. If there was ever a job cut to my shape, it was *go up there and have nobody see you.*

I had it most of the way solved before I felt the floor go out from under the having.

Because here is the thing about me and prep, and I've half-known it for years without letting it finish the sentence. Getting ready is how I never go. The readiness is the destination. I prepare a thing so thoroughly, so lovingly, that the preparing becomes the whole of the doing, and the actual step at the end of it sits there untaken, and that's fine, that's the design, because I was never going to take it — I was only ever going to get so beautifully ready that not taking it felt like rest instead of a dodge. It's the appointed hallway. I said it to myself plainer than I'd ever said it: every other time in my life, the prep was a hallway with no door at the end, a long pleasant corridor I could walk up and down forever, busy and going nowhere. You can live a whole life in a hallway if you keep it well-stocked.

I looked at the sack. Two bottles standing, the dry layer rolled, the wrist problem nearly cracked. And I understood that this hallway had a door at the end of it. There was nothing after the last item. Usually there's always one more thing to ready, one more leg to kick, and I can always find it, I'm gifted at finding it. I went looking for it now, out of habit, the way your tongue goes to a sore tooth — what else, what's not done, what needs another think. And there wasn't much. A bag of water, a dry shirt, a sleeve pulled long. The list was nearly out of items, and behind the last item there wasn't another hallway. There was the stair, going up, wet at the bottom and dry at the top, with a man on the far end of it walking toward his own date and no idea, and me, the one body that couldn't be sent.

I set the bottles down. Not put them back — just down, standing where they were, in the sack, the sack still open. I didn't empty it and I didn't shoulder it. I left it sitting there with the mouth folded back, ready, the way you leave a thing when you can't yet make your arms do either of the two things left to do with it.

Tessie came by for the spoon and saw the sack and didn't say anything about it, which is the loudest Tessie ever gets. She just looked at it, and looked at me, and put the spoon back in my hand like the pot still needed stirring whether or not a man had a bag packed. I stirred it. The lugaw doesn't care. And the sack sat there by the wall the whole morning, two bottles up in it, waiting to be picked up or put away, and I found I couldn't do the one and wouldn't do the other, and that, it turns out, is a place you can stand.

*End of Chapter 20.*
