## Chapter 13 — Where It Stops Reaching

I didn't decide to go out. I decided to go look, which is a different thing, and the difference was the only way I could stand to do it.

A man stuck between two doors can still walk the hallway. I'd been a Reader my whole life, and being a Reader mostly means I don't trust the shape of a thing until I've seen it, and "out" was still only Maning's word for it and a bent sign I couldn't read. The hard door — the one Sol named, the one I'd named myself — I didn't even know where its handle was. But the edge Maning kept going on about, the place where the counting stopped, that I could go put my hand on. Not to leave through it. Just to learn it was real. It was the smallest possible way of moving, and small was all I had in me.

I asked Sol, because Sol had roads.

"Out," she said. She was lacing a boot that had been somebody else's boot.

"Not out for good," I said. "Far enough to see the edge. You told me I only ever read one row at a time. I want to read the row where it stops keeping the book."

She tied the boot off and looked at me a while. Whatever she was checking for, she seemed to find it. "Out to leave is one trip," she said. "Out to look is a worse one, because you have to come back after and still decide." But she stood up. "Stay behind me. Don't talk to the water."

I won't tell the whole way. It was long and wet and dull in the way that real danger usually turns out to be — mostly waiting, a little swimming, a lot of holding still while Sol listened to things I couldn't hear. We went up before we went out, which surprised me. I'd had it in my head that out was down, that everything worth reaching was down. But the edge isn't a depth. It's a distance. You climb out of the drowned tiers into half-light and then you just go, past the last towers that still had their lamps lit, past the ones that didn't, until the buildings stopped being buildings and started being teeth.

Sol kept her wrist. I'd wondered about that. She came and went from the count, lived half on the grid and half off it the way I never could anymore, and hers still worked. And somewhere past the dead towers I watched it do the thing Maning had described, and it was worse to see than it had been to be told.

It didn't go dark. Dark is what mine did, under the line — a clean cut, a number switched off and gone. Hers just got slow. A message came in halfway and sat there halfway. The little count in the corner ticked, hung, ticked, hung, like a man trying to remember a name he used to know. Then it stopped trying. No alarm, no goodbye. The wrist just quit reaching for a thing that had quit reaching back.

And I felt it go. That's the part I can't say right to a person who's never been counted. I had no number left to lose. But I'd spent every day of my life under the thing's attention the way you spend every day under weather, and I never once knew the weather was pressing on me until it lifted. Somewhere out past the teeth, between one wet step and the next, a pressure came off the back of my neck that I hadn't known was sitting there. The air didn't change. Nothing visible changed. I just stopped being looked at, and my body knew it a full second before my head did, and I had to put my hand on a wall that wasn't a wall anymore to keep from sitting down in the water.

"There it is," Sol said, watching me find it. "Everybody does that. First time the eyes come off you, you feel how heavy they'd been."

The place we came to wasn't much. I should be honest about that, because I'd built something up in my head on the way — I don't know what, a free city, a green thing, a reward for the swim — and it was a clutch of huts pegged to a reef of old roof, nets drying, a smell of fish and rust. A few people who looked at us and checked nothing but our hands and our faces. Poor isn't a strong enough word for it. They had less than the kitchen, and the kitchen had nothing. But not one of them flinched when a wrist might've buzzed, because no wrist buzzed out here. They'd gone and gotten old in the gap, exactly like Maning said. Too small and too dull to file.

One of them gave us water without being asked, which up the line would have cost somebody a sentence. An old woman, brown and folded small, with a voice like a door hinge. She talked the way old people do when visitors come rare, all of it at once and none of it in order, and I let it wash over me, because I'm not good at talking and I'm very good at being talked at.

She told me her mother had walked these streets when they were dry.

I almost let it go past. Then the arithmetic in me, which never sleeps, sat up in its chair. I looked at her — old, sure, but not ancient, a woman with a working back and most of her own teeth — and I thought, *her mother.* Not her grandmother's grandmother. Not a thing worn smooth coming down a hundred years of mouths. Her mother. One person standing between this folded little woman and dry streets.

I do numbers the way other people do breathing, and these numbers would not sit down. A hundred years drowned, they tell us. A hundred years up the line is gospel, it's the first thing you learn. But a hundred years is four lives end to end, maybe five. It is so long that nobody walking around today should have a mother who saw the ground. It should be smooth myth by now, polished from handling. She didn't say it like myth, though. She said it the way you say a thing your own mother told you, with a half-remembered season attached and a complaint about the price of rice stuck to the side of it, the way true memories always come with something stupid riding along.

I didn't argue with her. You don't argue with an old woman about her mother; Maning had taught me at least that much. I filed it the way I'd filed the bent sign — off to one side, unconfirmed, a thin person's soft story from a place where the signal frays and the truth goes loose.

But it wouldn't file. That was the new thing, and it followed me the whole long wet way back down. Up the line I could always shrink a wrong number until it fit somewhere I didn't have to look at it. This one I carried home in my chest, in the spot where the weather used to press, and no matter how I turned it over it would not get small.
