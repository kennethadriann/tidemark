## Chapter 16 — He Carried It Down Himself

Maning came down for the list the next morning, and I got to watch the owing leave the room on a man's back.

That's what the list was, when you got down to it. Tessie had taken my count of eleven-and-a-heel and done her arithmetic behind her eyes and come out the other side with a thing to do — water this one less, ask up the line for that, trade what we could spare for what we couldn't make. She'd scratched it onto a piece of slate, and Maning folded the slate into his shirt without reading it, the way you take a thing from somebody you've already decided to trust. That was the water on its way to becoming more water. The owing, walking. He was going up, because people braver than me went up so the rest of us could keep counting jugs in the dark.

He'd come down heavy, the way he always did, with junk roped to his back. Slates mostly, the salvaged kind, pulled out of the wet rooms up the line where the score keeps its drafts. He traded them to us for almost nothing. To him they were weight he'd rather not carry; to us they were the wall. Tessie took them off his shoulders and stacked them by the archive and gave him his lugaw, and he drank it standing, and told a joke I'd heard before about a fish and a Director, and then he hitched himself back together and went up. We didn't make a thing of it. You don't, or you'd never let anybody leave.

I read the new slates that evening. That was mine to do now. Nobody made me — Sol read them too, and Lim when his hands were steady — but I'd put it on my own list, under *owe,* right below the water. Somebody had to read the wall like a Reader and not just a mourner, so the next name came up known as a person and not a number. It turned out I was the one in the room who could do that without it costing me the way it cost them. A cold skill, but mine. I'd decided that cold and useful beat warm and no help, which is the closest thing to a creed I've got.

So I sat by the lamp and read them the way I read everything, not one at a time but all at once, sorted, the whole table laid flat in my head. And I felt the floor tilt the way it had in the water passage, the same direction, a hair further over than the last time I'd stood on it. More names than there'd been. Same dates marching the same way. Still that column that only ever subtracts, and still nowhere on the slate a column that adds. I want to be honest that I went looking for one. I always do now. A correction, a name lifted off, a single number that went up instead of down. There wasn't one. There's never one. I sat in the tilt and couldn't say it any plainer than I could the week before, and didn't try, because the trying is a basement and I'd climbed out of one of those already.

Then a slate near the bottom of the stack, fresh-cut, the scratch still bright, and a name on it I knew.

Maning.

Dated out, past tense, the way they all are. A thing that had already happened to a man it hadn't happened to yet. I read it three times to be sure I wasn't tired and inventing it, and I wasn't. He'd pulled it out of a wet room up the line and roped it to his back with the rest of the morning's junk and carried it down the stairs to us, and never once felt the weight of that one over the others. He'd handed me his own sentence across the kitchen and taken his lugaw and made his fish joke and climbed back up into it. He couldn't feel it. Of course he couldn't. You can't feel a thing like that from inside any more than Lim can feel the gap in his song. The slate was just slate to him, cold and heavy and worth a cup of rice.

I sat there with it and waited for my shelf to take it. I've got a good shelf, you'll remember. Set the wrong number on it, label it later, sleep fine. I put Maning up there and reached for the spoon and the rest of the evening, and the shelf wouldn't hold him. He slid right back down into my lap.

It took me a minute to understand why, and then I did, and I didn't much like it. He wouldn't stay on the shelf because he was already on the other list. The one I'd made in the cold passage, the small one with a floor. *What do I owe, and to who.* Water was on it, and the water came down on Maning's back, which meant Maning was on it, a little ways under Pia, a little ways over the heel of the twelfth jug. And the thing about a list of people you owe — the thing I'd half-seen standing there with the count still in my mouth and hadn't wanted to finish seeing — is that it is also a list of things you might have to do.

To owe a man is to have a thing you might have to do about him. And the thing was already standing up in me before I'd decided to let it, and it was the one thing I don't do.

I'd have to go up after him. Or send word up, which for a man like me is the same as going, because I've never in my life trusted a message I didn't carry with my own hands. Up, where the signal's thick and the wrist works and the weather sits on your neck. Up, before I was ready, which is to say up at all, because ready and I have never once been in a room together. My whole life is a hallway of getting ready so I'd never have to walk through the door at the end of it. Prepare, and prepare, and prepare, and call it being careful, and never act. And here was the owing, polite as you like, asking for exactly the door.

I didn't get up. I want to be straight about that, because this isn't the part where the lazy man finds his spine. I sat by the lamp with Maning's slate in my two hands and did nothing at all, the same nothing I'm good at, and felt the new shape of it. The owing had been such a comfort a day ago. A low floor, but it held. Nobody tells you a floor you can stand on is also a floor that can ask you to cross it.

Pia turned over and didn't say a color this time. Somewhere up the stairs Maning was walking through warm dark with my count in his shirt and his own date on his back, telling the next person about the fish. The draft I'd felt at the foot of the list was still there, cold under the same door, *down, not out,* the one I wasn't ready to look at. But there were two doors now. The far one I could keep not-looking-at a while longer. The near one had a man's name on it, dated out, and a clock I could count.

I set the slate down where Tessie would see it in the morning, and I didn't sleep much, and that's all I did. Some nights the owing only asks. It doesn't mean it won't be back to collect.

*End of Chapter 16.*
