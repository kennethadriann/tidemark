## Chapter 24 — Where People Had Been

The line stopped being nobody's somewhere around the middle of what I'd have called a morning if there'd been any way to tell.

I didn't come on it the way you'd come on a door. There was no door. It was more that the passage, which had been the kind of raw that only water and time make — floor that gave up being floor, a ceiling that dripped where it felt like it — started, a little at a time, to show a hand in it. The first thing was a step. Just one, cut into a place where the slope steepened, a flat notch chipped out of the old skin of the floor so a boot had somewhere to sit. Then, higher, another. Somebody had come up here enough times to get sick of the slope and had spent an afternoon making it kinder to the next man, or kinder to themselves, and I stood on the first cut step with my wet boot fitting the hollow of it and felt, stupidly, looked after by a stranger who was long gone.

You read a thing like that without meaning to. That's the trouble with the eye I've got. A cut step isn't a step to me, it's a claim — somebody comes here, comes often, comes with the notion of coming again. And where one man comes often, others come at all. I was climbing up out of the part of the world that only water visited and into the part that people used, and the used part is the counted part, and I knew it in my feet before I'd have said it in my head, the same as always.

After the steps there was a rope. A handline, gray and furred with damp, run along the worst stretch and made off at both ends round knobs of old iron somebody had reckoned would hold. I didn't trust my weight to it — you don't trust a stranger's rope with your life, only with your balance — but I put a hand to it going up and it took the hand kindly enough, and I thought about the man who'd hung it, hauling a coil of line down into the dark on the chance it'd save him a fall someday. That's a lot of trouble for a maybe. Salvagers, I was learning, are a people who spend real work on maybes. Maning's kind. You'd have to be, doing what they do, walking loads down out of the light into the drowned end and back, on the chance the drowned end has something worth the walk.

Then the smell changed. For a long time it had been all one smell, the smell of water that's been shut under the world too long, rust and cold and something green gone wrong. Now, faint, threaded through it, there was smoke. Not a fire — nothing so near as a fire — just the memory of one, the way a room holds it, old smoke gone into the walls. Somewhere up ahead of me, not close, people had burned something to cook or to keep warm, and had done it enough times and long enough that the passage itself had taken the smell on. I stopped and breathed it and my stomach turned over, not from fear, from the plain animal fact that smoke means people and people means food and I'd been eating cold out of a bag for a day and a night.

I didn't hurry, though. Everything in me that wasn't a stomach knew better than to hurry toward smoke. The weather on my neck had been thickening the whole climb, a pressure going from a draft under a door to a hand laid flat on the back of my head, and I understood without being told that up past the smoke was where the eyes started. Not a wall you cross. There's never a wall. It's a getting-thicker. The count doesn't begin anywhere you could point to; it just gets denser the higher you go, the way a crowd does, and one moment you're a man alone on a stair and the next you're a man among men and can't say where the change happened.

So I did the thing I'd drawn up for myself. I stopped, there in a bend where nobody was, and I made myself ready before I needed to be, because being ready in front of the people who'd read you is too late. I worked the sleeve down over the dead wrist one more time and got it to lie flatter now the cloth had dried toward damp. I shook the arm out, walked it loose at my side, made it an arm that had never once had a reason to hide. On paper, weeks ago, in the dry, I'd have felt clever doing it. Here it just felt like work, one more thing carried instead of packed, and I was getting used to that — the whole trip had been a slow schooling in the difference. I said the plan over in my head anyway, not because I'd forgotten it but the way you say a thing to keep your hands from shaking: wear it ordinary, keep the arm moving, don't be the one still limb in a room full of chattering ones. Then I stopped saying it, because a man muttering to himself in a bend is his own kind of snag.

I went on up, easier now, into ground that had been kept. There were marks everywhere once I had the eye for them. A place where the wall had been knocked back to widen the way for a load. A bracket, old, that had held a light once and held nothing now. Down in a low spot, a boot — one boot, split at the toe, left because it wasn't worth carrying and wasn't worth burning, the most human thing I'd seen since the kitchen. I passed it and didn't touch it. It's a strange comfort, other people's leavings. It says the way goes somewhere. Nobody drops a boot at the bottom of a hole that ends in a wall.

And then, far up, where the passage bent again and the thin grey I'd been climbing toward finally had a source, I heard it. Not words. Too far and too muffled for words. Just the shape of a sound that people make and water doesn't and machines don't — a knock, a drag, a voice pitched low and then another under it, the ordinary racket of somebody up ahead doing something with their hands and not caring who heard, because down here they were the ones who belonged and I was the one who didn't. I stood still and listened to it a while, the first human noise not my own in a day, and I didn't go toward it yet and I didn't go away from it. I just stood in the bend with the smoke in my nose and the weather on my neck and let it be true that there were people up there, and that I was going to have to walk in among them soon, wet and dark-wristed and short a good reason, and that this was the errand and there was no running it from the shadows.

I fixed my sleeve one more time out of nerves, caught myself doing it, and left it alone. Then I went up toward the noise, slow, on my feet, in the open.

*End of Chapter 24.*
