## Chapter 15 — Something to Stand On

The doubt didn't go anywhere. I'd half expected it to. Big thoughts come at night and look smaller by breakfast; that's a rule you can mostly trust. Not this one. I woke with it sitting on my chest exactly where I'd left it, the same weight, in the spot where the weather used to press.

So I had a question I couldn't answer. Let me tell you what a man actually does with one of those, because it isn't brave and it isn't interesting. He does nothing. For about a day and a half I was no use to anybody. I stirred when somebody put a spoon in my hand. I sorted Pia's caps when she lost interest in them, which was always. And the whole time I was somewhere else, down in my own basement with a flashlight, running my hand along the floor looking for the seam. Was careful mine. Was any of me mine, or had it been set there by something with its own reasons. You can't get to the bottom of that. There isn't a bottom. I'd reach for the edge of the smooth place to pry it up and have a look under, and my fingers would go straight through it, and I'd be standing in the same spot with nothing in my hand, so I'd start over and go through it again.

Tessie watched me do this for a day and a half and then she'd had enough of it. She didn't ask what was wrong. She'd raised kids and buried a couple and she knew that a man staring at the middle of the air is not a puzzle to be solved, he's just furniture that's in the way.

"You," she said. "Caps are done. Go count the jugs and tell me what we're short."

I almost said I was busy. Then I heard how that would sound — busy doing what — and went and counted the jugs.

It's the kind of job they give me because it's the kind of job I can't ruin. Down a side passage off the kitchen there's a stack of water, scrounged and bartered and pulled up out of the dark by people braver than me, and somebody has to know how much of it there is. I counted. I counted again, because counting twice is the one religious practice I have. Eleven jugs and the heel of a twelfth.

I knew what eleven meant before I'd finished thinking it, the way you know a step isn't there in the dark. Eleven was three down from the last time I'd counted. And there was no week coming that would put those three back. That's the part a Reader can't unsee once he's seen it. I'd read the wall like this — the whole table at once, not one row — and found a column that only ever subtracts, and no column anywhere that adds. The jugs were the wall, just smaller and wetter. Things left. Nothing came in to meet them. The heel of the twelfth would be the heel of the eleventh next week, and so on down, and you didn't need to be cruel to see where a line like that was headed. You just had to follow it with your finger.

I want to be careful here, because back at the wall I'd felt this same thing and had no word for it, and I still didn't have one. It isn't that somebody was taking the water. Water's just hard to come by; jugs run down; that's the world. But up the line it had the exact same shape, and up there it wasn't water, it was people, and people don't just run down. Somebody's meant to fill the jug back up when it's people. And nobody was, anywhere I could see, and the not-filling had stopped feeling like a thing that was missing and started feeling like a direction the whole floor was tilted. I stood in the cold passage and felt the tilt under my feet and couldn't say it any plainer than that.

Here's the thing that turned me, though. Eleven is a bad answer. But it's an answer. It's a number I could carry back to Tessie and set down in front of her, and she could do something with it — water somebody less, trade for more, send Maning up with a list. I'd spent a day and a half with both hands in a question that had no floor, and here was one with a floor, even if the floor was low and getting lower.

And that's when it went over in me, quiet, the way the big ones do. No thunderclap. I just noticed I'd been asking the wrong question this whole time. Not wrong like false. Wrong like useless. Was careful ever mine, what was done to me, what is the thing up the line that maybe did it — I could chew on those until my teeth fell out and they would never once put water in a jug. There was no spoon at the end of them. They were a basement with no bottom, and I'd been almost happy down there, if I'm honest, because down there nobody could ask anything of me.

The smaller question had a floor, and it pointed the other way. Not what was done to me. What do I owe, and to who. That one I could count, same as jugs. Pia, asleep through all of it, three short on the water and drinking anyway, alive because one night a roomful of people decided to bail a boat that couldn't be emptied. Lim, humming over the hole where his Malou used to be. Tessie, who'd put her own in the ground and still got up to make lugaw for strangers. I owed the thing up the line nothing, and I'd never wring an answer out of it anyway. I owed this passage and this kitchen something, and the something was a list I could actually read.

It didn't fix me. I want to be clear that nothing got fixed. The hole in me was still a hole and I still couldn't feel the edge of it, and that's exactly why I had to set it down. You can't build on a floor you don't trust, so you go and find a floor you can. The owing was a floor. It was low, like the water, but it held my weight.

I carried the count back to Tessie. Eleven and a heel. She took it the way she takes everything, like she'd already known the number and just wanted it said out loud, and she started shifting jugs around behind her eyes, and I watched her do it and felt something I hadn't felt since the first morning with my number falling. Useful. Small and useful, which it turns out are not the same as nothing.

That would have been a good place to stop. But owing isn't free, and I knew it even standing there with the count still in my mouth. A list of people you owe is also a list of things you might one day have to do, and down at the bottom of mine, under Pia and Lim and the water, was a thing I wasn't ready to look at. A direction. Down, not out. I didn't take it out and turn it over. I just felt it sitting there at the foot of the list, cold, the way you feel a draft come under a door and know without getting up exactly which door it's coming from.

*End of Chapter 15.*
