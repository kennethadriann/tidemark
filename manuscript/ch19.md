## Chapter 19 — Send Word

I slept on the route the way you sleep on a sum that won't come out even, and woke up with the answer my mind always reaches for first, which is the answer where somebody else does the part with the legs.

It came to me clean and early, the way the good dodges always do. I didn't have to go up. That was the whole shape of it, laid out neat before I'd even sat up. The thing that needed doing was getting a fact to a man — *come down, there's a reason* — and a fact is the most portable thing there is. You don't carry a fact on your own back. People went up the line every day, and not a few of them. Salvagers, runners, the women who took the dry-room trade up and brought the wet-room trade down. Maning himself was a man who made his living carrying things one way and then the other. There was a whole road of backs already going where I needed a thing to go. You don't shoulder the load yourself when the road's full of people who shoulder loads for a cup and a kind word. You hand it off. You let the line carry it, same as I'd spent my whole life letting some system or other carry every weight I couldn't be bothered to hold myself.

So I lay there and built it, and I built it well, because the building is the part I love. I picked the carrier — somebody who went up regular, somebody trusted both ends. I worked out what they'd say and how they'd say it so it didn't sound like a summons, just a fellow passing on word. I even worked out the cup of lugaw Tessie would send up with them, because a message with a cup behind it lands softer than a message alone. I lay in the dark and furnished the whole thing, room by room, the way I do, and it was a good plan, and I was halfway to easy in my chest before the engineer in me did the thing the engineer always does last, which is walk the plan and kick every leg of it to see which one folds.

The first leg folded right away. What do you tell the carrier to put in his mouth? Not the true thing. You don't hand a man's date to a stranger and send it up a line where the signal's thick — we'd learned that with Pia, that you don't tell people the wall has their name, you just go and bail and let them keep not knowing. So the message had to be vague. *Come down. There's a reason.* And there went the second leg, because a vague word doesn't move Maning. The man had good water and a load he wanted and his own slow stubborn weather. He'd hear *come down* from somebody he half-knew and he'd say *why,* and the carrier wouldn't have a why, and a why-less call relayed by a fellow you only sort of trust doesn't pull a stubborn man down out of a dry room. It just makes him shrug and keep digging. To carry any weight at all the word would have to come from someone he trusted, with a reason that held, and the only reason that held was the one you couldn't send.

And under all of it, the last leg, the one I didn't want to look at: sending word up meant putting words into the thick of the signal, up where it reads everything clean. You don't light a flare and walk it toward the very thing you're hiding a marked man from, just to wave him down out of its sight. The whole point of going to get him was that he'd gone somewhere the count could see him fine. You don't fix that by mailing a letter into the same room.

So I walked the plan, and every leg of it folded, and they all folded the same direction, toward the same little patch of floor. The only thing that moves Maning is somebody he trusts standing in the dry in front of him, looking him in the face, saying come down and meaning it hard enough that the man feels the meaning. That can't be handed off. The words can travel. The going can't. The part that does the work isn't the message. It's the body that carried it.

I lay there a while longer with that, and it did the thing to me that I have to put down carefully, because it's small and it's the whole chapter and it isn't anything you could watch happen.

My whole life runs on one quiet article of faith, which is that if a thing can be sent, then I don't have to go, and if I don't have to go, then not going is free. It costs nothing. It's just the not-doing of an errand, and you can't be charged for an errand you never took up. That's the corner I've lived in. That's the corner the lazy man keeps swept and warm. And the morning took it from me, plain, without a fuss. The thing couldn't be sent. And the second it couldn't be sent, not-going stopped being free. Not-going turned into a thing I'd be *doing* — choosing to sit on my good solid floor and let the days run themselves down to the date with my hands around a cup. There was a price on staying put now, and when I checked it against the price of going, the two came out the same. I'd never once in my life stood in a place where doing nothing weighed exactly what doing something weighed. There'd always been a cheaper door. There wasn't a cheaper door.

I told Sol the send-word plan anyway, later, by the pot, half because I wanted her to find the leg I'd missed and tell me it could work after all. She didn't insult it. She just walked it, quiet, the same road I'd walked, and got to the same wall, and stood at it with me a second. Then she said the part I'd been steering around.

"You could send word," she said. "Somebody'd carry it. But you wouldn't believe it got there. You'd sit down here counting jugs and not trust a single thing went up that line right. I know you a little now." She turned her cup. "The only way you'd ever trust it's done is you carry it yourself. That's not me telling you to go. That's just the shape of you."

That was it exactly, and it was my own words from weeks back turned around and handed back gentle — that a list of people you owe is a list of things you might have to do — except now it had my name on the doing.

I didn't go. I want that clear. I didn't decide a thing. I just sat there with the last cheap door shut and the cup warm in my hands, and behind me, faint as ever, the far draft, *down, not out,* that I still wouldn't turn to look at. One door. The near one had lost its handle on the easy side. Some mornings you wake up with a clever way out of a thing, and you spend the whole morning watching it not hold, and what's left when it's gone isn't a decision. It's just you, and the thing, and no one between you to send.

*End of Chapter 19.*
