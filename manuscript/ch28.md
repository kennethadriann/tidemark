## Chapter 28 — The Parcel-Man

Two lamps up, the gray walker had said, and don't count the dead brackets. It was good he'd said it. There were more dead brackets than live lamps on that stretch — whole runs of iron arms still bolted to the wall with nothing in them, nothing under them but the pale ghost where a light used to hang. A man counting every bracket would have called himself at the Sling three times over and been wrong all three. I counted the ones that were burning and let the rest alone, and on the second one that burned, the way stopped being a way.

It opened out into a wide low room with too many doors. That was the first thing I understood about it — that it wasn't the end of a passage, it was a place where passages met. I could see three ways in besides the one I'd come by, dark mouths in the dark wall, and every so often a man came out of one of them under a load and set it down in the middle with the others, or came out of one empty and stood a while getting his breath back into him. The loads pooled in the middle. Bundles and sacks and roped-up shapes, more than I'd seen anywhere, sorted into rough heaps that meant nothing to me yet. The place breathed loads in from the low ways and out up the high ones, and in the middle of the breathing there was one man it all turned around.

I didn't have to ask which one he was. You could read it off the room. He was the still point everyone else pointed at without meaning to — a walker would come in and his eyes would go to this man before they went anywhere else, the way your tongue goes to the sore tooth. He was squat and unhurried and he had a slate across his knee, a real one, and a nub of something he wrote with, and he sat on a crate at the edge of the heaps and did the one thing the whole place was for, which was decide. A bundle would come and he'd look at it and look at the man who'd want it and say a short thing, and the man would shoulder that bundle and not another, and go. He never touched a load himself. He just said whose back each one rode.

I stood off in the dark of my doorway and watched him do it a while, because watching was free and I wasn't ready to spend anything yet. And the longer I watched the less I liked how easy it was to understand. He wasn't cruel about it and he wasn't kind. He'd weigh a load with his eyes and weigh a man with the same eyes and put the two together the way you'd match a lid to a pot, and the man would go off carrying whatever the man was judged to be worth carrying. Nobody argued it. It was just the sorting, calm, all day, one back at a time, and I stood in the dark and felt the calm of it settle wrong in me, because I knew that calm. I'd had that calm. It's the calm of a thing that's only ever deciding, and never once carrying, and I put the thought down before it got big, because I'd come up here to be a back, not to stand in a doorway thinking about who does the deciding.

So I went over. I waited till the bundle he was on had walked off on its man, and I came up in the little gap before the next one, and I said it plain, the way the pot and the lamp had taught me plain was the only thing that bought anything up here. I was after a back's work. I'd carry.

He didn't stop what he was doing to look at me, which I took for the ordinary rudeness of a busy man and not anything worse. He kept his eyes going over the heaps and he said, "You've not carried."

It wasn't a question so I didn't answer it like one. "No," I said.

That got me the look. He turned it on me the way he turned it on the loads — down the arms, across the shoulders, at the set of my legs under me, a fast honest reckoning of what I could be relied on to haul before I fell down under it. I let him do it. There's no lying to a man whose whole trade is that one glance, and the sack on my back and the soft still left in me from a life of sitting told him what they told him. His mouth did a small thing that wasn't quite a no but was shopping in that direction.

"You'll cost me a load halfway up," he said. "That's what a first back does. Sets it down where I can't get to it and walks off, and then I've a bundle sat in the dark going to nobody and a name I never want to hear again." He said it flat, not to wound me, just totting up what I was likely to run him.

I didn't argue it, because arguing it was exactly the interesting a man like me is better off not being, and because he wasn't wrong. So I just stood there and let it be true and waited, which is a thing I've always been able to do longer than most.

And the waiting was what turned it, or the short of a back was, or the two together. Because he looked past me at the heaps and I saw him land on one particular sorry pile off to the side — smaller bundles, meaner-looking, the kind of freight you could tell just by the wrap that nobody was going to weep over. And I watched him do the sum. A soft first back on that pile was no loss worth the word loss. If I made it up with one of those, he'd got a carry for nothing. If I set it down halfway and vanished, well — he'd already written it off by the look of it before I ever walked in.

"Wrist," he said.

I turned it up. Dark, no number, the way it had been for days. He looked at it about as long as he'd look at a rope, and less worried. Up here past where the count reached clean, half the backs going out the far ways had wrists gone quiet on them — it was near the shape of the job. He didn't care where I'd got mine dark. He cared whether it meant I'd bring somebody down on his stop, and something in how I stood must have said no, or said not-worth-the-trouble, which up here is the same door.

"One of the light ones," he said, and tipped his chin at the sorry pile. "You water where the lamps are. You don't leave the marked way with it. You get it to the top stop and it's off your back and you're paid what a light one pays, which is little, and if you drop it you owe it, and if you owe it you don't come back here, because I'll know your face and it's the one thing about you I'll keep." He was already looking past me at the next load and the next man. "Come at the morning parcel. It goes when I say it goes."

"The morning parcel," I said.

"You'll hear it called." And that was the whole of me to him, done, filed, a light one and a soft back and no more of his day.

I went and found the wall where the other waiting backs had theirs, off from the heat of the lamps, and I got down on my heels among men who knew each other and didn't know me and weren't troubled either way, because up here a man waiting for the morning parcel was the most ordinary thing there was. I had a load coming that nobody wanted and a warning tied to it and no way yet to carry it that I could be sure of. It wasn't much of a place to have got to. But it was a place with my name pending on a slate for once instead of my death, and I sat against the wall and waited for the morning to be called.

*End of Chapter 28.*
