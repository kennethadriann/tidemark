## Chapter 35 — The Way Down

The left mouth kept climbing a while longer, the way I'd got used to, and I let it, because climbing was the one argument my legs had stopped having with me days ago. Up was up. You lean into it and you spend the breath and you don't think about it any more than you think about the weight of your own head. I'd been going up so long that up had quit being a direction and turned into a plain fact of the world, like cold, like the wet coming off the walls. Which is why it took me a fair stretch of walking to feel the ground do what it did, and a good deal longer to believe it.

The way tipped over.

There wasn't much to it while it happened. No lip, no lookout, no place where a man stops and says here's the top and takes it in. The passage just went flat for a bit, flatter than it had been, and my legs kept spending breath they suddenly didn't have anything to spend it on, and I walked a few steps like a man who's misjudged the last stair in the dark and put his foot down hard on a floor that was already there. Then the floor started going the other way. Down and ahead, a slow tilt of it, the tended stone sloping off into the dark in front of my feet instead of rising up to meet them.

I stopped. Not for any reason I could have told you. It was the kind of stop your body does on its own when a thing it had been leaning against isn't there anymore.

Because here is the thing about going down, which I'd known my whole life the way you know a thing without ever once having to say it, and had never had cause to say until then. Up, your weight sits back over your feet and stays put, and the work is all in the pushing, and if you quit pushing you just stop. Down, your weight goes out ahead of you. It wants to get on. It leans into the dark like it's late for something, and the work stops being the pushing and turns into the holding-back, which is a meaner kind of work and one your legs are worse at. The knees take it. The tops of the feet, catching the load of you at every step so it doesn't run off downhill with the rest of you tied to it. I went a little way down and felt the old ache move out of the backs of my legs and into the fronts of them, into places that hadn't done a real day's work since I'd left the low way, and they complained about it like men roused to do a job they'd been told all week wasn't theirs.

The wet didn't help. The stone had been slick since the fork and it got no drier for pointing downhill. I found the good side still under my feet, the tended line, but a tended line that drops is a different promise than one that rises. Up, if your foot slips it slips back into a step you've already made. Down, it slips into the dark you haven't got to yet. So I went slow, slower than the slope wanted me to, feeling out each next bit of it with the flat of a foot before I gave it my weight, taking the tilt the way you take a full pot down a wet step, which is to say not proud and not fast and glad of every place there was to put a hand.

It got colder going down, and I'd have laughed at that if I'd had the breath spare, because a man spends his whole life learning that cold lives up high and warmth pools down low, and up here the world had it backwards along with everything else. The lower the way took me the more the air had that leaked-up feel to it, the downward cold I'd first put my hand into back before the fork, only now I was walking down into where it came from instead of standing at the top of it wondering. The walls sweated harder. Once or twice the good side ran through a low place where the water had gathered ankle-deep and cold enough to bite, and I waded it and climbed out the far side and my feet went from cold to a duller cold that was somehow worse for being quieter about itself.

And somewhere in the middle of all that careful downward walking the other thing caught up to me, the one in the head, and it was slower on its feet than usual. Up had meant the whole business for so long that I'd stopped hearing the word for what it was. Up after Maning, up to the yards at the far end of it. I'd said it and thought it and walked it until up and toward had come to mean the one thing, the way a man's road and a man's errand blur into each other when he's walked the road long enough. And here was the marked way, the load-way, the one the far men kept their marks on because loads came back down it, going down. It hadn't lied to me. I'd known at the fork the yards were down in the drowned country somewhere, breathing up cold; I'd stood at the mouth of the right one and felt it. I just hadn't carried the knowing in my legs yet, and now I was, and it sat strange in them. The place I was going to had never much cared whether I climbed to it or dropped to it. It was going to be wherever it was. I'd just spent so long climbing that dropping felt, for a bad little while, like walking the wrong way on purpose.

It wasn't. It was the way. I told my legs so and they didn't believe me any more than the road came faster for the watching, but they went on doing it, which is most of what you can ask of legs.

I went down a good while into the colder dark, holding my own weight back off my toes and finding the wet line one careful foot at a time, and when the slope finally eased off into something near flat again I was lower than I'd been since the top stop and a fair way further along than I'd been at the last lamp, in country that got no warmer for my being in it. I found a dry-enough shelf a little off the good side and I sat on it and worked my feet out of the ache a while before I gave any more of the down to my legs.

*End of Chapter 35.*
