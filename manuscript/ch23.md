## Chapter 23 — Back Under the Weather

The line went up the way a thing goes up when nobody built it to be a way. A slope of old floor that used to be level and had given up on it. Then a stretch where the water had left a lip of silt you could walk on if you didn't mind the way it gave under the boot. Then a bad bit where the ceiling came down and I went up on my hands too, the bag swinging round to knock my ribs like it wanted back in the water. I took it slow. There was no other speed on offer.

The wet came up the line with me a good while. My clothes hung at twice their weight and let go of their cold in no hurry, and then somewhere in the climbing they stopped being wet and started being damp, which is worse in the way the middle of a thing is always worse than either end of it. Wet, at least you know where you stand. Damp just settles on you and takes its time and doesn't tell you when it means to leave.

I won't pretend I was thinking about much up in there. You climb a dark line with your own breath loud in your ears and you shrink down to the size of the next handhold. That's most of what a body is good for, keeping the head small and pointed at the next thing so it can't wander off and start asking the big useless questions.

I noticed the change before I could have told you what it was. A fair way up, past the hands-and-knees stretch, the line widened and firmed and let me walk upright, and I did, and something was different and I stood there stupid a second before it came to me. The neck. Down in the kitchen, and out at the fraying edge that day with Sol, the thing I'd felt come off my neck had gone so quiet in the leaving I'd half forgotten it had ever sat there. That's the trick of a weight you carry your whole life. It doesn't feel like a weight. It feels like the temperature of being a person, right up until it's gone and you're standing there light and can't work out why the air's changed. Now it was coming back. And coming back it made itself known the way the leaving never had, a low pressure settling onto the back of me like weather you can feel in a bad knee before you can see it in the sky. Somewhere up the line the count was reaching down for me again, faint as a draft under a shut door. My skin had felt for it a half-step ahead of my head, the way skin does.

I stopped and worked my sleeve down over my wrist.

I'd solved this part already, back in the dry, the way I solve everything — sitting still and turning it over till the edges wear off. A dark wrist in thick-signal country. I'd had it clean in my head. You don't hide the wrist. A hidden wrist reads as a man with a reason to hide one, and the reading is the whole danger, not the wrist. You wear it ordinary. Sleeve down like anyone keeping the chill off. You keep the arm easy and moving so no eye snags on the one limb in the room that isn't chattering its number at everything it passes. On paper it was nothing. A note to myself. The kind of prep I could do all afternoon because it cost me not one thing and let me feel ready while I sat exactly where I was.

Doing it with a real wrist, in a real cold sleeve, on a real slope with the weather coming back down onto my neck, was a different article. The cloth was wet and wouldn't lie flat, and I stood there tugging at a cuff like a man fussing his good shirt before church, and I heard myself, in my own head, telling myself to leave it, that fussing the cuff was exactly the snagging thing the plan was built to stop. The plan had not accounted for the part where you have to not-fuss the cuff while your hands are cold and your whole self has just remembered it can be seen. On paper the man wearing the dark wrist was calm, because on paper I was the one writing him calm. Out here he was me, and I'd have to do the calm by hand, the same as the stepping-in, the same as the not-turning-back. Everything I'd thought I could pack turned out to need carrying.

That's the shape of what I was doing, if I'd wanted to say it out loud, which I didn't, there being no one to say it to. A man who'd got himself uncounted — the one clean thing this whole business had ever handed me, the dark wrist, the lifted neck, the going-by without a number falling — was climbing back up on purpose to where the counting is thickest, to fetch a man who didn't know he needed fetching. Walking back into the weather. You spend your life learning to stand where nobody's looking and then one day the errand's up in the light and there's no version of it you can run from the shadows.

Higher up, where the line bent and a little grey came down from somewhere ahead — not light, just the dark going thinner — I passed a place where the wall had been marked. Not a slate, not one of ours. Somebody had scratched into the old skin of the passage, deep, with a hard point, a row of strokes and a shape I didn't have a name for, worn soft at the edges the way a thing goes when hands and damp have been at it a long while. A salvager's mark, most likely. Maning's kind of people leave them, up and down the routes, this way's good, this way floods, don't. I ran my thumb over it without meaning to. It had that same wrongness the old woman's mother had carried, out at the edge — a worn that was more worn than the story allowed for, a hand older than the hands that were supposed to be the oldest. I didn't stop on it. I've learned that a number I can't make sit is a number to leave alone and keep walking past, or it'll have you standing in a wet line all day doing arithmetic in the dark. I filed it where I file those now, on the soft shelf, and went on.

The line kept climbing. The weather kept coming down. I kept my sleeve where I'd put it and my arm easy the way I'd drawn it up for myself weeks ago, and I found that the drawing did help a little, that a thing worked out in advance isn't worth nothing even when it turns out to need doing by hand after all. I was cold and I was damp and I was going up into the part of the world that keeps a record, one careful step at a time, with a bag on my back and a wrist that said nothing, and no clever way left to do it but the plain way, on my feet, in the open, getting nearer.

*End of Chapter 23.*
