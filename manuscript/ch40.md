## Chapter 40 — The Edge of the Yards

The slope let me down into it slow, and I was glad of the slowness, because a place gets bigger the nearer you come to it and the yards kept doing that the whole way down. What had been a scatter of low warm smudges from the top of the ease grew, the closer I got, into fires with men actually at them, and the men grew into the loads they were working, and the loads grew into a low steady racket of a size I hadn't heard since the top stop and had half forgotten a place could make. By the time the slope ran out under my feet and I was standing on the level floor of the widening, the yards had stopped being a thing I was looking at and become a thing I was in.

I want to say I felt something big walking in. Mostly what I felt was how quiet I'd got. A man who's been weeks alone with his own feet and the water working the stone forgets that the world has a volume to it, and the yards handed the volume back to me all at once, and for a while I just stood at the near edge of it and let my ears take the beating. Metal on metal somewhere off in the deep of it. A load coming down off a back onto a heap with that flat wet slap I knew now from the inside. Voices, not one of them aimed at me, going about the small mean business of men who work and eat in the same dark they sleep in. Somebody laughing, short, at something I'd never know. It was the most ordinary sound in the world and it near took the legs out from under me, how much I'd wanted it without letting myself count that I wanted it.

Then a man near the first fire looked at me, and I remembered what I was.

I was the wet stranger who'd just walked in out of the dark with nothing on his back. I'd been that man before, at the waystation and the top stop, and I knew the shape of the trouble, but the yards was bigger and deeper and rougher than any of them, and down here the trouble had more edges. A peopled place this far from anywhere is made entirely of men who know each other or at least know each other's kind, and a face with no kind to it stands out the way a dropped tool stands out on a swept floor. The man by the fire didn't do anything about me. He just took me in, the wet of me and the no-account of me, filed it wherever men down here file a thing that isn't yet their problem, and went back to his hands. But he'd looked, and the looking told me the first thing I needed to know, which was that I couldn't just walk into the middle of this and expect the middle to make room.

So I didn't try for the middle. That was the whole of the work, that first while, and it was work I knew. A stranger who comes fast into the heart of a place is asking to be dealt with, and the yards had the look of a place that dealt with things and didn't lose sleep over it. The middle was for men who belonged in the middle, the ones with a load to hand off or a load to take on, the ones the place was for. I wasn't any of those yet. I was a man who'd arrived, which is a smaller thing than a man who's come to do business, and the two don't stand in the same place. So I found the near edge of the widening, back where the fires threw more shadow than light, and I put myself there, out of the flow of the loads but not skulking in the dark past it either, because skulking is its own kind of loud. Just an odd man at the edge of a working place, of which there are always a few, the between-men, the ones resting off a load or waiting on one, too worn to be anywhere in particular. I made myself one of those. It's a station you can hold without anybody's leave, so long as you hold it quietly and don't reach for more of the place than it's offered you.

There was a low fire near my edge that nobody seemed to own, the kind that gets fed by whoever's nearest when it's dying and belongs to none of them. A couple of men were at it when I drifted over, not together, just two men warming at the same nothing fire the way you do, and neither of them made a thing of a third one doing the same. I crouched at the cold side of it, the side the between-men get, and I put my hands to what heat came around, and that was it. That was the footing. No word passed. Nobody asked me my kind. I was just one more worn man at a poor fire in a place full of them, and for a place like the yards, being nobody worth a word is the closest thing to welcome it hands out.

The hunger came up hard once I was still and warm enough to feel it, because that's when it does, and there was food in the yards, I could smell it working under the smoke, cooking somewhere in the middle where the men with something to trade were. I had a few mean bits of metal in my pocket from the delivery a lifetime ago, and I thought about it, and then I didn't, not yet. Food was a transaction, and a transaction meant walking up to somebody and opening my mouth, and I'd only just got done teaching the yards that I was nothing to bother with. I wasn't going to spend that in the first hour to quiet a stomach that had learned to wait this long. The stomach could wait a while more. I'd got good at teaching it to.

So I stayed at the edge of the poor fire while the yards went on being the yards around me, the loads coming and going in the middle, men I didn't know doing work I couldn't see for reasons that were none of mine. Somewhere in all of it, if the far man at his own fire a day back had told me true, was the talk I'd come all this way to catch, the talk of the men who go out and stay out and come back heavy. But I didn't reach for it. It wasn't the first hour's work. The first hour's work was just to be here, and to be nobody, and to let the place get used to the shape of me at the edge of its light. I let it. I warmed my hands and I watched the fires and I waited, which was a thing I could still do better than almost anything, and for once it was the right thing to be good at.

*End of Chapter 40.*
