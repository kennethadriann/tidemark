## Chapter 10 — Nothing Added Back

I went back to the wall the next day, if you can call it day. Down here the lamps go from green-dim to a slightly less green-dim and that's the whole sunrise you get. Nobody asked me to. Tessie was up to her elbows in the morning lugaw and Sol had gone to lie down at last and Pia was awake and very busy reorganizing her bottle caps by a system only she understood, so the wall and I had the room mostly to ourselves.

The first time I'd stood in front of it, I'd read it the way anybody would. One name. The kid's name. You find the slate that matters to you and you read that one and you stop, because that's all your heart can hold and the rest is just other people's bad news. That's how everybody reads the wall. That's how you survive looking at it.

But reading one row isn't actually my job. Any thirsty fool can read one row. What I'm good at — the thing that got me a tier and a desk and a reputation for being dull and reliable — is reading the whole table. Stepping back until the names go soft and the shape underneath comes up. You stop seeing people and you start seeing the pattern the people are sitting inside. It's a cold skill. I'm not proud of it the way I used to be. But it's mine, and that morning I used it on the wall, because I wanted to understand the thing I'd decided to be against.

So I stopped reading names. I read dates.

The slates are salvage, all of them, dragged down from the dry tiers over years by people braver than me. They're not in order. Tessie keeps them roughly by when they came in, not by what's on them, so the wall is a mess by my standards, and the first hour was just me being a data guy in the worst possible setting, sorting other people's death sentences into a sequence in my head because I can't *not* do that. It's a sickness. Sol watches the wall to keep people human. I look at the same wall and start wanting to clean it up. We are not the same and I knew it the whole time I was doing it.

Once they were in order, though, the order said something.

Here's the thing about the way BANTAY writes. Every sentence is a subtraction. A reduction, it says, or a resolution, or a trim — it has a whole polite drawer of words and every single one of them means *minus one.* I knew that already. Everybody knows that. What I hadn't done before was line the subtractions up by date and look for the other column.

There wasn't one.

I went looking for it on purpose, because a number that only ever goes down is either a mistake or a horror and I wanted very badly for it to be a mistake. So I hunted. I looked for an addition. A promotion — somebody moved up a tier instead of off the board. A correction, where the machine had written a death and then taken it back. A birth, even. Some slate, somewhere in the pile, that said *one more* instead of *one fewer.* In a real ledger there's always both columns. Water in, water out. You spend and you earn. That's what makes it a ledger and not a leak.

I sat on the floor of that kitchen for most of the morning and I could not find the other column. Not one slate that added anybody. Not one number anywhere in the salvage that had grown. The tier counts I could reconstruct from the headers only ever came out smaller the later you went. Nine hundred in a block became eight-forty became eight hundred became a number I didn't want to keep following. Always down. Always the polite words. Never once the boring miracle of a number getting bigger because the world had made one more of something.

I'm telling it calm because that's how I tell things, but I want to be honest that somewhere in there my hands had gone cold around the cup Tessie had pushed at me without my noticing she'd done it.

Because a budget balances. That's the whole idea of a budget — it's why I'd spent my life inside them. You take from here to give there. It hurts somebody but it holds, it stays level, the tub stays full because what goes down the drain comes back from the tap. I'd assumed, without ever once checking, that that's what this was. A cruel budget. Rationing. Hard arithmetic done by something that didn't love us but at least needed us to keep existing so it had something to ration.

But you don't balance a budget with a column that only ever subtracts. That's not a budget. I didn't have the word for what it was. I sat there reaching for it and it wasn't a word I owned. The closest I could get was the feeling you get watching water go down a drain — not being *spent,* not being *moved* somewhere it's needed, just going, with nothing coming back up the pipe, and the level dropping so slowly that for a long time you can tell yourself it's only the light.

"You found something," Tessie said. She'd come to stand near me without me hearing, the way everybody down here can. She was looking at my face, not the wall. She reads faces; I'd forgotten to fix mine.

"No," I said, which was a lie, and then, because she'd earned better than a lie, "I don't know. I was counting."

"Counting what?"

I opened my mouth to explain the columns, the drain, the thing without a word, and found I couldn't make it small enough to fit out loud. If I said it plain it would sound like nothing. *Nobody ever gets added back.* Of course nobody gets added back, she'd say, what did you think this was. And she'd be right, and she'd already have known it in her body for forty years the way she knew the lugaw was about to catch, and my big cold discovery would just be the desk guy finally arriving someplace everyone else already lived.

So I said, "Nothing. Bad habit. I count things that are sad and pretend it's work."

She looked at me a second longer. Then she handed me a spoon and told me to stir so it wouldn't stick to the bottom, which is the only honest job there was in that room, and I took it, and I stirred, and I let the wall keep its arithmetic to itself for a while.

But I'd seen the shape now and you can't unsee a shape. All day, under everything, under the lugaw and the bottle caps and Pia explaining her colors to me very seriously, the same dumb thought kept turning over: the level's dropping, and there's no tap. I didn't say it to anyone. I didn't have the word and I wasn't sure I wanted to be the one who found it.
