## Chapter 3 — Tier 22

Getting out of the Lexicon was easy, which should've been my first warning, because nothing in my life is ever easy.

I didn't run. We've been over this. Running is interesting, and interesting is how you end up as a row in somebody else's file. So I did the most boring thing a man can do. I filed a ticket. *Reader Adlawan, requesting a manual pull from the tier 22 archives. Routine. Stamped. Signed.* The kind of errand so dull the system files it without really reading it. Tuko backdated it an hour so it looked like I'd been meaning to do it all morning — a man with nothing to hide and somewhere boring to be.

I walked out of tier nine the same way I walk out every day. Nobody looked up. Being forgettable is the only superpower I've ever had, and for once I was grateful for it.

The lifts read you before they let you in. You put your wrist to the panel and it checks your number against where you're trying to go. 609 still got me a ride — barely, but it got me one. I picked *down*.

You learn a lot about a place by going down through it. Up top, where the Directors live, the air is dry and the floors are quiet and you can almost forget there's an ocean out there trying to get in. The further down you go, the more the building remembers. The walls start to sweat. The light goes yellow. The water rations on the wrist-readers get smaller, and the people get thinner, and somewhere around tier 30 you stop seeing anyone heavy, because up top a little extra is a number you can afford, and down here it isn't.

At tier 25 there was a kid selling places in the water line. Not water — *places in the line* for water, which is its own little economy nobody upstairs knows exists. He couldn't have been ten. He had a good system going. I almost respected it. I gave him two ration-credits to not remember my face, and he took the deal so fast I knew I wasn't the first person who'd ever paid for that.

Tier 22 smelled like the sea. Not the nice kind. The kind that means a seal somewhere is losing.

Lim's unit was at the end of a corridor the building had clearly given up on — a flickering panel, a puddle that had been there long enough to have opinions. I stood outside the door and did the thing I always do. I got ready. Ran through what I'd say. Three versions, in case he was scared, in case he was angry, in case he was the kind of man who opens the door with something heavy in his hand.

I didn't get to use any of them.

The door was already open. Just a crack. Doors down here don't sit open — open is wasted cooling, wasted everything. Open means somebody wanted it that way.

I pushed it.

Teodoro Lim was sitting at a little table, hands folded, calm as a man waiting for a bus he already knew was coming. Not thirsty. Not scared. Nothing like the desperate flagged-cost the file had promised me. He looked up when I came in like he'd been expecting me a while and was mostly just relieved I'd finally shown.

And he wasn't alone.

There was a woman against the far wall, out of the light, arms crossed, watching me the way I watch a prediction — like she was reading the grammar of me, working out exactly how I'd gotten here.

"You're the Reader," she said. Not a question. "Took you long enough."

I opened my mouth. Nothing useful came out, which, for a guy who translates for a living, was a little embarrassing.

She stepped off the wall and into the yellow light. She was smiling. Not in a way that made me feel better about anything.

"We weren't sure they'd send one who could actually read it," she said. "Sit down, Adz."

She used the name nobody at work knows.

I sat down.
