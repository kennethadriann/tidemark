## Chapter 29 — The Light One

Morning up there wasn't a color, it was a sound. Somewhere off in the heaps a man banged a bit of metal against another bit of metal, twice, not in a hurry, and all along the wall the waiting backs came up off their heels at once the way a row of dogs lifts its heads when the door goes. I came up with them a beat behind, because I hadn't known yet that the banging was the morning being called. Nobody said parcel. Nobody said anything. They just stopped being men resting and started being men about to work, and I copied them, because copying was most of what I'd brought up the line with me.

The parcel-man didn't look for me and I didn't make him. He sent the good loads off on the backs he trusted first, calm, one after another, and only when the room had thinned did he come down to the mean pile last, the way you feed the animals you're not too fond of after the ones you are. He tipped his chin at a bundle about the size of a sack of rice and roughly the shape of one, wrapped in sacking gone soft with handling, and he said, "That one. Top stop. Go." And that was the whole of the ceremony. I'd half thought there'd be more to it. There wasn't. There never is, with the things that turn out to matter.

I want to tell the next part straight, because the straight part is the part I hadn't seen coming. I'd watched the leaver do it down at the watering lamp and I'd thought the trade was four plain things. Back to the load. Squat. Arms through the ropes. Stand. I'd done harder-looking things in my life with a spoon and a bad knee.

So I backed up to it and squatted and got my arms in under the ropes the way he had, and I stood — and the load stood with me for about half a step, and then it decided it would rather go somewhere I wasn't going. It wasn't that it was heavy, though it was heavier than a sack of rice has any business being. It was that it was *alive*. It leaned. It found the weak side of me before I knew I had a weak side and it went for it, and I went with it three staggering steps into the dark before I got a shoulder under it and shoved it back over my spine, and stood there breathing like I'd run somewhere, with a bundle nobody wanted riding me like it had opinions.

The leaver's one clean motion, the thing I'd admired with my eyes — the finding of the center and letting your legs argue it down — turned out to be the whole job, and I couldn't do it. I could only do the after. I'd let the weight slide off true, feel it start to take me, and haul it back, and it slid again, and I hauled again, and that was how I went: not carrying the thing so much as losing it slow and catching it slower, all the way up, one save at a time.

The marked way I'd thought was near to cheating the day before, the pale scuffs and the stones set along the good side — I found out on that climb who'd marked it and why. It was marked by men with loads on them. The good side wasn't the pretty side or the dry side. It was the side that didn't cant you toward the drop when your weight had already made up its mind to go there. I didn't learn that off the signs. I learned it by getting it wrong, by drifting onto the low side where the stones weren't and feeling the whole bundle try to walk me off the edge of the way, and correcting only because my body was more scared than I was. A slower school. The one that stays.

I watered where the lamps were, because I'd been told to and because by the second lamp I'd have watered in a puddle if a puddle was what there was. The setting-down was the sweetest thing I'd felt in days — the load going onto a ledge and my back coming up off it slow, every knuckle of my spine reporting in one at a time. And the picking-up-again after was the meanest, because now I knew what it cost and had to spend it anyway, on purpose, with the memory fresh.

There was an old walker resting at that second lamp, older than the gray one down below, a man gone so far to leather he looked cured rather than aged. He watched me fight my bundle up onto my back without offering to help, which I took for manners rather than meanness — a man who's carried knows you have to learn the getting-up yourself or you never learn it. When I had it settled he tipped his chin at the run above us, the long dark climb to the top stop, and said the top used to be dry. Not drowned-and-drained. Dry. His father walked loads up to it when it was dry ground with a road on it, he said, and the water only came up over it in his own long life, a hand's height at a time. He said it the flat way old men say a thing they've stopped being surprised by. And some part of me, even bent double and stupid with the climb, did the arithmetic it always does and couldn't make it sit — a father in living memory, and a hundred years of drowning, don't fit on the same shelf. But I had a load on me and a top stop to reach and no room in me to hold a wrong number and a bundle both. So I let it go by. Old men mix their fathers up with their grandfathers; the water plays tricks on a man's years. I filed it soft, the way I'd filed the old woman and the worn mark before it, and I got back under the light one, and I went on up.

By the fourth lamp I'd stopped waiting to get good at it. There was no good at it coming, not today. There was only the load and the save and the next stone on the right side, and the far-off idea of a top stop where this particular bundle would come off my back and be somebody else's problem. I kept the pale scuffs on my right. I lost the weight and caught it. I climbed.

*End of Chapter 29.*
