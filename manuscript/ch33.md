## Chapter 33 — Between Lamps

I'd been walking on a lie my legs had been telling me, and it took a while up the long run to catch them at it. The lie was that the next lamp was close. Not that I'd ever thought the words. It was more that the whole climb had trained the idea into me the way a road trains a habit into a foot. All the way up from the low way there'd been a lamp in sight or near enough, or a bend I knew had one behind it, and I'd walked from one to the next without ever having to ask myself how far the next one was, because the next one was always about to be there. Water at the lamps, the old back had said, and I'd taken it the way you take a thing that's always been true, as if lamps were a kind of weather that just happened along the way at decent intervals.

Up here they didn't. That was the whole of it, and it took the morning to teach me and most of the afternoon to believe.

The way went up and then it went flat for a stretch and then it went up again, and the good side held under my feet, the tended stone, so I knew I hadn't lost the marked way. But the marks were the only company the way had. No smoke gone into the walls, no pot-smell coming down to meet me. No woman's voice calling a price at nobody, which I hadn't liked at the time and now would have walked a fair way to hear. Just the drip, which is a sound that keeps no man company however much of it there is, and my own feet, and the dark up ahead that I hadn't got to yet. I walked into a good deal of that dark. More of it than I'd walked into at any one stretch since I'd started up, and there was always more of it, and no light at the far end of it that I could point my eyes at and want.

That was the thing I hadn't reckoned on, and it wore worse than the climb did. On the low way the wanting had somewhere to go. You get tired, you find the next lamp with your eyes, you tell your legs it's just to there, and they mostly believe you because you're mostly right. Take the lamp away and the tired has nowhere to spend itself. It just sits in the shoulders and the backs of the knees and stays. I found myself doing sums I had no numbers for. How far since the field, and whether I'd passed the hour where a sensible man would have had the next lamp in his eye by now. I had nothing to put in the sums but the ache, and the ache is a poor clock. It runs fast when you're low and slow when you're stubborn and it never once tells you the truth about distance.

So I stopped doing the sums, which is a thing I'm better at than most, the stopping. I put my eyes back on the good side under my feet and I walked the pace my body had learned and I let the lamp be wherever it was going to be. A watched road doesn't come any faster. I'd have told a younger man that and he wouldn't have listened, same as I only half did.

The lamp, when it finally came, wasn't a place. I want to put that down plain because it took me a moment to understand it and the moment was a cold one. I came up a long tilt in the dark and there it was, a light, small and steady, hung where the way widened out a little, and my whole body did the thing it had learned to do at a light — got ready to be a customer, got ready to be read, got the true small line about the low way sitting up front in my mouth where I could reach it. And there was nobody to say it to. The lamp burned on a bracket over a bare widening in the rock, and the widening was empty. No stall. No pot. No back sitting on a wall. A light on nothing, kept alight by nobody I could see, the way a light is left in a window of a house where everyone's gone to bed or gone for good, and you can't tell which from the road.

There was water. I'll give the far country that. A cut in the wall under the lamp caught the drip into a stone hollow, and the hollow was clean, and the water in it was cold in a way the low-way water never was, a cold that had come off something deeper than the wall. Somebody had cut that hollow. Somebody kept the lamp fed. I looked for the somebody the way you look for a face in a place a face ought to be, and there wasn't one, and I understood then what the lamps were up here. Down below a lamp meant people, a stop, a pot, a wall to sit on and be nobody at. Up the long run a lamp didn't mean people. It only meant the way was still the way. A mark that somebody, sometime, had thought this stretch of dark worth telling a man he wasn't lost in. That was all the company it was built to be, and it did that job and no other, and it did it for a man it would never see.

I drank, because the water didn't care who kept it and neither did my thirst. I sat on the bare rock under the light a while, not resting so much as letting the fact settle, the way you let a cold room settle around you before you decide it's the room you've got. Then I felt it, or thought I did, coming up the way from ahead. Not a sound. A change in the air off the far dark, a breath of it that reached the lamp and turned the little flame once and went by. It was colder than the wall was, and it carried a smell on it I didn't have a name for, low and wide and old, not the drip and not the smoke, something the deep ground breathes that the high ground doesn't. It was there and then the flame stood straight again and it was gone, and I couldn't have sworn to it in front of a hard man. But I'd read enough true small things off the world by then to know when it was telling me the country up ahead was a different country than the one behind.

I filled what I could carry from the cold hollow and I got up off the rock. The lamp went on burning on its nothing behind me, keeping the way for whoever came next, and I went on up into the dark past it toward the smell I couldn't name, one lamp poorer in company than I'd have liked and one drink richer, which was the trade the long run offered and the only one on the table.

*End of Chapter 33.*
