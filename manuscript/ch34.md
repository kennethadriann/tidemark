## Chapter 34 — The First Fork

The smell I couldn't name didn't go away the way I'd half expected it to. On the low way a thing like that came and went, a draft under a door, gone before you'd finished turning your head to it. This one didn't leave. I walked up past the lamp on its nothing and into the dark and the smell came with me, or I went into it, and after a while it stopped being a thing that had happened to the air and started being the air. Low and wide and old, like I'd said to myself back at the hollow. I still didn't have a word for it. But it was the country now, not a message from it, and a man gets used to breathing most things given enough dark to do it in.

The ground changed under me before I let myself notice I'd noticed. That's a Reader's trick and not a good one, knowing a thing a while before you'll say it, so you can pretend you worked it out clever when really you'd just been sitting on it. The good side had been going wet. Not the low way's honest drip, the one that keeps no man company, that I'd have welcomed by then like an old face. This was different water. The walls had started to sweat it, a sheen you felt more than saw, and the tended stone underfoot had a give to it in places, a softness where the water had been working at it longer than anybody up here had been alive to see. Cold came off it the way it had come off the water in the hollow, a cold with a downward feel to it, as if it were leaking up from somewhere the light had never been.

I put my hand flat on the wall once, to know it, the way you'll touch a thing to make it true. It was wet and cold and old. The low-way rock had been rock a man had cut and smoked and handled, rock with people gone into it. This was rock the water had made and was still making, and it hadn't asked anybody. I took my hand back and wiped it on my leg and didn't make anything of it. There was nothing to make. The ground was turning into different ground, is all, and I was the fool walking up into it on purpose.

The way kept its marks a while longer, and I was glad of them past what I'd admit. Then it stopped being simple.

I came up a slow rise where the water ran thin across the whole floor of the passage, a sheet of it no deeper than the sole of a foot but wide, wall to wall, so there was no dry line to keep to. And on the far side of the sheet the way forked. Not a bend, not a widening with a lamp on nothing. Two mouths in the dark where there'd been one, both of them going up and back into the rock, and the marked way, which had been a single tended line I'd only had to keep my feet on since the top stop, went into both of them. Or into neither. The good side ran down to the water's edge and the sheet swallowed it, and on the other bank I couldn't for the life of me say which mouth it climbed back out of.

I stood at the near edge of the water a good while. There was no hurry in it, no one behind me and no lamp ahead to make for, so I let myself have the while. This was the first time up the long run the way had asked me a question instead of just being the way. Down below a fork meant a choice a man made, this stall or that one, the pot or the wall, and somebody had cut a mark to tell you which was which because somebody down below cared whether you got where you meant to. Up here nobody had cut me a thing. The two mouths sat there in the dark not caring at all which one I put my body into, and the water ran flat and cold across the both of them, and it was on me to read it or stand there till my legs gave out.

So I read it, because that was the one thing I'd brought up the long run that still worked. I crossed the sheet slow, feeling for the tended stone through the cold with the flats of my feet, the give of the worked rock against the rougher give of the rock the water had made, and on the far bank the good side came up under my soles again, faint, but there, leading into the left of the two mouths. The right one, when I put my head into it a step, breathed at me. The cold came harder out of it, and the smell with it, the low wide old one, stronger there than anywhere yet, coming up out of that dark like the dark had a throat. The left mouth breathed nothing. It just went up, tended, quiet, ordinary as the long run had been.

A man could stand a long time working out what that meant, and I've been that man, and I didn't have the room in me for him tonight. The marked way went left. The far men kept their marks for the ways that got them where the loads were, and the load-ways were the ones a man could come back down. I took the left. But I'll put down plain that I stood at the mouth of the right one a breath longer than I needed to, feeling the deep country breathe up the throat of it, because I understood now, standing at the fork with the cold coming up out of the one side, that the yards weren't a place at the top of a climb the way the top stop had pretended to be. They were the edge of a whole country the water had made down there in the dark, a country that forked and forked and breathed, and I was at the first seam of it, where the tended way and the drowned ground first had to argue about which of them the passage belonged to.

I went up the left mouth on the good side and let the right one keep its breathing to itself. The smell thinned a little once the fork was behind me, or I got used to it again, which up here amounts to the same thing. The stone under me stayed wet and the cold stayed cold and my feet found the tended line and held it, one mouth chosen out of two and the country a little more the deep country's than it had been at the last lamp, which was all the further I was going to get tonight and further than I'd been the night before.

*End of Chapter 34.*
