## Chapter 36 — Somebody's Line

I gave the rest of the down to my legs when the ache in my feet had gone from a shout back to a grumble. That's about as good as feet get on a wet slope, and a man who waits for better than that is a man deciding to sleep on a cold shelf in the deep country, which I wasn't fool enough to want twice in one going. So I got up off the shelf and put my weight out ahead of me again, careful the way the last stretch had taught me to be careful, and I let the tended stone take me on down into the colder dark.

It went on being what it had been. Slick underfoot, cold coming up the way it did now, the good side holding faint but there under my soles when I felt for it. I'd got the trick of the down into my knees a little by then. Not so it was easy, but so it was mine, so I could think about something besides my own toes while I did it, which after the last while was close enough to rest that I took it for rest.

Then the good side ran me down to water and stopped pretending it had anywhere else to be.

It was a black flat of it filling the passage, wall to wall, no thin sheet like the one at the fork that a man crosses with his mind on the far side already. This sat still and it sat deep. I could tell it was deep the way you can tell a thing is deep in the dark, by how the little light there was didn't come back off it the same, by how it took the cold and held it. The floor of the way went down under it and didn't come up again that I could see. A man wanting the far side of that was a man getting wet past the waist, maybe past that, in cold that had already bitten me twice today through nothing worse than an ankle-gather.

I stood at the edge of it and did what I do, which is read before I move, and this time the reading stopped me cold in a different way than the water would have.

There was a line across it.

I didn't see it all at once. I saw the near end of it first, a hitch of cord gone round a horn of the rock at about the height of my hand, worn pale where it wrapped, and my eye went along it out over the black water the way your eye follows a thing that oughtn't to be there till it's sure it is. It ran low and level over the flat, dipping toward the middle where the weight of it and the wet had pulled it, and it fetched up on the far bank at another set of rock I couldn't make out clear, but the line went taut into the dark and held, so there was a hand's work over there answering the hand's work here.

Somebody had rigged that. Somebody had stood where I was standing with cord over his shoulder, found the horn of rock and known it for a good one, taken a wrap and hauled it tight and gone across cold to make the far end fast, so that the next time a man came down to this black water in the dark he'd have a thing to hold that wasn't just his own nerve. And it wasn't the next time either. It was the times after that, and the times before, because you don't spend cord and cold rigging a crossing you'll only make once. You rig it because the way runs through here and you run the way, loaded, in the dark, and you want your two hands doing something better than shaking.

I put my hand on the near hitch to know it, the way I'd put my hand flat on the wet wall back before the fork. This was a different thing to know entirely. The wall had been rock the water made and asked nobody about. This cord had been carried down here in a man's kit and worked by a man's fingers, and it was old, and it had been hauled tight more than the once, and it was the first thing since the top stop that a person had left in the dark on purpose for another person to find. Even if the person he left it for was mostly just himself, later, coming back down under a load with his knees full of the same ache mine had. It was still a thing set out by a hand for a hand. I stood there in the cold holding it and I'll own I held it a breath longer than knowing it took.

Because I'd been alone a long stretch by then. Not lonely, I don't mean that, a man on an errand hasn't the room for lonely. But alone in the plain way, no mark cut for me, no lamp kept for me, the two mouths at the fork not caring which I fed myself into. And here was a length of worn cord telling me flat that people came down this black country, that they came often enough and loaded enough to fix their crossings, that I was near enough their ground now to be walking on the work of their hands. I hadn't found the far men. I'd found where they'd been, and that they meant to come back, which is a different thing than a man and closer than I'd been to one since I left the wall.

I didn't leave it any better or worse than I found it. That was the other thing the top stop had put in me, the load-walker's small honesty, that you don't spend another man's rig and you don't foul it either. I took the cord in both hands, tested it once with a lean the way you'd test a step before a full pot, and it took the lean and gave nothing, whoever's fingers had made the far end fast having made it fast to stay. Then I went out into the black water along it.

The cold came up my legs and past my waist the way it always does, in the installments it likes to be paid in, and the line stayed low and taut in my two hands and I went hand over hand along it out over the deep, my feet finding the drowned floor as long as there was floor and losing it toward the middle and finding it again on the rise to the far bank. It held me the whole way. A stranger's work I'd never thank, bearing a stranger's weight it was never rigged for, in the dark under the drowned country, and it held, and I came up out of the water on the far side with the cold sitting in me and the line behind me still fast to its two horns of rock, waiting on the next man down.

I stood on the far bank a while and let the water run off my legs before I gave the down any more of me. Their country was closer than it had been. I'd come across on the proof of it.

*End of Chapter 36.*
