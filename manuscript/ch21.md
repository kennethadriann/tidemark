## Chapter 21 — The Foot of It

The sack sat by the wall a day and a half, and I let it, and I told myself that letting it sit was the same as never having packed it. That's a sum I can always make come out right when I need it to. A packed bag nobody picks up is just a pile of things that happen to be near each other. I'd nearly talked myself the whole way back to that when my hands went and did the next thing.

I was crossing the kitchen for no reason, which is the kind of crossing a man does when he's avoiding the one spot he's actually walking toward, and on the way past I bent and took the sack up by the mouth and put it on my shoulder. Not a decision. The same nothing as before, the hands moving while the rest of me watched from a little ways off, mildly interested, like a neighbor. The weight settled across my back the way a weight does, and that was new. By the wall it had been a thing standing there. On my shoulder it was a thing I was carrying, and a man carrying a bag is going somewhere with it, even if the somewhere turns out to be three steps and then a long stand.

I didn't announce it. You don't announce these things. Announcing is just asking the room to talk you out of it, and I wasn't ready to be talked out of it and I wasn't ready to not be. So I walked. Past the pot, past Lim humming over the caps the girl had left him to sort, past the dry-storage where the wall of names lived under its tarp. Nobody looked up in a way that meant anything. Tessie clocked me, because Tessie clocks everything, it's her one indulgence, but she let me go by with a bag on my back the way you let a man go by with his face arranged a certain way. Whatever it was, she'd decided, it wasn't ready to be a conversation yet.

The kitchen gives onto the way up through a low flooded run, the place we'd come in. I'd been through it once, the other direction, half-drowned and not minding much, because the not-minding was the only trick I had that day. Now I came at it dry and on my own feet with a bag of water on my back, which is a different errand entirely. The floor slopes down to it. You feel the cold before you see the water, a flat black shine where the light from the kitchen gives out. The way up starts by going down into that, wet to the waist, and then up the far side into the dry of the line. Wet at the bottom, dry at the top, like I'd put it to myself the other morning. The bottom of it was right there in front of my boots.

I stopped at the edge. Not a planned stop. My feet did it the way feet do at the top of a stair in the dark, the body counting a step that isn't there and stalling on it. The water sat a hand's width from my toes. I could feel the cold coming up off the surface, the particular cold of water that's been standing in the dark under the world a long time and isn't in a hurry to warm for anybody. The bag was on my back. The route was under my feet. Everything I'd spent two days getting ready was ready, and the only thing left between me and the man up the line was the plain wet act of stepping into that and starting.

I didn't step into it.

Standing there, I felt the thing I'd half-known since the night I started packing finish its sentence. Every other time I'd readied a thing, the readiness had been the far end of it. There was never anything past the prep I had to walk into. Here there was the water, an actual cold I'd have to put my actual legs down into, and the prep didn't reach across it. You can pack for a swim on dry ground all day and stay dry. The pack doesn't get you wet. At some point a thing stops being a list and turns into a temperature, and I was standing right on the line where my list ran out and the temperature started, and the bag had carried me exactly that far and not one inch into the water.

So I stood. The bag got heavier the way a bag does when you quit walking and just wear it. Behind me the kitchen went on with its noises, the spoon, the caps, somebody laughing at the front half of a joke I couldn't hear. In front of me the black water sat there not caring, same as the lugaw doesn't care, same as everything in this place that's only doing its own flat job and isn't impressed by a man having a moment at the edge of it. I waited to feel myself decide. I'd half expected the body, having got me this far on its own steam, to just finish the errand and step me in before the rest of me could file the objection. It didn't. The body carried the load to the water and then it handed the rest back to me, like a porter who'll take your bag as far as the door and won't come inside.

I lifted the sack off my shoulder and set it down on the dry lip of the run, up out of the wet where it would keep. Not back at the wall. Here, at the foot of it, where a man leaves a thing he means to pick up on his way through. That was the only thing I let myself settle, and it wasn't much: the bag waits here now, not back there. A few feet of floor closer to the cold. It doesn't sound like anything and it mostly wasn't.

Then I went back for the spoon, because it was my turn on the pot and Tessie had the look that meant it had caught on the bottom. I scraped it loose and stirred up from under so the burnt part wouldn't spread into the rest. Lim hummed. The girl came back for her caps and counted them out loud, wrong, and started over. The bag sat at the foot of the wet run where I'd left it. We ate.

*End of Chapter 21.*
