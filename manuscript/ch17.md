## Chapter 17 — The First Inch

Tessie found the slate where I'd left it, the way I knew she would. I was awake to watch her find it, because I hadn't really gone to sleep, just lay there with my eyes shut doing arithmetic that wouldn't come out. She picked it up with the rest of the morning's odds, read it the way she reads everything, and her face didn't do anything. That's a thing about Tessie. The worse the news, the less her face spends on it. She set it down a little apart from the others, the way you set a cup down when there's a crack in it and you don't want it taking a knock, and went on with the lugaw.

I waited for her to say something. She didn't. So after a while I said it. "You saw the name."

"I saw it."

"Maning."

"I can read, Adz." Not unkind. She just doesn't waste words on the obvious, and she'd already moved past me to the question that mattered, which was the pot. She gave it a stir. "When's he due back down?"

I didn't know. That was the first thing I noticed about myself, and I didn't like it. I'd read that man's death three times by lamplight and I didn't know the most ordinary thing about him, which was when he'd next be standing in this kitchen drinking his lugaw and telling his fish joke. I knew the date on his slate. I didn't know his schedule. That's the kind of man I am. I'll memorize the shape of a catastrophe and not know what day the bread comes.

Sol came over then, drawn by the quiet the way she is, and Tessie passed her the slate without a word, and Sol read it and went still in that contained way of hers, and the three of us stood around the little crack in the morning and nobody said the useless thing. Nobody said *we have to save him.* We'd all been to that wall before, with Pia. Saving a man off it doesn't empty the wall; it just writes the next name. We knew that. So what was left wasn't *can we win,* it was the smaller, dumber, more useful question. What do you actually do about a man walking around up there with his date already written.

"He comes down on no schedule I ever found," Sol said. "Whenever he's got a load and the water lets him. Could be tomorrow. Could be a week."

"And his date?"

"Out. Days, not hours. Like Pia's was." She looked at me. "Not a sprint."

"No," I said. "Not a sprint." And I felt something then that I want to put down carefully, because it's the whole of what happened that morning and it doesn't look like much.

I'd started planning.

Not deciding. I want to be honest, because the two get mixed up and I've spent my life hiding in the gap between them. I hadn't decided a single thing. I didn't know if I'd go up, or send word, or sit on my hands and let the days run out the way they were always going to. But some part of me had quietly opened a drawer and begun laying things out on the table, the way I do. When does he come down. What's the route up to where he salvages. Where does the signal go thick, and how far in could a man go before the wrist starts reaching and the weather sits back on his neck. How many days is *days.* I was doing it without being asked, under the talk, the way you find you've been tapping your foot.

Now, here's the thing. That tapping is the oldest thing I do. It's the thing I'm *for.* Give me a problem and I'll lay it all out on the table and look at it from every side and find the seams and the soft spots, and I will do this for as long as you let me, which is forever, because laying it out is the safe part. The table never asks you to pick anything up. I once spent two weeks readying a clean way to skip a shift and in the end just went to the shift, because going was easier than the last step of the plan, which was the step where you stop planning. My whole life has been a beautifully appointed hallway of getting ready, with a door at the end I've never once had to open, because there's always one more thing to lay on the table first.

So I knew this feeling. I'd have known it in the dark. And I sat there in the kitchen waiting for it to be that — the old dodge, the prep that's really a place to hide — and it wouldn't quite be that. It was the same motions exactly. Route, timing, the thick of the signal. But it was pointed at the door instead of away from it. I wasn't laying things on the table so I'd never have to get up. I was laying out the few things a man would actually need to know if he got up. The prep was the same shape and the opposite direction, like a word said backward that comes out another word.

I didn't say any of this. You don't, in that kitchen, over the lugaw. I just said, "Find out when he's due. That's all I'm asking for now. When."

Sol looked at me a second longer than the question needed. She catches things in my face; she caught one then, I think, because she didn't push. "I can ask around up the line," she said. "Quiet. Just the *when.*"

"Just the when," I said.

Tessie filled three bowls and put one in my hands, and the conversation was over, because that's how it ends in there — not resolved, just fed. I held the bowl and didn't eat right away. I was watching myself the way you watch a sound in the house you're not sure you heard. I'd asked for one fact. The most useless, smallest thing a man can ask for. When does the bread come. It wasn't going up. It wasn't even deciding to go up. It was the first inch of the route to the door, and I'd asked for it out loud, in front of two people who'd remember I'd asked.

That's the part that wouldn't sit flat. Not that I'd done anything. I hadn't. But I'd put a thing on the table that, if I kept pulling the thread, ended somewhere I'd never let a thread end before. And I noticed I wasn't reaching to cut it. The basement with no floor, the great unanswerable question of whether *careful* was ever mine — I'd climbed out of that and found this little floor, the owing, low and solid, and the trouble with a floor is it gives you somewhere to stand while you do a thing. I had somewhere to stand now. The standing was new and I didn't trust my legs on it.

The draft was still under the far door, cold as ever, *down, not out,* and I still didn't look at it. One door at a time. The near one had a man's name on it and a question I could actually send somebody to answer, and that morning that was as far as I went, and it was further than I'd ever gone.

I ate the lugaw. It was good. Some mornings the owing only asks for one small fact, and you find out you're the kind of man who can stand to go get it.

*End of Chapter 17.*
