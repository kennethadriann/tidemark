## Chapter 41 — The Talk of the Man

Nobody spoke to me for a long while, and that suited me, because being spoken to was a thing I had to earn and I hadn't earned it yet. The two men who'd been at the poor fire when I came drifted off, one and then the other, and others came and warmed and went, and the wet went slowly out of my shirt, and the hunger settled from the sharp kind into the low kind you can sit with. I didn't ask anybody anything. I'd learned up the long run that a man who reaches for a thing too plain comes away with his hand empty, and I wanted the thing at the far end of this fire badly enough that I made myself want nothing out loud at all, for as long as it took.

The man who finally spoke was old, or worn down to where old and worn look the same, with hands that had stopped being any color but the color of the work. He'd been at the fire a while, not talking, feeding it a little when it sank, the way a man does at a fire he half-owns just by staying. He looked at me a couple of times the way the first man had, taking in the wet and the no-account of me and filing it, and then a good while later, out of nothing, he said I'd come down the wet way.

He didn't ask it. He said it, the way you name a thing you already know to see what the other man does with it. I said I had. A few days of it. I said it tired, because I was, and because tired is the truest thing a man in my shape can hand over and the cheapest to keep straight. He nodded like that squared with something. Men come up to the yards, he said, from the load-country, the way I'd have come if I'd come the ordinary way. Coming down to it out of the deep wet, that was the far men's direction, and the far men didn't generally look like me. He let it sit between us, and I let it sit too, because I'd learned that the less you rush to fill a man's quiet the more of his own he'll put into it.

He talked, once he'd started, the way old men at fires do when the load's off and a new face is as good as a reason. He told me things I hadn't asked for, which is the best kind of telling because you don't have to pay for it. The yards weren't always this big, he said. The yards weren't always this deep, either, and here he said a thing my head caught on and turned over while the rest of me kept still. The whole poor edge we were sitting at, the fire and the black water past it, this used to be middle ground. Dry standing. Good standing, back when he first came down to work it. The water had come up on it since. They'd moved the fires up the slope twice in his own time that he could put a hand to, and the low stalls that used to stand where the black water now stood were under.

I kept my face the way I keep it. But I was doing the arithmetic under it, the way I can't not, and it wouldn't sit any better here than it had sat with the old woman a whole world ago, the one whose mother walked dry streets, or with the walker under his load who said his father crossed the top stop on dry ground. A hundred years, they always told it. A hundred years drowned and done, the water long since come to its line and stopped, the world the shape it was always going to be. But here was a man not so old as all that, pointing at water he'd watched climb over standing he'd once stood on, with his own feet, in the years he could still count. Water that was still coming up didn't fit a thing that finished before anybody living was born. I didn't say any of it. I filed it, the way I'd filed the rest. Only it filed harder this time, and heavier, because the old woman had been telling me a story and this man was only telling me where not to put my feet.

When he'd wound down a little I put my own thing in, careful, the way you set a cup on a crowded table so as not to knock the others over. I said I was looking to fall in with the deep work. The far work, the men who go out past the yards and stay out and come back heavy. I said it like a man asking after a job, because in the yards that's what it would sound like and that's the only shape the question was allowed to have. I didn't say a name. The name was still mine to spend, and a first fire and a man I'd known the length of one telling was not the place to spend it.

He made a sound that wasn't quite a laugh. The far men, he said, weren't men you fell in with. They weren't a gang you joined at a fire. They went out one and two at a time to places most men wouldn't, for the stuff most men couldn't reach, and they came back through the yards to drop it onto fresh backs and take their pay before turning straight around, and they didn't sit, and they didn't take on a wet stranger who'd never once been out. If I wanted the deep work I'd start where everybody started, on a load up the ordinary way, and maybe in a year the yards would know my name enough to trust me any deeper than that.

That wasn't what I'd come for and he could likely see it wasn't, but I took it the way you take the answer you got instead of the one you wanted. Then, because he was a talker and the night was long, he gave me the thing I hadn't dared put a straight hand on. There was one of them out right now, he said. The kind I meant. Been out a long stretch, longer than the deep usually keeps a man, working the far side of everything, and the parcel-men down in the middle were holding backs against a heavy drop off him whenever he decided to surface. He'd come through here to hand it off, they always did. When, the old man couldn't tell me. The far men came when they came. But he'd come through the yards, and I was in the yards, and that was the first time since the kitchen that the man I was after and the ground under my own feet had been named in the same breath.

It still wasn't my man. I knew that sitting there. A far man out working was a shape, not a face, and the yards likely had more than one of that shape drifting through it in a season, and I hadn't spent the name that would've told me true. What I had was a place he'd come to and a rough word that somebody of his kind was out and owed the yards a drop. A track where a man will be is not the man. I'd been living in that gap the whole way up and down, and I knew better by now than to mistake the one for the other. So I took the little I'd bought and set it where I keep such things, and I stayed at the poor fire, and I waited on a man who hadn't come in yet.

*End of Chapter 41.*
