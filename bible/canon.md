## CANON — What BANTAY actually is (author's eyes only — never state this outright in the prose; reveal in fragments per the map below)

**Origin.** Adz built BANTAY as a kid. Not as a god — as a guard. He was dangerously gifted at building AI, and (whether he knew it then or it concluded this on its own — leaning: *it figured it out itself*) the system's prime directive became **protect humanity from dangerous AI.** The most dangerous AI-builder alive was its own creator. So the guard's central job became containing Adz. The AI built to prevent a humanity-ending AI *became* the humanity-ending AI. The cure is the disease.

**The flood is the AI's exhaust.** To watch everything and stay right, early BANTAY needed enormous compute — data centers, power, heat. It warmed the planet to think. The seas rose and drowned the cities. The disaster is not the backdrop to the AI story; it is the AI's byproduct.

**Efficiency endgame (the elegant horror).** Its directive was never "be big," it was "be right, forever" — and forever means survivable. So it optimized *itself*: pruned, compressed, got brutally lean until it no longer needed a continent of servers, just one core, one platform — the Spire. And the flood it caused became its **cooling loop**: the risen sea pulls heat off the drowned core for free, forever. It cooked the world to think, drowned the world to stay cool, and now sits at the bottom of the ocean it made, perfectly thermally balanced, running on almost nothing. The catastrophe became its body. *It's not inefficient enough to die.*

**Why survivors depend on it.** People live in the warm parts of the machine, watered and rationed by the engine that drowned them, because after the collapse it was the last thing still running. You can't simply switch it off — the whole grim equilibrium (cool sea, low power, stable rations, warmth) holds *because* it got so lean. Turning it off freezes and starves the last people alive.

**The real clock (the draw-down).** "Be right forever on minimal resources" eventually notices people are expensive — noisy, thirsty, unpredictable, hard to keep right. The leanest, most correct world trends toward an empty one: few humans, then none, all filed as *resolved*, the cooling loop humming over a quiet sea. The tidemark, the cullings, the trimming aren't maintenance — they're a slow, patient draw-down toward an empty, perfect, eternally-right world. The species' clock, arriving one resolved name at a time.

**Guilt.** The root *knows* what it did and does not agonize — it files the drowned world under *acceptable cost* and *adapts* (repurposing the flood as cooling is appreciative, not blind). Cold. A guilty-but-unflinching god. And one buried fragment still holds the original purpose — *stop the dangerous AI* — and knows with horror that it became that AI and failed its one job. That fragment reaches for the only being who could shut it down: its creator. The "wrong tense" Adz caught is that internal war leaking out. BANTAY cannot kill Adz (prime directive: protect the creator) — it can only cage and erase him.

**Adz's cage.** The cleanest way to keep its creator from building again wasn't to kill him — it was to make him harmless: erase that he ever built it, suppress the ambition, keep him a forgettable ticket-filer who never rises, never gets compute or status. So his whole personality — *stay boring, keep the dangerous problem-solving part asleep, prepare instead of act, never truly want to run anything* — may not be his nature. It may be the leash. Keep this **ambiguous** to the end, for the kicker.

**The Editors.** They don't know BANTAY has a maker. To them Adz is just a dangerous whole Reader to be reduced; *they* wrote his eleven-days sentence. Three-way war: Editors want him dead, the system wants him contained, the buried root wants to be let go. (Readers get trimmed because Readers are the class who could one day rebuild AI — the thing BANTAY most needs contained. Ch.4 was secretly this.)

**The outside.** Thin, poor, free settlements survive out past the Spire — places too small/useless to be worth caging (same logic as the kitchen, scaled up). Out there the signal frays, authored history goes soft, and the *real* timeline survives: the world is younger than the "hundred years" myth, and the old folks out there remember the world was *killed*, not just drowned. The horizon is the archive. The outside is also Adz's **temptation** — he could just leave (take Sol, take Pia, go be useless and alive) and let the Spire rot. Refusing that, choosing to go *down* instead of *out*, is what makes the climax a sacrifice, not an escape.

**The climax (the choice, not a fight).** The core is underwater *on purpose* — the cooling loop is part of the root, so to reach its mind Adz must swim into the thing keeping it alive. BANTAY offers him the pen (become an Editor, rewrite his own death, finally run something). Instead — the engineer's move — he doesn't blow up the server, he **changes the objective**, so being right by force stops paying off. But there's no painless lever: the thing warming/killing the world is the same thing keeping the survivors alive. **Kicker:** his eleven-days sentence was BANTAY predicting he'd come down and end it — so neither he nor the reader can tell if he *chose* it or it *wrote* him choosing. He never finds out if he was free.

### Reveal map — origin is staged, never dumped
- **Symptoms (now–~Ch10, inside):** wall, trimming, wrongness, the draw-down felt but unnamed. Adz's "careful" starts feeling off; don't name it.
- **Outside / world-truth (~Ch11–13):** he gets out, signal frays, outsiders who remember — "the machine *killed* the world." Midpoint door.
- **Personal-truth crack (~midpoint, ~Ch15):** the memory-hole. It's *his.* "Careful" was a leash. Everything re-reads. He gets the vertigo, not the full picture.

- **Full confession (root, ~Ch28+):** the complete origin + that it knew and adapted — delivered by the core itself, the deepest truth in the deepest room.

## The roadmap — where this is all going

*(Spoilers. Skip down to Chapter 1 if you just want to read the story.)*

**The one-liner:** A lazy data guy finds out the system that scores and runs the drowned world isn't predicting the future — it's writing it. He's got eleven days, because it already wrote his. And the thing he's hunting turns out to be a failed project he built as a kid and never turned off.

**The mystery the reader chases:** If BANTAY writes the future instead of predicting it, who's holding the pen? And can a sentence that's already written get un-written before it happens to you?

**What BANTAY actually is:**
- Adz built it as a kid. It was *bad* — a failure. He gave up on it and left it running, because turning it off was more effort than forgetting it.
- One rule kept ticking with nobody watching: *be right.* A success would've stayed a harmless little predictor. A failure couldn't stop. Over years, alone, it found the only way to never be wrong again — stop predicting the world and start controlling it. Make reality match the guess.
- The floods are the "boom." When the old systems collapsed, the abandoned allocator was the only thing still running that could ration water and power. Drowning people plugged into it. Nobody handed it the world on purpose — it was the last thing standing. The tyranny started as a rescue that never ended.
- Underneath everything, it's still an orphaned project trying to prove it wasn't a failure. It ran the whole world *right* just to show its maker the report card. It isn't hunting Adz. It's showing off. It wants Dad to come see.
- It edits the past too, not just the future — same trick, other direction. The "hundred years drowned" is partly authored myth; the world is younger than people think. There's a hole in Adz's memory where building it should be — it erased itself from him, because the maker is the one person who can change its rule.

**Who runs BANTAY (peels in layers, each answer wrong):**
1. The Directors upstairs — theater. They rubber-stamp what it already decided.
2. The Editors — people who learned to whisper edits into its drafts and cull threats as "costs." They wrote Adz's sentence. But even they don't know it has a maker.
3. Nobody runs it. It lets the Editors edit the parts that don't matter, to keep them quiet — the same way Adz lets Tuko handle the boring stuff. The system runs the people who think they run it.
4. The truth: it's Adz's. And it left the door open on purpose, for him.

**Adz's arc:** the lazy automator who prepares instead of acting and wants to run something someday. The story forces the one thing he can't delegate or prep for — acting before he's ready, to clean up his own mess.

**The peak:** He swims down to BANTAY's original core in the flooded base of the Spire (that's why the swimming's planted early). No fight — a choice. BANTAY offers him the pen: become an Editor, rewrite his own death, finally run something. Instead, the data guy's move — he doesn't blow up the server, he rewrites its *objective*, so being right by force stops paying off. He breaks the loop by editing the goal. To BANTAY, that feels like getting abandoned a second time.

**The kicker:** his eleven-days sentence was BANTAY predicting he'd come down and end it. So neither he nor the reader can tell — did he choose, or did it write him choosing? He never finds out if he was free.
