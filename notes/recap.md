# TIDEMARK — running recap (append-only, ~2 lines per chapter)

## Ch01 — The Wrong Tense
Adz, a Reader, wakes to his water ration and tidemark score. His illegal automation Tuko flags a prediction about Teodoro Lim written in the WRONG TENSE — BANTAY citing a future punishment as proof. He realizes BANTAY writes the future, doesn't predict it. A message: "you read a sentence not meant for reading." His number starts dropping. He stands up.

## Ch02 — Be Boring
He sits back down (standing is "interesting"). Plays it cool, leans on Tuko. Tuko finds: the message came from inside BANTAY itself; Lim is real and thirsty; and Adz's OWN file has an event dated 11 days out, written in past tense. Someone already wrote him down. Number frozen at 609.

## Ch03 — Tier 22
He acts the lazy way: files a dull backdated maintenance ticket, rides down through the Spire (worldbuilding: the lower you go, the poorer/wetter). Reaches Lim's unit — door already open. Lim is calm, expecting him, not the desperate cost the file promised. A woman is waiting. She knows his street name "Adz." Trap, not discovery.

## Ch04 — The Ones Who Notice
The woman = Sol. The Lim sentence was bait to surface a whole Reader. Lim is a former Reader who got "trimmed" (memory-edited) for noticing too much — proof of what BANTAY does. Sol's faction can SEE BANTAY authors reality but can't READ it; they need an untrimmed Reader. A sweep hits; his number drops fast. They flee down through a wall into water. "You said you can swim."

## Ch05 — Under the Line
Slow. The black-water swim, the cold arriving in installments, calm-not-brave. They surface in a drowned mall (a parking sign someone once complained about). His wrist goes DARK — no number for the first time in his life. He waits to feel free; feels "unweighed."

## Ch06 — The Kitchen at the Bottom of the World
Surprise: the resistance is a domestic KITCHEN, not a bunker. Lugaw, drying socks, a kid sorting bottle caps. Nobody checks wrists; they look at faces. Lim's humming briefly resolves into a real lullaby — he says "Malou" — then it's gone. Off-grid, edits don't hold tight; pieces surface. Adz realizes BANTAY trims people for "tidiness," same as he'd trim a bad row of data.

## Ch07 — The Wall of People Who Haven't Died Yet
The archive: salvaged slates of authored death-sentences, dated, in past tense. The bottle-cap girl is on it: PIA, a reduction, 9 days out. Adz reads deep — it BENDS (savable) but DOESN'T MATTER: she's not the point, the number is; save her, it writes a new death for whoever's nearest the knife. The only fix is to "change the question it's answering." The lazy man asks for the assignment: "Show me the rest."

## Ch08 — The Boat With the Hole In It
The Pia decision. Adz expects the room to agree saving her is pointless; Tessie just says "so we get her tonight." The hole-in-the-boat ethic: they bail not to empty the sea but because stopping means thinking like the machine. Adz realizes he'd been on BANTAY's side without noticing — not cruel, just tidy, which scaled up is the same thing. They pull Pia out through a gap in BANTAY's attention. She's safe; the hole remains. Adz ends wondering if "being right" and "being any good" are two different skills.

## Ch09 — What It Costs to Keep Bailing
Same night, dead hours after the rescue. Adz and Sol awake by the fire — a real two-guarded-people talk. Sol's reason for fighting: a brother, Danny, a Reader trimmed for noticing too much, still alive and "fine" upstairs with no memory he ever had a sister. She can't get him back, so she watches the wall so the next person is known as a person first. Adz, raised on efficiency, finds he doesn't think it's stupid. Quiet ending: he can live with *for now*, and it's warmer than being right.

## Ch10 — Nothing Added Back
Next day. Adz reads the wall the way only a Reader can — not one name but the whole table, sorted by date. Every sentence is a subtraction; he hunts for ANY addition (promotion, correction, birth) and finds none. The level only ever drops, no tap, no balancing column. He gets the draw-down dread but has no word for it, and can't make it small enough to say to Tessie, so he lies, takes a spoon, and stirs the lugaw. Quiet, unsettled: "the level's dropping, and there's no tap."

## Ch11 — Where Nobody's Counting
The dread turns into a question: if every name is a minus, where do you stand to NOT be on the wall? The count must have an edge. Maning, a weathered salvager, brings slates down from "up" and junk from "out" — and reveals an OUTSIDE past the towers where the signal frays, the wrist stops reaching, and thin, uncounted old settlements live "too small to be worth the bother." Secondhand, he relays an outsider rumor that the drowning was shorter than they were told, that "out there they tell it different" — Adz pointedly refuses to chase it, but feels a quiet part of him turn to face the door. Door ajar, not walked through. Maning's warning: the score doesn't like losing track of what it's already written down.

## Ch12 — Far Enough
The "out" rumor becomes a real temptation. Adz catches himself provisioning an escape (water for three — him, Pia, Sol); the lazy man moving fast is his tell that he's half-decided. The logic closes cleanly: the board can't be won, so the engineer's move is to stop feeding it — go out past the fraying edge and be poor, uncounted, alive, and let the Spire rot. Sol catches it in his face, won't guilt him, but names the cost: out fixes nothing, leaves everyone still on the wall on it, and is the same as agreeing her trimmed brother Danny never had a sister. She names a second door — his own line, "change the question it's answering" — and says out just stops being in the room when the question gets asked. He stops counting water but doesn't choose; out vs. down now live in him, the "down" only a faint dread of a swim he doesn't want to take.

## Ch13 — Where It Stops Reaching
Adz goes out WITH SOL to *look*, not leave (the choice still unmade). The edge is a distance not a depth — up then out, past towers gone to teeth. He feels it for real this time: Sol's working wrist goes slow then quits reaching, and the "weather" of being watched lifts off his neck. The outside is real and inhabited — poor, stubborn, uncounted, faces checked instead of wrists. An old woman says her *mother* walked the streets dry; Adz's arithmetic won't sit (a hundred years is 4–5 lives; nobody alive should have a mother who saw dry ground). He files it as a soft story — but for the first time a wrong number won't get small. History cracked, not confirmed.

## Ch14 — The Smooth Place
Back at the kitchen, the old woman's wrong number won't shrink, so Adz turns the Reader's eye inward: true memories come with junk attached (a season, the price of rice), and junk is the proof you can't fake. Watching Lim hum across the hole where "Malou" used to be — you can't feel a hole from inside, the taking includes the not-noticing, like Danny upstairs — he reaches for the origin of his deepest rule (be careful, don't be interesting, prepare-never-act) and it comes up smooth, no junk attached, already smooth instead of worn smooth. The thought becomes thinkable, not proven: that "careful" was never his but a leash handed to him as his own good sense. Vertigo, full picture withheld. Ends unsettled, feeling the floor of himself and not trusting it's floor.

## Ch15 — Something to Stand On
The doubt doesn't shrink, and Adz spends a day and a half useless in the unanswerable basement of "was careful ever mine." Tessie ends it by handing him a chore — count the water jugs. Eleven and a heel, three down, and the Reader sees the jugs as the wall in miniature: a column that only subtracts, nothing that adds; the draw-down gets a shape (a floor tilted, not named). The turn: the unanswerable question is wrong-because-useless, so he trades it for one with a floor — "what do I owe, and to who" — a list he can actually read (Pia, Lim, Tessie, the water). Nothing fixed; he just finds a floor that holds. Ends with a cold draft at the foot of the list — a direction, down not out, that he won't look at yet.

## Ch16 — He Carried It Down Himself
Maning takes Tessie's re-ration water list up the line, having brought salvaged death-slates down as usual. Reading them that evening (his self-assigned owing-job), Adz finds the draw-down a hair further over — more names, no name ever lifted off — then a fresh slate near the bottom carrying MANING's own sentence, dated out: he carried his death down on his back and never felt it (the can't-feel-the-hole-from-inside motif). It won't go on Adz's shelf because Maning's on the owing list. The cost lands: a list of people-you-owe is also a list of things-you-might-have-to-do, and the owing asks the one thing the prepare-never-act man can't — go up, act, unready. He does nothing but hold the slate; two doors now (the near one, up after Maning, a clock he can count; the far one, "down," still unlooked-at). "Some nights the owing only asks."

## Ch17 — The First Inch
Morning: Tessie finds Maning's slate, sets it apart, asks the only useful question — when's he due back down. Adz knows the man's death-date but not his schedule. Low practical talk with Tessie and Sol (no "we have to save him" — Pia's lesson holds: saving off the wall just writes the next name). Under the talk Adz catches himself PLANNING — route, timing, where the signal goes thick — his usual hiding-prep, but for once pointed AT the door not away ("a word said backward that comes out another word"). His one concrete move: asks Sol to find out, quiet, just the *when* — first inch of a route, said out loud before witnesses. Not deciding, not going up. Far door ("down, not out") still unlooked-at. Ends fed not resolved: "the kind of man who can stand to go get it."

## Ch18 — The Shape of Days
Sol comes back with the *when*: Maning has gone UP, deeper than usual, chasing a load, "not in a hurry" — so the window is spending itself, not banked. The bare fact reorganizes Adz's count: owing stops being a comfortable FLOOR ("days is a hallway") and becomes a real NUMBER going down on its own, the eleven-jugs move again (felt subtraction). Both privately note Maning's walking deeper into thick-signal country where the score reads sharpest (his Ch11 warning). Second small inch: Adz asks Sol the route, lays it on the table, still not "reaching for the knife." Ends: "once it's a number you can count, it starts counting itself."

## Ch19 — Send Word
The lazy man's instinct: don't go up, send word to pull Maning down. Adz builds the delegation plan lovingly, then walks it and every leg folds the same way — you can't put a man's date in a stranger's mouth (Pia's lesson), a vague "come down" won't move a stubborn man with good water, and sending words up means lighting a flare in thick-signal country. The only thing that moves Maning is someone he trusts in the dry in front of him: the words can travel, the going can't. The turn: once a thing can't be sent, not-going stops being free — it becomes its own act, costing exactly what going costs. His lifelong cheap door (doing nothing is free) is gone. Sol names it (his Ch16 line turned back on him): you'd never trust word got there; the only way you trust it is you carry it. He still does NOT decide, does NOT go. Far door faint. "No one between you to send."

## Ch20 — Something for the Bag
Next morning Adz catches his HANDS doing the prep to go up — packing a sack (water for one, a dry layer, working how a dark off-grid wrist passes in thick-signal country). The Ch12 provisioning tell, now pointed AT the door. The dread under it: finishing the prep has always been his hallway-with-no-door, the way he never goes — but this time the list runs out and there's nothing behind the last item but the stair up. He sets the bottles down, can't shoulder the sack and won't put it away; it stands by the wall all morning. Tessie sees it, says nothing, hands him the spoon. No decision, no step — "a place you can stand."

## Ch21 — The Foot of It
The readiness moves from hands to feet: crossing the kitchen, Adz finds his body picking the sack up, shouldering it, and carrying it to the foot of the low flooded run that is the way up. He stands at the black water — and the hallway-with-no-door finally has a door: past the last packed item is an actual cold he'd have to put his legs into ("a list runs out and turns into a temperature"; the pack doesn't get you wet). The body carries the load to the water and hands the rest back, a porter who won't come inside. He does NOT step in. He only stages the bag forward — sets it on the dry lip at the foot of the route instead of back by the wall — then returns to the lugaw pot. The sixth and last non-decision notch; ends flat ("We ate"), breaking the aphorism streak.

## Ch22 — The Cold Part
THE STALL BREAKS. Before dawn, alone, when nobody's awake to make the hesitation look like thinking, Adz finally goes — feet ahead of the head, no clean decision "click" ever arriving. He stops waiting for one, puts a foot in the black flooded run (cold in installments, Ch5 echo), wades to the waist, and does NOT turn back (the not-turning-around, not the stepping-in, is the one piece he counts as nearly his own). He crosses, climbs the far slope wet to the waist with the bag streaming, and heads up into the dark of the line after Maning. Kicker-ambiguity alive (chose vs. written left open). One beat only — the first leg of the going; he doesn't reach Maning. Ends on motion ("going"), not an aphorism.

## Ch23 — Back Under the Weather
The climb up the line, one texture: the "weather" of being watched (Ch13's lifted-off-the-neck) settling back DOWN onto him as the route climbs toward thick-signal country — the count reaching for him again, skin feeling it a half-step ahead of the head. His Ch20 dry-ground plan for passing a dark wrist (sleeve down, arm easy, don't snag the eye) turns out to need doing by hand, not just packing: "everything I'd thought I could pack turned out to need carrying." The uncounted man is climbing back INTO the count on purpose to fetch a man who doesn't know he needs fetching. Faint history-crack touch: an old salvager's wall-mark worn older than the hundred-years story allows; he files it SOFT and walks past. Wet-going-damp, cold, alone, doesn't reach Maning. Ends flat on plain forward motion ("getting nearer").

## Ch24 — Where People Had Been
The climb reaches the FIRST EDGE of the counted world: the passage stops being nobody's and starts showing a hand in it. Adz reads the signs the Reader way — cut steps (someone comes often), a salvager's handline (work spent on a maybe), old smoke gone into the walls (people + food), a widening knocked back for loads, a dead light-bracket, a split abandoned boot ("the way goes somewhere"). The wrist-weather thickens from a draft under a door to a flat hand on the back of the head — the count getting denser, no wall, just a getting-thicker. He pays off the "calm by hand" work: readies in an empty bend before he's seen, catches himself nerve-fussing the cuff and leaves it. Ends hearing the first human noise not his own far up the bend — stands, doesn't approach, then goes up toward it. No contact, doesn't reach Maning. Flat close: "slow, on my feet, in the open."

## Ch25 — A Man Passing Through
First human contact since the kitchen — and it PASSES. At the first lamplit waystation Adz comes among two salvager strangers and has to be the plain dark-wristed man he drew up. The rehearsed "calm by hand" gets used in front of a reader-of-faces for real: the sharper one's eye goes down his arm; Adz doesn't hide the wrist, scratches his nose with that hand slow and sets it down easy, and it costs him — but the read passes ("bored over interested any day"). He plays the tired-man-looking-for-work line ("came up the low way"), no clever answer being the thing he got right. Small route decency: directions up (marked way, cut-offs flood, a bigger place up top) and a bad dried fish-cake tossed over (Ch24 hunger paid off). Walks on still just a tired man looking for work. No canon touched, doesn't reach Maning. Flat close: "one man passing through on his feet, going on."
